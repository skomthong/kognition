# Self-Efficacy (Bandura)

## Definition

Self-efficacy (Bandura, 1977/1997) is the belief in one's capability to execute actions required to produce specific outcomes. It is domain-specific — a person can have high self-efficacy for public speaking and low self-efficacy for data analysis. Efficacy beliefs influence what people attempt, how much effort they invest, how long they persist, and how they respond to failure.

## How It Works

Four sources of self-efficacy (in order of strength):
1. **Mastery experiences** — succeeding at progressively difficult tasks (strongest source)
2. **Vicarious experiences** — observing similar others succeed
3. **Social persuasion** — verbal encouragement from credible sources
4. **Physiological/affective states** — interpreting arousal (anxiety = "I can't" vs. excitement = "I'm ready")

## Key Principles

- Build efficacy through mastery experiences: scaffolded practice → early success → progressive challenge → feedback.
- Simulations create mastery experiences safely — meta-analytic evidence (Sitzmann) reports higher post-training self-efficacy from simulation games.
- Peer modeling and coaching leverage vicarious experience and social persuasion simultaneously.
- Efficacy is task-specific — measure it with scales tailored to the target performance, not generic confidence.
