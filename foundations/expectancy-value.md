# Expectancy-Value Theory

## Definition

Eccles & Wigfield review expectancy-value as a framework where engagement, choice, and persistence are driven by two factors: **expectancy for success** (Can I do this?) and **subjective task value** (Is this worth doing?).

Task value has four components:
- **Attainment value** — importance of doing well for identity
- **Intrinsic value** — enjoyment of the activity itself
- **Utility value** — usefulness for future goals
- **Cost** — what must be given up (time, effort, emotional toll)

## How It Works

Adults continuously evaluate whether learning is "worth it." If expectancy is low (I can't succeed) or value is low (this doesn't matter to my role), engagement drops regardless of instructional quality. Cost is especially salient for adult learners balancing multiple roles.

## Key Principles

- Design should make value explicit and authentic to the workplace context — not assumed.
- Reduce perceived cost: minimize friction, respect time, eliminate busywork.
- Build expectancy through clear criteria, early success, and progressive challenge.
- Relevance to adult learner persistence — adults are more sensitive to cost than traditional students because their opportunity costs are higher.
