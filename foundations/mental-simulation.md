# Mental Simulation

## Definition

Mental simulation is the cognitive process of imagining scenarios — running "what if" models in the mind to predict outcomes, prepare for events, and evaluate options before acting. Kahneman & Tversky's simulation heuristic shows people judge probability partly by how easily they can mentally simulate an event. Klein's recognition-primed decision model shows experts use mental simulation to evaluate options by imagining how they would play out.

## How It Works

- **Prospection** (Gilbert & Wilson): the brain uses memory and schema to construct possible futures. We don't just recall the past — we use the same systems to simulate what hasn't happened yet.
- **Scenario evaluation**: when facing a decision, people mentally "run" the most plausible option and check whether the imagined outcome is acceptable. If it fails in simulation, they try the next option (Klein's RPD model).
- **Emotional pre-experiencing**: mental simulation generates real emotional responses — anticipatory anxiety, excitement, dread. These emotions influence decisions before any action is taken.
- **Counterfactual thinking**: imagining how things COULD HAVE gone differently. "If I had done X instead..." — this supports learning from experience but can also produce rumination.

## Key Principles

- **Experts simulate better than novices** — rich schemas enable more accurate and detailed mental simulation. A doctor can mentally "run" a diagnosis because they have thousands of pattern-matched cases. A novice can't simulate what they've never experienced.
- **Simulation is limited by schemas** — you can only imagine scenarios built from what you know. This is why exposure to diverse cases, scenarios, and examples expands the repertoire of mental models available for simulation.
- **Scenario-based learning works BECAUSE of mental simulation** — when learners engage with a scenario, they mentally simulate the situation, predict outcomes, and compare their prediction to the actual consequence. This is the learning mechanism.
- **Premortems leverage mental simulation** — "Imagine this project has failed. What went wrong?" forces simulation of failure, which surfaces risks that optimism bias hides.
