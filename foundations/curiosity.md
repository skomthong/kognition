# Curiosity

## Definition

Loewenstein's information-gap theory frames curiosity as arising from a perceived gap between what one knows and what one wants to know. The gap creates an aversive state that motivates information-seeking. Curiosity is both epistemic (desire for knowledge) and perceptual (desire for novel sensory input).

## How It Works

Curiosity is triggered when learners become aware of a gap — something surprising, incomplete, or contradictory to their current understanding. The gap must be solvable (not too vast) and relevant (connected to something the learner cares about). Curiosity enhances attention, encoding depth, and memory.

## Key Principles

- Use curiosity ethically: reveal gaps and stakes, then provide an achievable route to closure. Avoid manipulative suspense.
- Curiosity prompts in adult programs: "What decision would you make with incomplete information?" then provide data incrementally and require justification.
- Curiosity is most effective when it triggers deeper processing — asking why, exploring consequences, generating explanations.
- Curiosity-driven exploration without feedback or structure can lead to inefficient learning; pair with retrieval checks.
