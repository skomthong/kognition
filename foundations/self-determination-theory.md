# Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

## Definition

SDT (Ryan & Deci, 2000) describes how social-context conditions can enhance or undermine intrinsic motivation, self-regulation, and well-being. Three basic psychological needs drive motivation quality:

- **Autonomy** — feeling volitional and self-endorsed in one's actions
- **Competence** — feeling effective and capable
- **Relatedness** — feeling connected and belonging

When these needs are supported, motivation becomes more internalized (autonomous); when thwarted, motivation becomes controlled or absent.

## How It Works

SDT distinguishes motivation types on a continuum from amotivation through extrinsic motivation (external, introjected, identified, integrated) to intrinsic motivation. Autonomous motivation (identified, integrated, intrinsic) produces better persistence, learning, and well-being than controlled motivation (external, introjected).

Design choices either support or undermine the three needs:
- **Choice** supports autonomy; surveillance and controlling deadlines undermine it
- **Clear criteria and feedback** support competence; ambiguity and harsh evaluation undermine it
- **Belonging and peer connection** support relatedness; isolation and competitive ranking undermine it

## Key Principles

- Adults continuously evaluate whether learning is worth their time. SDT explains *why* adults disengage even when instruction is cognitively sound — value and autonomy are key.
- Gamification that relies on extrinsic rewards (points, badges) can undermine intrinsic motivation if it shifts the perceived locus of causality from internal to external (overjustification effect).
- Align learning design to all three needs: choice in practice cases (autonomy), progressive mastery checks (competence), peer coaching pods (relatedness).
