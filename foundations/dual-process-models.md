# Dual-Process Models (System 1 / System 2)

## Definition

Dual-process accounts distinguish two modes of cognitive processing: fast, automatic, heuristic processes (System 1) and slower, deliberative, analytic processes (System 2). Kahneman's framing popularizes this distinction, grounded in decades of heuristics-and-biases research including Tversky & Kahneman's foundational 1974 work.

## How It Works

- **System 1** operates automatically with little effort or voluntary control. It relies on heuristics, pattern matching, and learned associations. It is fast but prone to systematic biases.
- **System 2** allocates attention to effortful mental activities including complex reasoning, self-control, and deliberate choice. It is slow but more accurate when engaged.

Both systems operate continuously. Most everyday behavior is System 1-driven. System 2 is recruited when novel, complex, or high-stakes situations demand conscious deliberation — but it is resource-limited and fatigable.

## Key Principles

- Many training failures are not due to ignorance but due to **System 1 defaults** under stress, time pressure, fatigue, or habit (e.g., safety shortcuts, procedural drift).
- Adult learning design should combine:
  - **System 2 supports**: explicit rules, checklists, reasoning practice, decision frameworks
  - **System 1 shaping**: environmental cues, defaults, friction reduction, repeated practice until automaticity
- Heuristics and biases (anchoring, availability, framing) are System 1 artifacts that affect learner judgment, assessment responses, and workplace decisions.

## Bounded Rationality (Simon)

Herbert Simon's bounded rationality (1955) argues that humans don't optimize — they **satisfice** (satisfy + suffice). Decision-makers face limited information, limited time, and limited cognitive capacity, so they use heuristics to find "good enough" solutions rather than computing the optimal one. This is not a flaw — it's adaptive. But it means design must account for how people ACTUALLY decide, not how they theoretically should.

## Common Biases (System 1 Artifacts)

| Bias | What happens | Learning design implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | First number/information disproportionately influences judgment | The first example or framework you present becomes the anchor — choose carefully |
| **Availability** | Events that come easily to mind seem more likely/important | Vivid case studies feel more "true" than statistics; balance with data |
| **Confirmation** | Seeking evidence that confirms existing beliefs, ignoring contradictions | Learners accept evidence that fits their model and resist what doesn't — design for [conceptual change](../principles/schema-and-mental-models.md) |
| **Sunk cost** | Continuing investment because of past investment, not future value | "We already built the content" — justifying bad design because of prior effort |
| **Dunning-Kruger** | Low competence + high confidence; high competence + low confidence | Novices overestimate their ability; experts underestimate — calibration exercises help |
| **Overconfidence** | Excessive certainty in one's judgments | Especially dangerous in high-stakes decisions; mitigate with premortems and confidence calibration |
| **Status quo bias** | Preference for the current state; resistance to change | Why "the way we've always done it" persists even when evidence supports change |
| **Framing** | Same information presented differently leads to different choices | See [Framing Effects](../principles/framing-effects.md) for full treatment |
| **Halo effect** | Positive impression in one area influences judgment in others | A confident speaker is perceived as more knowledgeable, regardless of content quality |
| **Survivorship bias** | Only seeing what succeeded; ignoring what failed | Studying successful programs without studying failures produces distorted lessons |

## Application to Learning Design Decisions

Designers are subject to biases too:
- **Anchoring** on the first design approach considered
- **Confirmation bias** toward evidence supporting preferred methods
- **Sunk cost** keeping bad content because it was expensive to produce
- **Availability** overgeneralizing from a single dramatic success or failure
- **Status quo** defaulting to "how we've always designed" rather than evidence-based alternatives

Debiasing strategies: premortems, structured decision protocols, evidence reviews, peer critique, explicit criteria before evaluating options. See Larrick (2004) for a review of debiasing approaches.
