# Narrative Cognition

## Definition

Narrative cognition holds that storytelling is a fundamental mode of human thought — not merely a communication technique but a way of constructing reality and meaning. Bruner argues narrative is one of two primary modes of knowing (the other being paradigmatic/logical-scientific). Schank positions stories as central to memory, intelligence, and knowledge application.

## How It Works

Humans organize experience into narrative structures: characters, settings, conflicts, sequences, and resolutions. These structures serve as retrieval scaffolds — information embedded in a story is more easily recalled and applied to analogous situations than decontextualized facts.

- **Bruner**: narrative shapes how people interpret experience, assign causality, and construct identity. It is not a decorative layer over "real" knowledge but a cognitive mode in its own right.
- **Schank**: stories function as indexed memory structures. When encountering a new situation, people search memory for relevant stories and adapt them. This makes narrative a mechanism for both storage and transfer.
- **Transportation theory** (Green & Brock): absorption into a narrative world — including concentration, imagery, and affect — can shift beliefs and evaluations. Transportation is a persuasive mechanism, not just engagement.

## Key Principles

- Narrative is most defensible in adult learning when it serves a specific function:
  - Provide a **case-based decision context** that approximates real practice
  - Reduce abstraction by embedding concepts in **situated meaning** and consequence-based decisions
  - Support **identity-oriented learning** (leadership, ethics, professional role formation)
- Narrative immersion (transportation) can shift beliefs and attitudes — a powerful mechanism that requires pairing with critical reflection to prevent unexamined belief adoption.
