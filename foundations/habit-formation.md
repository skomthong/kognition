# Habit Formation

## Definition

Habit formation is the process by which behaviors become automatic through repetition in stable contexts. Lally et al. modeled real-world habit formation and found that automaticity increases following an asymptotic curve — early repetitions produce the largest gains, with diminishing returns as the behavior approaches full automaticity. Median time to plateau was 66 days, but with wide individual variation.

## Underlying Constructs

- [Dual-Process Models](../foundations/dual-process-models.md) — habit is System 1 automaticity. Once formed, the behavior executes with minimal System 2 involvement.
- [Cognitive Load](../foundations/cognitive-load.md) — habitual behaviors consume fewer cognitive resources, freeing working memory for novel or complex tasks.

## How It Works

Three elements must converge for a habit to form:

1. **Stable context cue** — a consistent situation, time, or preceding action that triggers the behavior (e.g., "after shift handover," "after morning coffee").
2. **Repeated behavior** — the action must be performed consistently in response to the cue. Missing occasional repetitions slows but does not reset the process.
3. **Sufficient motivation** (at least initially) — early repetitions require deliberate effort. As automaticity develops, the motivation requirement decreases.

Key models that formalize this:
- **COM-B** (Michie, van Stralen, & West): Behavior occurs when Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation are sufficient. Useful for diagnosing *why* a habit isn't forming.
- **Fogg Behavior Model**: Behavior occurs when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge at the same moment. Design the prompt, make the behavior small enough, and ensure minimal motivation threshold is met.

## Factors That Influence It

**Enhancers:**
- Consistent context cues (same time, place, or preceding action)
- Simplicity — smaller behaviors automate faster
- Early success experiences and intrinsic satisfaction
- Environmental design that reduces friction for the desired behavior

**Inhibitors:**
- Variable contexts and schedules (common for adult learners balancing roles)
- High complexity or cognitive cost of the behavior
- Competing habits occupying the same cue-response slot
- Lack of prompts or reminders during the formation period

## Methods That Leverage This

- [Choice Architecture](../methods/choice-architecture.md) — defaults and friction reduction shape the environment to support habit cues
- [Gamification](../techniques/narrative/gamification.md) — streaks and progress tracking can support repetition during the formation period
- [Microlearning](../methods/microlearning.md) — small enough to attach to stable daily cues
- [Retrieval Practice](../methods/retrieval-practice.md) — daily retrieval routines can become habitual when anchored to consistent cues
