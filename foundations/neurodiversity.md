# Neurodiversity

## Definition

Neurodiversity, widely attributed to Judy Singer's 1998 thesis, frames cognitive and perceptual differences (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, etc.) as natural human variation rather than deficits. It extends disability rights discourse to neurological difference.

## How It Works

The neurodiversity paradigm reframes design from "accommodating deficits" to "reducing barriers for cognitive variation." In learning design, this means building flexibility into instruction from the start rather than retrofitting accommodations.

## Key Principles

- Use **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)** as the primary design framework: provide multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression to reduce barriers for all learners (CAST UDL Guidelines 2.2 / 3.0).
- Provide options for attention, pacing, and medium (text, audio, visual) while keeping learning goals constant.
- Avoid forced competition or public leaderboards that may increase anxiety. If game elements are used, favor cooperative mechanics and autonomy-supportive choices (consistent with SDT).
- Assessment accommodations and alternative evidence pathways should be available where valid.
- Gather accessibility usability evidence (qualitative + usability metrics) rather than assuming compliance equals usability.
