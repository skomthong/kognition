# Distributed Cognition

## Definition

Distributed cognition (Hutchins, 1995) holds that cognitive processes are not confined to individual brains — they are distributed across people, tools, artifacts, and environments. A cockpit crew navigating an aircraft, a surgical team coordinating a procedure, or a designer working with sketches and software are all examples of cognitive systems that extend beyond any single mind.

Related concept: **extended mind theory** (Clark & Chalmers, 1998) argues that external tools (notebooks, calculators, AI systems) can be genuine parts of a cognitive process, not just aids to it. If you always use a calculator for arithmetic, the calculator is part of your arithmetic cognition.

## How It Works

- **Cognitive artifacts**: tools that carry cognitive load — checklists, decision aids, dashboards, reference materials, templates. They store, organize, and present information so the human doesn't have to hold it all in working memory.
- **Social distribution**: knowledge distributed across team members, each contributing expertise. No single person holds the full picture. Coordination mechanisms (communication protocols, shared displays) enable the distributed system to function.
- **Environmental structuring**: physical and digital environments designed to support cognition — signage, spatial layouts, interface design, information architecture.

## Key Principles

- **The unit of analysis is the SYSTEM, not the individual** — when evaluating performance or designing training, consider the full sociotechnical system: people + tools + environment + processes.
- **Cognitive artifacts reduce individual load** — checklists, job aids, and decision tools are not "crutches" — they're components of a well-designed cognitive system. See [Cognitive Load](../principles/cognitive-load.md).
- **AI extends the cognitive system** — [AI copilots](../systems/ai-copilot-systems.md) and [adaptive learning systems](../systems/adaptive-learning.md) are new cognitive artifacts that change the distribution of cognition between human and machine.
- **Design the system, not just the training** — if the environment doesn't support the cognition (missing tools, poor information display, no team coordination), training individuals won't fix the system.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Job aids and performance support** are distributed cognition in action — don't train people to memorize what a well-designed artifact can hold.
- **Team training** should address the COORDINATION, not just individual skills — how does information flow? Where are the handoffs? What shared representations do they need?
- **Technology adoption** changes the cognitive system — new tools redistribute who does what. Train the new distribution, not just the new tool.
