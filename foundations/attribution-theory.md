# Attribution Theory

## Definition

Attribution theory (Weiner, 1985) explains how learners interpret the causes of their successes and failures along three dimensions: **locus** (internal/external), **stability** (stable/unstable), and **controllability** (controllable/uncontrollable). These attributions shape emotional responses, expectations, and future behavior.

## How It Works

| Attribution | Example | Emotional response | Behavioral consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Internal + stable + uncontrollable | "I'm not smart enough" | Shame, hopelessness | Give up, avoid challenges |
| Internal + unstable + controllable | "I didn't practice enough" | Guilt, determination | Increase effort, change strategy |
| External + stable + uncontrollable | "The test is always unfair" | Anger, helplessness | Disengage, blame system |
| External + unstable + uncontrollable | "I had bad luck" | Surprise | Try again without change |

The most adaptive attributions for learning are internal, unstable, and controllable — they point to changeable factors (effort, strategy) rather than fixed traits.

## Key Principles

- Feedback and debriefing can reshape attributions: emphasize strategy ("Your approach to the diagnosis missed X — try Y next time") rather than ability ("You're not good at this") or vague praise ("Good effort").
- Attribution retraining: help learners reframe failures from stable causes to unstable, controllable causes.
- A learner who attributes failure to lack of ability will respond very differently to the same instructional support than one who attributes it to insufficient practice.
- In adult professional contexts: attribution patterns interact with identity, status, and psychological safety.
