# Expertise Development

## Definition

Expertise development describes how people progress from novice to expert performance — and how the cognitive architecture changes along the way. Dreyfus & Dreyfus (1986) propose a five-stage model. Chi et al. document how experts and novices categorize problems differently. The concept is foundational to scaffolding, cognitive load theory (expertise reversal), and instructional sequencing.

## How It Works

### Dreyfus Five-Stage Model

| Stage | Characteristics | Instructional implication |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Novice** | Follows rules rigidly, no context sensitivity | Provide rules, worked examples, high scaffolding |
| **2. Advanced beginner** | Recognizes some situational elements, still rule-bound | Introduce context, guided practice with feedback |
| **3. Competent** | Plans deliberately, sets priorities, manages complexity | Problem-based practice, decision-making scenarios |
| **4. Proficient** | Perceives situations holistically, recognizes patterns intuitively | Case-based practice, reflection, pattern exposure |
| **5. Expert** | Acts intuitively from deep experience, responds fluidly to context | Deliberate practice on edge cases, mentoring others, novel challenges |

### What Changes Cognitively

- **Schema development**: novices store isolated facts; experts store organized, interconnected schemas that enable rapid pattern recognition ([Schema and Mental Models](../principles/schema-and-mental-models.md))
- **Automaticity**: routine procedures move from controlled (System 2) to automatic (System 1) processing, freeing working memory for novel aspects
- **Problem representation**: novices categorize by surface features ("this is a physics problem about pulleys"); experts categorize by deep structure ("this is a conservation of energy problem")
- **Metacognitive calibration**: experts know what they don't know; novices are often overconfident (Dunning-Kruger connection)

### Adaptive Expertise vs. Routine Expertise

| Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Routine expertise** | Efficient execution of known procedures in familiar contexts; breaks down in novel situations |
| **Adaptive expertise** | Flexible application of deep understanding to novel, unfamiliar problems; transfers broadly |

Adaptive expertise requires not just practice but varied practice, reflection, and exposure to novel challenges — connects to [Desirable Difficulties](../principles/desirable-difficulties.md) and [Interleaving](../techniques/instructional/interleaving.md).

## Key Principles

- **Design differently for different stages** — worked examples for novices, problem-solving for competent learners, edge cases for experts. Same instruction for all stages fails everyone (expertise reversal in CLT).
- **Expertise is domain-specific** — a medical expert is a novice at instructional design. Don't assume general expertise.
- **10,000 hours is misleading** — Ericsson's [Deliberate Practice](../techniques/instructional/deliberate-practice.md) research shows that QUALITY of practice (structured, feedback-rich, at the edge of ability) matters more than hours alone.
