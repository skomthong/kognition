# Cognitive Aging

## Definition

Cognitive aging describes age-related changes in cognitive processing that affect how older adults learn, remember, and perform. Two key theories frame design implications: Salthouse's processing-speed theory and Park & Reuter-Lorenz's Scaffolding Theory of Aging and Cognition (STAC).

## How It Works

- **Processing-speed theory (Salthouse)**: Age-related slowing in processing speed is a primary contributor to cognitive differences in older adults. Mechanisms include limited time (less processing completed in available time) and simultaneity constraints (earlier products of processing may decay before later processing completes).
- **STAC (Park & Reuter-Lorenz)**: The aging brain compensates through scaffolding — adaptive recruitment of additional neural resources in response to challenge. This compensatory capacity is strengthened by engagement, learning, exercise, and cognitive training, and weakened by disease, stress, and disuse.

## Key Principles

- Reduce time pressure in early learning stages; allow repeated practice with feedback before introducing realistic speed constraints.
- Use spaced, repeated retrieval and practice over time (especially for safety or certification) rather than massed cramming.
- Prioritize accessibility: larger text, clear contrast, consistent navigation (WCAG 2.2).
- Distinguish **accuracy** from **speed** outcomes in measurement. If speed is a performance requirement, measure and train it explicitly.
- Use transfer checks in realistic contexts after mastery.
