# Psychological Safety

## Definition

Psychological safety (Edmondson, 1999) is a shared belief held by team members that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — asking questions, admitting errors, proposing ideas, and giving feedback without fear of punishment or humiliation.

## How It Works

In psychologically safe environments, people are more willing to:
- Disclose knowledge gaps and ask for help
- Report errors and near-misses
- Offer dissenting views and challenge assumptions
- Participate in peer feedback and debrief honestly
- Practice publicly and risk failure

Edmondson's research links psychological safety to team learning behavior, innovation, and quality improvement. In learning environments, safety determines whether learners engage authentically or perform defensively.

## Key Principles

- Psychological safety is not "being nice" — it's the ability to be candid. High safety + high standards produces learning; high safety + low standards produces comfort zone.
- Facilitator behavior is the strongest lever: admitting uncertainty, responding constructively to errors, asking genuine questions, and norm-setting early.
- Competitive gamification (public leaderboards, winner-take-all) can undermine safety. Cooperative mechanics and anonymous participation options preserve it.
- Safety must be actively built — it doesn't emerge from good content alone.
