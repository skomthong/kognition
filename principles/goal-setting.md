# Goal Setting Theory

## Definition

Locke & Latham's goal setting theory identifies five key principles: specific and difficult goals lead to higher performance than vague or easy goals, mediated by direction of attention, effort mobilization, persistence, and strategy development. Goal commitment, self-efficacy, feedback, and task complexity moderate the effect.

## How It Works

- **Specificity**: "Score 85% on the certification exam" outperforms "do your best"
- **Difficulty**: challenging goals produce higher performance than easy goals, up to the limit of ability and commitment
- **Commitment**: goals only work when the person is committed — influenced by importance, self-efficacy, and participation in goal setting
- **Feedback**: goals plus feedback outperform goals alone; feedback allows monitoring and strategy adjustment
- **Task complexity**: for complex tasks, "do your best" or learning goals can outperform specific performance goals, because rigid targets can induce tunnel vision

## Key Principles

- Distinguish **learning goals** ("develop three new strategies for handling objections") from **performance goals** ("close 10 deals this month"). Learning goals are better for novel/complex tasks; performance goals are better for straightforward tasks where the strategy is known.
- Learning contracts (andragogy) are goal-setting instruments: learner and facilitator negotiate goals, strategies, evidence, and evaluation criteria.
- Goal setting can harm when it produces tunnel vision, unethical shortcuts, or undermines intrinsic motivation for exploratory learning.
- In adult programs: use goal setting for skill acquisition and performance improvement; pair with self-regulation training and progress feedback.
