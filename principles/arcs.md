# ARCS Motivational Design

## Definition

The ARCS model (Keller, 1987) provides a practical framework for designing motivational support into instruction through four categories: Attention, Relevance, Confidence, and Satisfaction.

## How It Works

- **Attention**: capture and sustain curiosity and interest (perceptual arousal, inquiry arousal, variability)
- **Relevance**: connect learning to learner goals, experiences, and values (goal orientation, motive matching, familiarity)
- **Confidence**: help learners believe they can succeed (learning requirements, success opportunities, personal control)
- **Satisfaction**: reinforce achievement with intrinsic and extrinsic rewards (natural consequences, positive consequences, equity)

ARCS is a design audit tool: for each unit or module, check whether all four categories are addressed.

## Key Principles

- Use short "hook" scenarios (Attention), explicit job relevance statements (Relevance), success criteria and guided practice (Confidence), and recognition tied to meaningful performance (Satisfaction).
- ARCS is complementary to SDT — Relevance maps to identified motivation, Confidence maps to competence, Satisfaction maps to intrinsic reward.
- Risk of "engagement theater" if ARCS elements are not tied to learning outcomes.
