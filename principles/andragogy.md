# Andragogy (Knowles)

## Definition

Andragogy (Knowles, 1980) is a set of design assumptions about adult learners that distinguish adult education from pedagogy. Six assumptions: adults need to know *why* they should learn something; adults' self-concept moves toward self-direction; adults bring accumulated experience as a learning resource; adults' readiness to learn is tied to social roles and developmental tasks; adults are oriented toward immediate application and problem-solving; adults are motivated by both internal and external factors.

## How To Apply It

- **Use learners' experience as an asset**: discussion, cases, peer teaching, reflection on practice — not blank-slate instruction.
- **Design for immediacy and role relevance**: authentic tasks, problems, and performance contexts. "When will I use this?" should have a clear answer.
- **Shift from teacher control to shared control**: planning, norms, pacing, choice of projects. Self-direction is both a motivator and a design constraint.
- **Make "need to know" discoverable**: surface consequences, performance standards, and real constraints that create authentic demand for learning.
- **Plan explicitly for transfer**: the "transfer problem" is persistent; generalization and maintenance depend on learner, design, and work environment factors.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** designing for adult professionals, executive education, workplace training, continuing education — any context where learners have experience, role demands, and time constraints.

**Use with caution when:** treating andragogy as a universal theory rather than a set of design heuristics. The assumptions vary by context and culture; not all adults are equally self-directed; some adult learners prefer more structure.
