# Framing Effects

## Definition

Framing effects (Tversky & Kahneman, 1981) occur when the same information presented in different ways leads to different decisions or judgments. People are not purely rational processors of information — how a message is FRAMED changes how it is evaluated, even when the underlying facts are identical.

## How It Works

### Gain vs. loss framing
The same outcome framed as a gain or loss produces different choices:
- "This program has a 90% success rate" (gain frame) → accepted
- "This program has a 10% failure rate" (loss frame) → hesitation

**Loss aversion** (Kahneman & Tversky): losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. This means loss-framed messages are more attention-getting but can also trigger avoidance.

### Attribute framing
How you describe an attribute changes its evaluation:
- "75% lean beef" vs. "25% fat beef" — same product, different perception
- "Learners retain 85% with spacing" vs. "Learners forget 15% with spacing" — same finding, different emotional impact

### Goal framing
How you frame the purpose changes behavior:
- "Implement spacing to IMPROVE retention" (approach/gain) → motivates aspiration
- "Implement spacing to PREVENT forgetting" (avoidance/loss) → motivates fear

## Application to Learning Design

| Context | Framing choice |
|---|---|
| **Stakeholder buy-in** | Frame the cost of inaction (loss) to create urgency: "Without spacing, your $500K training investment has a 4-week shelf life" |
| **Learner motivation** | Frame benefits of engagement (gain): "Completing practice improves your pass rate by 40%" |
| **Risk communication** | Use both: state the gain AND the loss to give complete picture |
| **Problem definition** | How you frame the problem determines what solutions are visible: "engagement problem" → gamification; "retention problem" → spacing + retrieval |
| **Feedback** | Reframe failure as growth: "not yet" instead of "failed" (connects to [Growth Mindset](growth-mindset.md)) |

## Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)

Framing effects are explained by prospect theory — a descriptive model of how people actually evaluate risky choices (vs. expected utility theory which describes how they "should"):

**Key features:**
- **Reference point dependence** — outcomes are evaluated as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms. A salary of $80K feels like a gain if you expected $70K and a loss if you expected $90K.
- **Loss aversion** — losses loom larger than equivalent gains (roughly 2x). Losing $100 hurts more than gaining $100 feels good. This is why loss-framed messages are more attention-getting.
- **Diminishing sensitivity** — the difference between $0 and $100 feels larger than the difference between $1,000 and $1,100, even though both are $100. This means small changes near the reference point matter most.
- **Certainty effect** — people overweight certain outcomes relative to merely probable ones. "Guaranteed 5% improvement" is more attractive than "50% chance of 12% improvement" even though the expected value is higher for the second.

**Application**: when designing choice architecture for learning programs, the reference point matters. "You're currently at Level 2; this program moves you to Level 3" (gain from current position) frames differently than "Level 3 is the standard; you're currently below it" (loss from the standard).

## Key Principles

- **Framing is unavoidable** — every message is framed somehow. The question is whether you frame deliberately or accidentally.
- **Ethical obligation**: framing for manipulation (exploiting loss aversion to pressure) is different from framing for clarity (presenting the most useful perspective). Be transparent.
- **Frame-awareness is a critical thinking skill** — teach learners to ask "how is this framed?" and "what would change if it were framed differently?"
- **Loss aversion is the strongest lever** — but use it ethically. "You will lose X" is more motivating than "You will gain X," but manufactured urgency destroys trust.
