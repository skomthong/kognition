# Connectivism

## Definition

Connectivism (Siemens, 2005) proposes that learning in the digital age occurs through connections across networks — nodes of information, people, and organizations. Knowledge is distributed across networks, and the ability to navigate, evaluate, and synthesize across nodes is more important than storing information internally. Learning is the process of creating connections and recognizing patterns.

## How It Works

Core principles:
- Learning and knowledge rest in a **diversity of opinions** across a network
- Learning is a process of **connecting** specialized nodes or information sources
- Knowledge may reside in **non-human appliances** (databases, tools, organizations)
- Capacity to know more is more critical than what is currently known
- Nurturing and maintaining connections is needed for **continuous learning**
- Currency (accurate, up-to-date knowledge) is the intent of all connectivist learning activities
- **Decision-making** itself is a learning process — choosing what to learn and interpreting incoming information through the lens of a shifting reality

## Key Principles

- Connectivism captures digital-era learning realities: knowledge changes faster than formal curricula, and professionals must continuously navigate networks to stay current.
- Design implications: support network-building (CoPs, professional learning networks, social media curation), teach information literacy, design for knowledge navigation rather than only knowledge storage.
- MOOCs and cMOOCs (connectivist MOOCs) are direct applications — open, networked, learner-driven.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** the domain changes rapidly, knowledge is distributed, and learners need to build and maintain professional networks. Complex, uncertain domains where no single source has the answer.

**Limitations:** debated as a "learning theory" vs. a descriptive lens — limited direct causal testing. Does not specify instructional sequencing or assessment. Can feel abstract without concrete design guidance. Works better as a complement to other frameworks (CoP, CoI) than as a standalone design method.
