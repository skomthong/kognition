# Collaborative Learning

## Definition

Collaborative learning is learning through structured group interaction where the group's collective understanding exceeds what individuals could achieve alone. It is distinct from cooperative learning (divided labor on a shared task), social learning (observation of models), and communities of practice (sustained informal practice communities).

## How It Works

Key mechanisms:
- **Co-construction of knowledge**: learners build shared understanding through dialogue, explanation, and negotiation
- **Cognitive conflict and resolution**: disagreement forces re-examination of assumptions and deeper processing
- **Distributed cognition**: the group can hold more working memory and bring more perspectives than any individual
- **Shared regulation**: group members monitor and regulate collective progress

## Key Principles

- Structure matters: unstructured group work produces social loafing and uneven participation. Assign roles, set individual accountability, and scaffold interaction.
- Methods: think-pair-share, jigsaw, collaborative problem-solving, peer instruction (Mazur), group investigation.
- Group composition: heterogeneous groups produce more cognitive conflict and perspective-taking; homogeneous groups may be more efficient for routine tasks.
- Adult learners bring expertise that makes peer learning especially valuable — but status differences can silence junior members. Facilitation and psychological safety are essential.
- Assess both individual and group contributions to prevent free-riding.
