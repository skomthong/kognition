# Community of Inquiry (CoI)

## Definition

The CoI framework (Garrison, Anderson, & Archer, 2000) identifies three interconnected presences that enable meaningful online learning: cognitive presence, social presence, and teaching presence.

## How It Works

- **Cognitive presence**: the extent to which learners construct meaning through sustained reflection and discourse. Four phases: triggering event → exploration → integration → resolution.
- **Social presence**: the ability to project personal characteristics and establish interpersonal relationships. Components: open communication, group cohesion, affective expression.
- **Teaching presence**: design, facilitation, and direct instruction that structures and supports cognitive and social processes. Components: design/organization, facilitating discourse, direct instruction.

All three presences must be present and balanced for deep learning to occur. Weak social presence leads to isolation; weak teaching presence leads to aimless discussion; weak cognitive presence leads to socializing without learning.

## Key Principles

- Design for cognitive presence: use triggering events (cases, problems, provocations), structured exploration, and integration tasks — not open-ended "what do you think?" prompts.
- Build social presence through icebreakers, peer introductions, small groups, and norms for respectful disagreement.
- Teaching presence is the most critical design lever: instructor visibility, timely facilitation, and clear structure.
- The CoI survey instrument can be used for course evaluation and redesign — measure each presence and target improvements.
