# Transactional Distance

## Definition

Transactional distance (Moore, 1993) is a pedagogical concept describing the psychological and communication gap between teacher and learner in distance education. It is determined by three variables: dialogue, structure, and learner autonomy.

## How It Works

- **Dialogue**: the extent and quality of interaction between teacher and learner. More dialogue reduces transactional distance.
- **Structure**: the rigidity or flexibility of the course design. Highly structured courses have more distance; flexible courses have less.
- **Learner autonomy**: the learner's ability to self-direct. Higher autonomy means the learner can tolerate more distance.

The three variables interact: a highly structured course with little dialogue requires high learner autonomy. A loosely structured course with rich dialogue can support learners with lower autonomy.

## Key Principles

- Balance structure and dialogue based on learner autonomy level — not all adult learners are equally self-directed.
- Use transactional distance as a diagnostic: if learners feel "far" despite being in the same LMS, the issue is insufficient dialogue or excessive structure, not physical location.
- For highly autonomous adult learners: provide resources and flexibility; reduce mandatory synchronous sessions.
- For less autonomous adult learners: increase facilitator visibility, structured checkpoints, and peer interaction.
