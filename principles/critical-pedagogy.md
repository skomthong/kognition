# Critical Pedagogy

## Definition

Critical pedagogy (Freire, 1970) rejects the "banking model" of education (teacher deposits knowledge into passive students) in favor of **problem-posing education** — dialogue-based learning where teacher and students co-investigate reality, develop critical consciousness (conscientization), and work toward liberation from oppressive structures.

## How It Works

- **Dialogue** replaces transmission: teacher and students are co-learners investigating shared problems
- **Problem-posing**: learning starts from the learners' lived experience and the contradictions they face
- **Conscientization**: developing awareness of social, political, and economic forces that shape one's situation
- **Praxis**: reflection and action together — understanding the world in order to change it

## Key Principles

- Relevant to adult civic education, community education, DEI programs, and empowerment contexts.
- In workplace settings: critical pedagogy surfaces power dynamics, challenges assumptions about "how things are done," and supports voice and agency for marginalized participants.
- Facilitator role: not neutral — explicitly committed to equity and dialogue, but must create genuine space for dissent and exploration.
- Pair with psychological safety: critical examination of power requires a safe container.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** the learning goal involves social awareness, power dynamics, equity, civic engagement, or organizational culture change. **Limitations:** not primarily a prescriptive "how-to" for skill teaching. Can be politically sensitive in corporate contexts. Requires skilled, committed facilitation.
