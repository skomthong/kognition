# Schema and Mental Models

## Definition

**Schemas** (Sweller/CLT) are organized knowledge structures stored in long-term memory that allow experts to process complex information as single chunks, reducing working-memory load. Schema acquisition and automation are the central learning mechanisms in cognitive load theory.

**Mental models** (Norman) are internal representations of how systems work. Norman distinguishes the target system, the designer's conceptual model, the user's mental model, and the scientist's conceptualization of that model. Mismatches between conceptual model and user mental model cause errors and slow transfer.

**Johnson-Laird** frames reasoning and comprehension as operating over internally constructed models of situations.

**Conceptual change** (Posner et al.) describes how learners replace or reorganize existing conceptions when new conceptions are intelligible, plausible, and fruitful.

## How It Works

- Novices lack schemas and must process each element individually, consuming working memory. Experts have automated schemas that bypass working-memory limits.
- Adults often have stable but sometimes faulty models; instruction should surface and test them through prediction/explanation, not just provide correct information.
- Misconceptions are often stable alternative mental models, not simple missing facts; corrections must target explanatory structure.
- Expertise grows through schema automation; as expertise rises, instructional support should shift from high guidance to purposeful practice (expertise reversal).

## Key Principles

- When the workplace system is complex (processes, software, policies), teach the conceptual model explicitly so learners can generalize beyond a click path.
- Use **concept mapping** (Novak & Gowin) to externalize knowledge structures for diagnosis and comparison.
- Use "predict what happens next" checks to surface mental models, then correct with evidence and practice.
- Use misconception-driven scenarios in safety training: show plausible but wrong actions and resulting consequences, then debrief.
