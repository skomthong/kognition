# Communities of Practice

## Definition

Wenger (1998) expands situated learning into a theory of learning through communities of practice (CoP) — groups of people who share a concern or passion for something they do and learn how to do it better through regular interaction. Three structural elements define a CoP: mutual engagement, joint enterprise, and shared repertoire.

## How It Works

- **Mutual engagement**: members interact regularly around shared work
- **Joint enterprise**: they negotiate a shared understanding of what matters
- **Shared repertoire**: they develop tools, stories, practices, and vocabulary

Learning in CoPs is not primarily instructional — it's the ongoing negotiation of meaning through participation and reification (making abstract knowledge concrete in artifacts, tools, and documents).

## Key Principles

- CoPs need facilitation and nurturing, not top-down management. Community managers sustain participation and psychological safety.
- Value can be difficult to measure — CoP outcomes are often emergent (better problem-solving, faster onboarding, innovation) rather than predetermined.
- In workplace learning: monthly case clinics, shared playbooks, scenario libraries, and knowledge artifacts are CoP expressions.
- Sustain CoPs by connecting them to real work problems, not by mandating participation.
