# Self-Regulated Learning (SRL)

## Definition

Self-regulated learning (Zimmerman, 2002) describes how learners proactively direct their own learning through cyclical phases of forethought, performance, and self-reflection. SRL is distinct from but related to metacognition (which is the knowledge/monitoring component) — SRL adds the motivational, behavioral, and environmental regulation dimensions.

## How It Works

Zimmerman's cyclical model:

1. **Forethought phase**: task analysis (goal setting, strategic planning) + self-motivation beliefs (self-efficacy, outcome expectations, intrinsic interest, goal orientation)
2. **Performance phase**: self-control (task strategies, imagery, self-instruction, time management, environmental structuring) + self-observation (self-recording, self-experimentation)
3. **Self-reflection phase**: self-judgment (self-evaluation, causal attribution) + self-reaction (self-satisfaction, adaptive/defensive inferences)

## Key Principles

- SRL is **trainable** — explicitly teach planning, monitoring, and evaluation strategies. Adults often have strong domain expertise but weak learning strategies.
- High SRL predicts achievement across domains and is especially critical for self-paced, online, and workplace learning where external structure is minimal.
- SRL connects to high-utility learning techniques: self-regulated learners choose retrieval practice and spacing over rereading and highlighting.
- Teach strategy use, not just effort — "try harder" without strategy change doesn't improve outcomes.
- Use tools that support SRL: study planners, confidence ratings, progress dashboards, reflection journals with structured prompts.
