# Metacognition

## Definition

Metacognition (Flavell) is knowledge about cognition and the monitoring and regulation of cognitive processes. Schraw & Dennison develop the Metacognitive Awareness Inventory (MAI) to assess adults' awareness across knowledge of cognition and regulation of cognition. Zimmerman frames self-regulated learning as goal setting, self-monitoring, strategy use, and self-reflection.

## How It Works

Two components:
- **Knowledge of cognition**: beliefs about self as a learner, task demands, and available strategies
- **Regulation of cognition**: planning (goal setting, strategy selection), monitoring (self-checks during learning), and evaluating (post-task reflection and adjustment)

Adults often self-direct; metacognition must be intentionally supported so learners choose high-utility strategies (retrieval practice rather than rereading) and calibrate confidence against performance. Bjork's work on desirable difficulties shows that people are often misled about effective learning — what feels fluent isn't always what's durable.

## Key Principles

- Teach metacognitive strategies explicitly at the start of adult programs — adults may have strong domain expertise but uneven learning strategy knowledge.
- Embed metacognitive prompts into assignments and simulations, not as a separate lecture topic.
- Use **confidence ratings** after retrieval quizzes; mismatches (high confidence, low accuracy) become coaching targets.
- Combine MAI-style self-assessment with behavioral indicators (practice frequency, spacing adherence) — self-report alone is unreliable.
