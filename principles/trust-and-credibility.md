# Trust and Credibility

## Definition

Trust and credibility determine whether an audience accepts, acts on, or rejects a message — regardless of the message's actual validity. In learning design, credibility governs whether learners engage with content, trust facilitators, accept feedback, and adopt new practices. Aristotle called this ethos — the most fundamental rhetorical appeal.

## How Trust Is Built and Destroyed

### Sources of credibility
| Source | How it works | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Expertise signals** | Demonstrated knowledge, credentials, track record | Cite research, show domain competence, avoid errors |
| **Transparency** | Acknowledging limits, uncertainties, conflicts | "Here's what the evidence shows AND here's what we don't know" |
| **Consistency** | Actions match words over time | Follow through on promises; consistent quality |
| **Relatability** | Shared experience, empathy, understanding context | Show you understand the learner's reality, not just theory |
| **Processing fluency** | Professional, clear, well-designed materials | [Processing Fluency](processing-fluency.md) — polished materials feel more credible |
| **Social proof** | Others trust this source | Peer endorsements, adoption data, testimonials |

### Trust destroyers
- **Overclaiming** — "This will transform your organization" without evidence
- **Errors** — factual mistakes, typos, broken links signal carelessness
- **Inconsistency** — saying one thing, doing another
- **Dismissiveness** — ignoring legitimate questions or concerns
- **Opacity** — hiding methods, data, or limitations

## Key Principles

- **Trust is earned slowly and lost quickly** — one credibility violation can undo years of good work
- **Source matters more than content for most audiences** — a trusted source with a weak argument often beats an untrusted source with a strong argument (ethos > logos for initial acceptance)
- **Evidence-level transparency builds trust** — this knowledge base's classification system (empirical/mixed/theoretical/practitioner) IS a trust-building practice
- **Facilitator credibility is the strongest lever in live learning** — a facilitator who admits uncertainty, asks genuine questions, and shows intellectual humility is more trusted than one who projects false certainty

## Application to Learning Design

- **Content credibility**: cite sources, classify evidence levels, acknowledge limitations
- **Facilitator credibility**: prepare deeply, admit what you don't know, respond to challenges with curiosity not defensiveness
- **Platform credibility**: professional design, reliable technology, responsive support
- **Organizational credibility**: follow through on learning promises, measure and report honestly
