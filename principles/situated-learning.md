# Situated Learning and Legitimate Peripheral Participation

## Definition

Lave & Wenger (1991) frame learning as legitimate peripheral participation in communities of practice — newcomers learn by participating in authentic practice alongside experienced practitioners, moving gradually from peripheral to full participation. Learning is not primarily about acquiring knowledge but about becoming a practitioner.

## How It Works

- **Legitimate**: the newcomer's participation is recognized as valid by the community
- **Peripheral**: initial tasks are low-risk but authentic — real contributions, not simulations
- **Participation**: learning happens through doing, not through instruction separated from practice

The trajectory from newcomer to full participant involves identity transformation — the learner doesn't just learn *about* the practice, they become a practitioner.

## Key Principles

- In workplaces, learning is often participation in practice; design should create pathways from observation to contribution to mastery (shadowing → co-performing → supervising).
- Apprenticeship structures with explicit criteria and fading support are direct applications.
- The learning environment IS the practice environment — separating training from work creates transfer problems.
- Access matters: if newcomers are denied legitimate participation (gatekeeping, status barriers), learning stalls.
