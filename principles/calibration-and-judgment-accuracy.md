# Calibration and Judgment Accuracy

## Definition

Calibration is the alignment between how confident you are and how accurate you actually are. Well-calibrated people are confident when they're right and uncertain when they're wrong. Poorly calibrated people are either overconfident (certain but wrong) or underconfident (uncertain but right). Calibration is distinct from accuracy — you can be accurate but poorly calibrated, or well-calibrated but frequently wrong (just honest about it).

## How It Works

| State | Confidence | Accuracy | Problem |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Well-calibrated** | Matches accuracy | Varies | None — this is the goal |
| **Overconfident** | High | Lower than believed | Most common error; dangerous for decisions |
| **Underconfident** | Low | Higher than believed | Less common; leads to unnecessary hedging, missed opportunities |
| **Dunning-Kruger zone** | High confidence + low competence | Low | Don't know what they don't know |
| **Expert imposter** | Low confidence + high competence | High | Know too much to feel certain |

## Key Principles

- **Overconfidence is the default** — across domains, people are systematically more confident than their accuracy warrants. This is one of the most robust findings in judgment research.
- **Calibration is trainable** — weather forecasters and professional gamblers show excellent calibration because they get rapid, unambiguous feedback. The mechanism: predict → check → adjust.
- **Feedback quality determines calibration** — vague feedback ("good job") doesn't calibrate. Specific, verifiable feedback ("your prediction was 80% confident but only 55% accurate") does.
- **Retrieval practice with confidence ratings** is a calibration exercise — predict your score, take the test, compare. The gap IS the learning signal. See [Metacognition](metacognition.md).
- **Calibration matters most for high-stakes decisions** — an overconfident medical diagnosis, investment decision, or assessment score interpretation can cause real harm.

## Application to Learning Design

| Context | Calibration technique |
|---|---|
| **Assessment** | Add confidence ratings to quiz items: "How sure are you?" Then show calibration curves |
| **Simulation debrief** | Compare predicted outcome vs actual: "What did you expect would happen?" |
| **Professional development** | Prediction exercises: estimate how long a task will take, then track actuals |
| **AI-assisted work** | Calibrate trust in AI: predict AI accuracy, then verify. Adjust trust accordingly |
