# Behavior Change

## Definition

Behavior change is the process by which individuals alter what they do — adopting new actions, stopping harmful ones, or sustaining beneficial ones. In adult learning, behavior change is both a goal (transfer to workplace performance) and a challenge (overcoming established routines, defaults, and environmental pressures).

## Underlying Constructs

- [Dual-Process Models](../foundations/dual-process-models.md) — behavior is governed by both automatic (System 1) and deliberative (System 2) processes. Effective interventions target both.
- [Narrative Cognition](../foundations/narrative-cognition.md) — narrative transportation can shift attitudes and behavioral intentions, serving as a pathway to belief-driven behavior change.

## How It Works

The COM-B model (Michie, van Stralen, & West) provides a comprehensive diagnostic: behavior occurs when three conditions are met:

- **Capability** — the person has the physical and psychological capacity (knowledge, skills, stamina)
- **Opportunity** — the social and physical environment permits or supports the behavior
- **Motivation** — reflective (goals, plans, beliefs) and automatic (emotions, impulses, habits) motivation are sufficient

The **Behaviour Change Wheel** wraps COM-B in a larger system: once the COM-B diagnosis identifies what's missing, intervention functions (education, persuasion, incentivization, coercion, training, restriction, environmental restructuring, modeling, enablement) and policy categories can be selected systematically.

## Factors That Influence It

**Enhancers:**
- Environmental restructuring (defaults, cues, friction reduction) — small environmental changes can produce outsized behavioral effects
- Social norms and modeling — visible peer behavior shapes what feels normal
- Timely prompts at decision points
- Transparent, autonomy-preserving framing (opt-out rather than coercion)

**Inhibitors:**
- Status quo bias and loss aversion (System 1 defaults)
- Weak transfer climate — the work environment doesn't reinforce what was learned
- Competing motivations and role overload (common in adult learners)
- Opaque or coercive intervention design (ethical backlash, resistance)

## Methods That Leverage This

- [Choice Architecture](../methods/choice-architecture.md) — nudges, defaults, MINDSPACE, EAST frameworks
- [Gamification](../techniques/narrative/gamification.md) — game mechanics as persistence and motivation supports
- [Scenario-Based Learning](../techniques/narrative/scenario-based-learning.md) — practicing decisions in consequence-rich contexts
- [Entertainment-Education](../techniques/narrative/entertainment-education.md) — narrative transportation as a population-scale behavior change mechanism
