# Growth Mindset

## Definition

Dweck's implicit theories of intelligence distinguish **fixed mindset** (belief that ability is innate and unchangeable) from **growth mindset** (belief that ability can be developed through effort, strategy, and feedback). Mindset shapes how learners interpret difficulty, failure, and feedback.

## How It Works

- Fixed mindset: difficulty signals low ability → avoid challenges, give up easily, ignore feedback, feel threatened by others' success
- Growth mindset: difficulty signals a learning opportunity → embrace challenges, persist through setbacks, learn from feedback, find inspiration in others' success

Meta-analyses show small-to-moderate positive effects of mindset interventions, particularly for specific populations (students facing transitions, those with lower prior achievement). Recent replications have moderated initial effect-size claims — the effect is real but smaller and more context-dependent than early popular accounts suggested.

## Key Principles

- For adult learners: mindset framing affects willingness to attempt difficult tasks, seek feedback, and persist after failure — all critical for professional development.
- Design implications: frame difficulty as expected and productive ("not yet" rather than "failed"), emphasize strategy and effort over innate talent, normalize asking for help.
- Avoid superficial applications: praising effort alone without strategy guidance can backfire. Effective mindset support involves teaching concrete strategies, not just positive messaging.
- Boundary conditions: mindset interventions are not a universal fix. They work best as brief, targeted interventions alongside structural changes (better feedback systems, scaffolded challenge, reduced threat).
