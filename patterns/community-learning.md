# Community Learning Patterns

## Goal

Design learning programs around sustained community interaction — cohort-based experiences, alumni ecosystems, and peer learning networks — where the community IS the learning environment, not just a supplement to content delivery.

## Pattern Types

### Cohort-based learning
| Feature | Design choice |
|---|---|
| **Fixed group** | Same people throughout the program — builds trust, shared context, accountability |
| **Shared timeline** | Everyone progresses together — enables discussion, peer feedback, collaborative projects |
| **Facilitated interaction** | Structured discussions, peer coaching pairs, group projects — not just "a forum" |
| **Ritual and identity** | Cohort naming, shared experiences, graduation — creates belonging |

Best for: certification programs, leadership development, executive education, professional development.

### Alumni/continuing community
| Feature | Design choice |
|---|---|
| **Persistent platform** | Stays active after formal program ends — Slack, community platform, LMS group |
| **Valued artifacts** | Shared playbooks, case libraries, templates — reasons to return |
| **Regular touchpoints** | Monthly calls, quarterly meetups, annual events — sustain connection |
| **Peer expertise** | Members help each other — the community becomes a knowledge resource |

Best for: post-program transfer support, professional networks, sustained learning.

### Peer learning network
| Feature | Design choice |
|---|---|
| **Self-organizing** | Members choose topics, form groups, set agendas |
| **Diverse membership** | Cross-functional, cross-level, cross-organization for fresh perspectives |
| **Problem-centered** | Real challenges as learning material — not curriculum, but practice |
| **Light facilitation** | Community manager nurtures but doesn't control |

Best for: informal professional development, innovation, cross-functional learning.

## Design Principles

- **Community needs facilitation, not management** — nurture participation, model vulnerability, surface interesting threads, connect people. Don't control.
- **Psychological safety is prerequisite** — without safety, people lurk rather than contribute. Establish norms explicitly and early.
- **Value exchange must be clear** — members stay when they GET value (answers, connections, resources) AND when they GIVE value (contributing feels meaningful). If it's one-way, people disengage.
- **Content alone won't sustain community** — content is the seed; interaction is the soil. A library without discussion is a database, not a community.
- **Measure participation quality, not quantity** — 5 substantive exchanges beat 50 emoji reactions. ICAP applies: constructive and interactive contributions matter most.

## Checklist

- [ ] Community purpose and value proposition are clear to members
- [ ] Psychological safety norms established and modeled
- [ ] Structured interaction (not just "post when you want")
- [ ] Regular facilitated touchpoints sustain rhythm
- [ ] Shared artifacts give reasons to return
- [ ] Facilitation/community management resourced and trained
- [ ] Success measured by participation quality and member outcomes, not just activity volume
