# Strategy Simulation Programs

## Goal

Design programs where leaders practice strategic decision-making in simulated environments with realistic complexity, competing stakeholders, imperfect information, and consequential trade-offs. These extend [simulations](../../methods/simulations.md) from tactical/procedural skills to strategic/judgment skills.

## Design Components

| Component | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Simulated environment** | Market, organization, or crisis context with realistic dynamics |
| **Roles** | Participants play leadership roles with specific responsibilities and constraints |
| **Information asymmetry** | Different teams/roles have different data — mirrors real strategic decisions |
| **Time pressure** | Decisions must be made before the next "turn" or event |
| **Consequences** | Decisions produce outcomes that affect subsequent rounds |
| **Competitor/stakeholder dynamics** | Other teams or facilitator-driven actors create realistic pressure |
| **Structured debrief** | The primary learning mechanism — reasoning analysis, not outcome celebration |

## Program Types

| Type | Structure | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Business simulation** | Multi-round game with market dynamics, financial outcomes | MBA/executive education, strategic thinking |
| **Crisis simulation** | Rapid-fire scenario with escalating stakes and time pressure | Crisis leadership, emergency management |
| **Wargame** | Adversarial teams competing with imperfect information | Competitive strategy, scenario planning |
| **Policy simulation** | Multiple stakeholders negotiating competing interests | Government, NGO, cross-sector leadership |
| **Organizational simulation** | Internal dynamics: budget, talent, politics, culture | Change leadership, organizational design |

## Key Principles

- **Complexity, not complication** — strategic simulations should involve genuine uncertainty and emergence ([Cynefin](../../methods/cynefin-framework.md) Complex domain), not just many variables.
- **Debrief is 50% of the time** — the simulation generates the experience; the [debrief](../../techniques/instructional/debriefing-techniques.md) generates the learning. Never skip it for "one more round."
- **Decisions > outcomes** — evaluate reasoning quality: "What did you consider? What did you miss? What would you do differently?" A team that made good decisions but lost to luck learned more than a team that won by accident.
- **Realistic messiness** — incomplete data, conflicting stakeholder demands, ambiguous signals. Clean simulations produce unrealistically confident decision-makers.
- **Safe-to-fail** — the simulation IS the safe space. Debrief must reinforce that failure in simulation is learning, not judgment of competence.
