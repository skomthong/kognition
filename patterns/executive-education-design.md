# Executive Education Design

## Goal

Design learning for senior professionals who have deep domain expertise, severe time constraints, high autonomy expectations, and zero tolerance for content that isn't immediately relevant to their challenges.

## Design Principles

- **Respect their expertise** — executives are NOT novices. They bring decades of experience. Design must leverage that experience (andragogy), not ignore it.
- **Relevance or nothing** — if they can't connect it to a decision they face THIS QUARTER, they disengage. Every activity should have an obvious "so what for me."
- **Case method and peer learning** — the most valuable resource in the room is each other's experience. Use Christensen-style cases, peer problem-solving clinics, and structured peer feedback.
- **Compressed intensity** — 2-day immersive modules beat 8 weekly sessions for executives. But build in spacing: pre-work before, application assignments after, booster check-ins at 30/60 days.
- **Facilitation over instruction** — executives don't want to be taught; they want to be challenged, provoked, and given frameworks to apply.
- **Psychological safety at the top is rare** — senior leaders rarely get honest feedback. Create conditions where vulnerability is possible (Chatham House rules, small cohorts, skilled facilitation).
- **Transfer is the only metric that matters** — did they DO something different after? Track behavioral commitments and follow through.

## Checklist

- [ ] Content relevance validated with participants or their stakeholders before design
- [ ] Case method or problem-centered learning as primary pedagogy
- [ ] Peer learning structured (not "networking breaks" — structured case clinics, peer coaching)
- [ ] Pre-work and post-program application assignments
- [ ] Facilitator is a peer-level expert, not a junior instructor
- [ ] Psychological safety actively designed (norms, small groups, confidentiality)
- [ ] Transfer measured through behavioral commitments and follow-up
