# Conflict and Tension

## Summary

Conflict is the engine of narrative — without it, there is no story, only description. In learning design, conflict creates the NEED to think, decide, and act. A scenario without genuine conflict is an illustrated lecture. Tension is the emotional state conflict produces in the audience — the uncertainty about what will happen and what should be done.

## Types of Conflict

| Type | Description | Learning application |
|---|---|---|
| **Person vs. Person** | Disagreement, competing interests, power dynamics | Stakeholder negotiations, team conflicts, client interactions |
| **Person vs. Self** | Internal struggle, values conflict, doubt | Ethical dilemmas, leadership identity, career decisions |
| **Person vs. System** | Organizational constraints, policy limits, bureaucracy | Compliance tension, resource constraints, change resistance |
| **Person vs. Environment** | Time pressure, market conditions, technology failure | Crisis management, safety scenarios, adaptation under uncertainty |

The richest learning scenarios combine multiple types — a protagonist facing a person vs. person conflict while also wrestling with person vs. self (ethical doubt) under person vs. environment pressure (deadline).

## Creating Tension

- **Stakes**: what happens if the protagonist chooses wrong? Higher stakes = more tension. But stakes must be REALISTIC — melodrama undermines adult credibility.
- **Incomplete information**: the protagonist (and learner) doesn't have all the facts. This mirrors real decision-making.
- **Time pressure**: a deadline forces action before certainty is possible.
- **Competing goods**: the hardest decisions aren't between good and bad — they're between two goods that can't both be served (efficiency vs. thoroughness, honesty vs. diplomacy, individual vs. team).
- **Consequences that ripple**: early decisions affect later options. This teaches systems thinking.

## Productive Tension in Learning

Tension in scenarios parallels [desirable difficulties](../../principles/desirable-difficulties.md) in retrieval practice — it feels uncomfortable but produces deeper processing. The key is ensuring the tension is PRODUCTIVE:
- **Productive**: "I'm not sure what the right answer is — I need to think carefully" (cognitive engagement)
- **Unproductive**: "I'm confused by the scenario setup" (extraneous load) or "I'm anxious about being judged" (psychological threat)

[Psychological safety](../../principles/psychological-safety.md) is the container that makes productive tension possible.

## How To Practice

1. Take a flat scenario and add one conflict of each type. Which one creates the most interesting decision?
2. Write a scenario where the protagonist faces competing goods — no clearly "right" answer. Test with colleagues: do they disagree about what to do? If yes, you've created real tension.
3. Add time pressure to an existing scenario. Notice how it changes the decision dynamic.
