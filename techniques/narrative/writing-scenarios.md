# Writing Scenarios and Cases

## Summary

Scenarios and cases are the primary vehicle for practice-based learning in adult contexts. Writing effective scenarios is a craft skill that combines narrative design with instructional alignment — the story must serve the learning objective, not just engage.

## Scenario Anatomy

| Component | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Setting** | Establish realistic context | "You're a project manager at a mid-size tech firm. It's Thursday afternoon..." |
| **Role** | Place the learner in a decision-making position | "You need to decide whether to escalate this to the VP or handle it yourself" |
| **Constraints** | Create realistic pressure (time, resources, stakeholders) | "The client meeting is in 2 hours. Your lead developer just called in sick." |
| **Decision point** | Force a choice that reveals understanding | "Do you: (A) reschedule, (B) present without the demo, (C) assign a backup" |
| **Consequences** | Show realistic outcomes of choices | Each option leads to a different result with trade-offs |
| **Debrief prompt** | Extract the learning from the experience | "What cues did you rely on? What did you trade off? What would you do differently?" |

## Writing Techniques

### Linear scenarios (single decision)
- Best for: focused practice on one skill or decision type
- Structure: context → decision → consequence → debrief
- Keep context lean — only include details that inform the decision

### Branching scenarios (multiple decisions)
- Best for: complex judgment, consequence chains, "ripple effect" understanding
- Use **bounded branching**: meaningful branches that converge via "foldback" nodes to avoid exponential content growth
- Rule of thumb: 3-4 decision points with 2-3 options each is manageable; more than that becomes authoring hell
- Show **delayed consequences** — the impact of early decisions surfaces later, teaching systems thinking

### Character-driven cases
- Use a protagonist whose constraints match learner constraints
- Make the character's dilemma genuine — no obviously "right" answer
- Include secondary characters (stakeholders, team members, clients) who create competing demands

## Common Mistakes

- **Content dump disguised as scenario**: "Read this 3-page policy, then answer a question about it" — that's a reading comprehension test, not a scenario
- **Obvious right answer**: if one option is clearly correct and the others are absurd, there's no decision practice
- **Melodrama over relevance**: emotional stakes should come from realistic role pressure, not soap opera drama
- **No debrief**: a scenario without reflection is entertainment, not learning
- **Gaming the branches**: learners click randomly to find the "right" path — mitigate with reflection prompts and delayed consequences

## How To Practice This Skill

1. Start from a real incident or common decision — interview SMEs for "war stories"
2. Identify the decision type the objective targets (diagnostic, ethical, prioritization, conflict resolution)
3. Write the decision point FIRST, then build context backward (what information does the learner need to make this decision?)
4. Write plausible wrong options based on real misconceptions or common errors
5. Playtest with 3-5 learners: do they understand the context? Is the decision genuinely hard?
6. Write the debrief prompts: rationale capture, alternative generation, principle extraction
