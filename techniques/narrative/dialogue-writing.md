# Dialogue Writing

## Summary

Dialogue in learning scenarios serves three functions: revealing character (how someone speaks shows who they are), advancing the situation (new information, conflict, decisions), and creating realism (adult learners engage more deeply with authentic-sounding conversations than with narrated summaries).

## Key Principles

### Each character sounds different
- A CEO speaks differently from a frontline worker. A nervous new hire sounds different from a confident veteran.
- Differentiate through: vocabulary, sentence length, directness, formality, what they notice and what they ignore.

### Dialogue carries subtext
What characters DON'T say is as important as what they do:
- "That's an interesting approach" can mean "I think that's wrong but I'm being polite"
- "I'll take it under advisement" can mean "No"
- Subtext creates the realistic ambiguity that develops interpretive skill in learners

### Exposition through conversation (not info-dumps)
*Bad:* "As you know, our company was founded in 1985 and has 5,000 employees across 12 countries..."
*Good:* "I've been here 15 years and I've never seen the board this nervous about a product launch."

Characters should reveal information naturally — because they need to, not because the learner needs to hear it.

### Conflict lives in dialogue
- Disagreements, competing perspectives, and power dynamics play out in conversation
- "I hear you, but the numbers don't support that" vs. "You're wrong" — different characters, different approaches, different learner reactions

## Common Mistakes

- **All characters sound the same** — same vocabulary, same sentence length, same register
- **On-the-nose dialogue** — characters saying exactly what they mean with no subtext ("I'm angry because you didn't follow the process")
- **Info-dump dialogue** — characters explaining things they would both already know
- **Perfect dialogue** — real people hesitate, interrupt, misspeak, and trail off. Overly polished dialogue feels fake.

## How To Practice

1. Write a 6-line dialogue between two characters who disagree. Can a reader tell which character is which without dialogue tags?
2. Rewrite a narrated scenario section as a dialogue scene. What information do you need to embed naturally?
3. Listen to a real workplace conversation and note: how do people actually talk? Where do they hedge, interrupt, change topic?
