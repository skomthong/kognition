# Literary Techniques

## Summary

Literary techniques are specific narrative devices that create surprise, emphasis, complexity, or emotional impact. Learning designers don't write novels — but selective use of these techniques makes scenarios richer, cases more engaging, and communications more memorable.

## Technique Catalog

| Technique | What it does | Learning application |
|---|---|---|
| **Juxtaposition** | Places contrasting elements side by side to highlight difference | "Before/after" scenarios; contrasting expert vs. novice approaches; good vs. bad examples |
| **Irony** | Gap between expectation and reality | Scenarios where "best practices" produce bad outcomes; challenging assumptions |
| **Satire** | Uses humor/exaggeration to critique | Highlighting organizational dysfunction; making compliance failures vivid and memorable (use carefully) |
| **Humor** | Creates connection, reduces threat, aids memory | Opening hooks, lightening heavy topics, building facilitator rapport. But: humor that excludes or mocks undermines safety |
| **Foreshadowing** | Hints at what's coming | Early details in a case that become crucial at the decision point — teaches attention to cues |
| **Flashback** | Jumps to an earlier time | Case history that explains current situation; reflection on how we got here |
| **Fragmentation** | Presents incomplete, non-linear pieces | Puzzle-based scenarios where the learner assembles the picture; mirrors real-world incomplete information |
| **Unreliable narrator** | Narrator's account is biased or incomplete | Cases with conflicting stakeholder perspectives; teaches critical evaluation of sources |
| **In medias res** | Start in the middle of the action | "The phone rings at 2 AM. It's the hospital." — hook first, context later |

## Key Principles

- **Purpose over cleverness**: use a literary technique because it serves the learning goal, not because it's impressive
- **Satire and humor require psychological safety**: what's funny to the writer may be threatening to a learner who identifies with the target
- **Juxtaposition is the most versatile**: before/after, good/bad, expert/novice contrasts are immediately applicable to any learning context
- **Fragmentation mirrors reality**: real decision-makers work with incomplete information. Presenting fragmented information teaches assembly and judgment skills.

## How To Practice

1. Start a scenario in medias res — drop the learner into the crisis, then provide context.
2. Write two versions of the same event from two different (conflicting) perspectives. Use both in a case to force the learner to evaluate reliability.
3. Add one piece of foreshadowing to an existing scenario — an early detail that becomes crucial at the decision point.
