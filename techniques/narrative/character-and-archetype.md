# Character and Archetype

## Summary

Characters are the vehicles through which learners enter a story. A well-designed protagonist gives the learner someone to identify with, make decisions through, and learn from. Archetypes (Jung, Campbell) are universal character patterns that audiences recognize intuitively — they provide structure for character design without requiring original invention.

## Protagonist Design for Learning

The protagonist in a learning scenario should:
- **Face the learner's actual constraints** — time pressure, incomplete information, stakeholder conflict, resource limits. If the protagonist's world is unrealistically clean, the learner can't transfer.
- **Have a clear motivation** — what do they want? What's at stake?
- **Have a relevant flaw or gap** — not incompetent, but facing something they don't yet know how to handle. This is the learning opportunity.
- **Be relatable** — close enough to the learner's role and experience that identification is natural

### Christensen case method protagonist
In HBS-style cases: the protagonist is a specific named person, in a specific role, facing a specific decision, at a specific moment. The case is THEIR story. The learner steps into THEIR shoes.

## Key Archetypes (Applied to Learning)

| Archetype | Role in story | Learning application |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero/Protagonist** | Faces the challenge, makes the decisions | The learner's surrogate — the character they identify with |
| **Mentor** | Provides guidance, tools, wisdom | The facilitator, coach, or SME in the narrative |
| **Threshold Guardian** | Blocks entry, tests readiness | Gatekeepers, skeptics, compliance requirements |
| **Shapeshifter** | Allegiance unclear, challenges trust | Stakeholders with hidden agendas, ambiguous situations |
| **Shadow** | Opposes the protagonist, embodies the threat | Competing priorities, organizational inertia, the "wrong" approach |
| **Trickster** | Disrupts, challenges assumptions, adds humor | Comic relief, the colleague who asks the uncomfortable question |

## Character Dimension

Flat characters serve simple scenarios. Dimensional characters serve complex ones:
- **Motivation**: what they want (external goal) and what they need (internal growth)
- **Flaw**: what holds them back — overconfidence, avoidance, rigidity, fear
- **Growth**: how the experience changes them (or doesn't — in cautionary tales)
- **Voice**: how they speak, think, and react — distinct from other characters

## How To Practice

1. Design a protagonist for a scenario: name, role, motivation, flaw, decision they face. Can you describe them in 3 sentences?
2. Add a secondary character who creates tension — a stakeholder who disagrees with the protagonist. What's THEIR motivation?
3. Analyze a case you've used: is the protagonist dimensional or flat? Does the learner identify with them?
