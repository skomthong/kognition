# Story Arc

## Summary

A story arc is the structural shape of a narrative — the pattern of rising tension, complication, and resolution that carries the audience from beginning to end. Different arc shapes produce different emotional and cognitive effects. Understanding arc shapes lets learning designers choose the right structure for the learning goal.

## Major Arc Models

### Freytag's Pyramid (1863)
The classic five-act dramatic structure:
1. **Exposition** — setting, characters, status quo
2. **Rising action** — complications build, stakes increase
3. **Climax** — the moment of highest tension, the turning point
4. **Falling action** — consequences unfold
5. **Denouement** — resolution, new equilibrium

### Three-Act Structure
Simplified version used in screenwriting and case design:
- **Act 1: Setup** — establish the world, the protagonist, and the problem
- **Act 2: Confrontation** — obstacles, complications, rising stakes, failed attempts
- **Act 3: Resolution** — climax and outcome

### Campbell's Hero's Journey (Monomyth)
A 12-stage cycle (simplified): ordinary world → call to adventure → refusal → meeting the mentor → crossing the threshold → tests/allies/enemies → ordeal → reward → return → transformation. Most useful for leadership development and identity-transformation narratives.

### Vonnegut's Story Shapes
Vonnegut mapped stories as simple emotional curves plotting fortune (good/bad) over time:

| Shape | Pattern | Example | Learning application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Man in a Hole** | Good → bad → good (recovery) | Character faces a setback, overcomes it | Resilience scenarios, troubleshooting cases |
| **Boy Meets Girl** | Good → great → bad → great (loss and recovery) | Something wonderful gained, lost, regained | Change management, adoption narratives |
| **Cinderella** | Bad → good → very good → bad → very good | Rise, setback, ultimate triumph | Leadership journey, professional growth |
| **Kafka** | Bad → worse (no recovery) | Steady decline, no resolution | Cautionary tales, ethics cases, safety scenarios |
| **Which Way Is Up?** | Ambiguous, oscillating | Unclear whether things are getting better or worse | Complexity, moral ambiguity, reflective cases |

Vonnegut's insight: the SHAPE of the story is what audiences respond to emotionally, independent of content.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Scenario-based learning**: map the emotional arc of your scenario. Does tension rise? Is there a genuine climax (decision point)? Is there resolution (consequences + debrief)?
- **Case writing**: "Man in a Hole" is the default business case shape — protagonist faces a crisis, must decide how to recover
- **Program narrative**: a multi-week program can follow an arc — early success (rising), mid-program challenge (complication), capstone (climax/resolution)
- **Microlearning series**: each episode can have its own mini-arc while the series follows a larger one

## How To Practice

1. Map the emotional arc of a scenario you've written. Does it have a clear shape? If it's flat, add tension.
2. Choose a Vonnegut shape and write a 200-word scenario that follows it.
3. Analyze a case study you admire: what's the arc shape? Where is the climax?
