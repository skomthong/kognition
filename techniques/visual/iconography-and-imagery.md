# Iconography and Imagery

## Summary

Images and icons in learning materials are either instructional (they carry meaning that supports learning) or decorative (they fill space). CTML research shows that instructional images improve learning while decorative images can harm it by adding extraneous cognitive load (seductive details effect). Every image must earn its place.

## Instructional vs. Decorative

| Type | Purpose | Example | Effect on learning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Instructional** | Explains, demonstrates, or organizes information | Process diagram, annotated screenshot, data chart, concept map | Positive — supports dual coding |
| **Representational** | Shows what something looks like | Photo of equipment, screenshot of interface | Neutral to positive — aids recognition |
| **Organizational** | Shows structure and relationships | Flowchart, hierarchy diagram, timeline | Positive — reduces integration load |
| **Decorative** | Fills space, creates visual interest | Stock photo of smiling people, abstract patterns | Neutral to negative — can distract |

**Rule**: if an image doesn't help the learner understand, remember, or do something, remove it.

## Icon Design Principles

- **Recognizable**: the icon should be understood without a label (test: can someone identify its meaning without text?)
- **Consistent style**: all icons in a set should share the same style (outline, filled, line weight, size)
- **Consistent meaning**: one icon = one meaning throughout the entire experience
- **Size**: icons should be large enough to be recognized (24px minimum) and small enough not to dominate
- **Accessibility**: icons should NOT be the sole way to convey meaning — pair with text labels

## Image Selection

- **Relevance**: does this image relate to the content? Would a different image serve better?
- **Diversity and representation**: people in images should reflect the audience's diversity
- **Cultural sensitivity**: gestures, settings, and scenarios carry cultural meaning — validate with representative users
- **Quality**: low-resolution or watermarked images undermine credibility
- **Stock vs. custom**: custom illustrations/screenshots are usually more instructionally relevant than generic stock photos

## How To Practice

1. Audit a learning module: classify every image as instructional, representational, organizational, or decorative. Remove the decorative ones. Does the module improve?
2. Replace a paragraph of text with a diagram. Does the diagram convey the same information more efficiently?
3. Choose icons for 5 concepts in this knowledge base. Test: can someone guess the meaning without labels?
