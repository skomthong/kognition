# Decision Visualization

## Summary

Decision visualization makes trade-offs, risks, alternatives, and their consequences visible — so decision-makers can evaluate options rather than just see data. It bridges [data storytelling](../narrative/data-storytelling.md) (guiding interpretation) and [executive communication](../writing/executive-communication.md) (supporting action).

## Types

| Type | What it shows | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Decision matrix** | Options × criteria, scored and weighted | Structured comparison of alternatives (vendor selection, approach comparison) |
| **Trade-off chart** | Two competing dimensions with options plotted | When decisions involve inherent tensions (cost vs quality, speed vs thoroughness) |
| **Scenario comparison** | Side-by-side outcomes under different assumptions | When the future is uncertain and multiple paths are plausible |
| **Risk matrix** | Likelihood × impact grid with items plotted | Prioritizing risks, deciding what to mitigate |
| **Decision tree** | Branching choices with probabilities and outcomes | Sequential decisions with quantifiable probabilities |
| **Pros/cons with weighting** | Structured argument for and against, with relative importance | When qualitative factors dominate |
| **Tornado/sensitivity chart** | Which variables have the biggest impact on the outcome | Identifying which assumptions matter most |
| **Before/after comparison** | Current state vs proposed state | Making the case for change |

## Key Principles

- **Make the trade-off explicit** — every decision involves giving something up. If the visual hides the trade-off, it misleads.
- **Show uncertainty honestly** — ranges, scenarios, and confidence levels. A single-point projection is a false promise. See [Uncertainty Visualization](uncertainty-visualization.md).
- **Frame the question first** — "Which vendor?" is a comparison. "Should we invest?" is a yes/no with threshold. "How much risk?" is a tolerance assessment. The question determines the visual.
- **Annotate the recommendation** — don't just show options; indicate which you recommend and why. Decision-makers want guidance, not just data.
- **Include the "do nothing" option** — the status quo is always an alternative. Make its consequences visible.

## How To Practice

1. Take a decision you're facing and build a decision matrix: options as rows, criteria as columns, scores in cells. Does the visual clarify your thinking?
2. Create a trade-off chart for two competing design approaches. Plot them on two dimensions that matter to stakeholders.
3. Show the same recommendation with and without uncertainty ranges. Notice how the confidence of the recommendation changes.
