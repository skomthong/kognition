# Visual Composition

## Summary

Visual composition is the arrangement of elements on a page or screen to guide the viewer's eye, establish hierarchy, and communicate clearly. In learning design, good composition reduces extraneous cognitive load — the viewer understands the structure before reading a word.

## Core Principles

### Visual hierarchy
Not all elements are equally important. Hierarchy tells the viewer what to look at first, second, and third.

Tools: size (bigger = more important), weight (bold = more important), color (contrast = attention), position (top-left in Western reading cultures = first), white space (isolation = emphasis)

### Alignment
Elements that are aligned look organized and intentional. Elements that are slightly off look like mistakes.

- Align to a grid or to other elements — never "eyeball it"
- Left-align text by default (easier to scan than center-aligned)
- Consistent margins create visual rhythm

### Proximity (Gestalt)
Elements that are close together are perceived as related. Elements that are far apart are perceived as separate.

- Group related content visually (label near its data, heading near its content)
- Separate unrelated content with white space, not lines

### White space
Empty space is not wasted space — it's a design element that:
- Reduces visual clutter (extraneous load)
- Creates emphasis (isolated elements draw attention)
- Gives the eye a resting point
- Signals grouping (space between groups, closeness within groups)

### Balance
Visual weight should feel distributed — not necessarily symmetrically, but deliberately.

- Symmetrical balance: formal, stable, predictable (good for data-heavy displays)
- Asymmetrical balance: dynamic, engaging, directional (good for storytelling and emphasis)

### Figure-ground
The viewer must instantly distinguish the focal element (figure) from the background (ground). Ambiguity between figure and ground creates confusion.

## How To Practice

1. Take a cluttered slide and redesign it using only: alignment, proximity, and white space. Don't change the content.
2. Squint at a page layout. Can you see the hierarchy without reading? If not, the visual composition needs work.
3. Screenshot 3 learning screens you find effective. Analyze: what creates the hierarchy? Where is white space doing work?
