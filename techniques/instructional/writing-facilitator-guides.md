# Writing Facilitator Guides

## Summary

A facilitator guide is the operational document that enables someone other than the designer to deliver a learning experience effectively. It bridges design intent and delivery execution — without it, facilitated learning depends entirely on the original designer being in the room.

## What a Facilitator Guide Should Contain

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Session overview** | Objectives, duration, audience, prerequisites, materials needed |
| **Setup checklist** | Room layout, technology setup, materials prep, pre-session tasks |
| **Timed agenda** | Minute-by-minute flow with time allocations per activity |
| **Activity instructions** | For each activity: what to say, what to do, how to transition |
| **Facilitation notes** | Tips, common questions, how to handle difficult moments, what to watch for |
| **Debrief prompts** | Specific questions for each activity debrief — not "discuss" but exact prompts |
| **Assessment guidance** | What to observe, rubric criteria, how to provide feedback |
| **Contingency plans** | What to do if time runs short, technology fails, or the group is unresponsive |
| **Materials and handouts** | All participant-facing materials, referenced by activity |

## Key Principles

- **Write for someone who wasn't in the design meetings** — the guide should be self-contained enough for a competent facilitator to deliver without the designer present
- **Script key moments**: opening framing, activity transitions, debrief questions, and closing. These are the moments where facilitation quality matters most.
- **Include the "why"**: explain why each activity exists and what it accomplishes — facilitators who understand the design rationale make better in-the-moment decisions
- **Time everything**: facilitators need realistic time estimates, including transition time, not just content time
- **Debrief prompts are the most important section**: a scenario without good debrief prompts wastes the experience. Write 3-5 specific questions per activity, ordered from descriptive ("What happened?") to analytical ("What principles apply?") to transfer ("How will you apply this?")

## Common Mistakes

- **Too vague**: "Facilitate a discussion about the topic" — what discussion? What prompts? How long?
- **Content script without activity design**: a facilitator guide that reads like a lecture script means the design is lecture-based
- **No timing**: facilitators routinely run out of time or pad with unplanned content when timing is missing
- **No contingency**: everything goes wrong eventually — the guide should prepare for it

## How To Practice This Skill

1. Write a facilitator guide for a 30-minute session you've designed
2. Hand it to a colleague who wasn't involved in the design and ask them to deliver it
3. Observe: where do they get confused? Where do they deviate? Where do they need more guidance?
4. Revise based on their experience — repeat
