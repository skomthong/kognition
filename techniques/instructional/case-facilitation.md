# Case Facilitation

## Summary

Case facilitation is the art of leading a group through a case discussion so that learners develop analytical judgment through their OWN reasoning — not through the facilitator's lecture about the case. The facilitator asks questions, manages the flow, creates productive tension, and connects contributions without providing the answer. It is the delivery skill that makes [case writing](../../techniques/writing/case-writing.md) effective.

## The Christensen Approach

### Facilitator role
- **NOT a lecturer** who reveals the right answer
- **NOT a passive moderator** who lets conversation wander
- **A choreographer** who sequences questions, manages energy, builds argument, and surfaces disagreement

### Discussion arc (typical 75-90 minute case)

| Phase | Purpose | Facilitator moves |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening** (5-10 min) | Establish the facts, frame the decision | Call on someone to set up the case: "What's the situation? What decision does [protagonist] face?" |
| **Analysis** (30-40 min) | Examine the data, surface different perspectives, build competing arguments | "What does Exhibit 3 tell us?" "Who disagrees?" "What's the strongest argument AGAINST what you just said?" |
| **Decision** (15-20 min) | Force a choice and defend it | "What would you DO? Why? What's the risk?" "Who would do something different?" |
| **Generalization** (10-15 min) | Extract transferable principles | "What principle does this case illustrate?" "When would this analysis apply in YOUR context?" |

### Key Facilitation Techniques

| Technique | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold call** | Keeps everyone prepared; prevents hiding | "Maria, what's your analysis of the financial data?" |
| **Redirect** | Builds dialogue between participants, not facilitator-student ping-pong | "Interesting — James, do you agree with Maria's analysis?" |
| **Probe** | Pushes past surface answers | "Why do you say that? What evidence supports that? What would change your mind?" |
| **Play devil's advocate** | Surfaces the counterargument | "Let me push back — couldn't you argue that [opposite position]?" |
| **Synthesize** | Connect disparate contributions into a coherent thread | "So we have three perspectives on the table: A argues X, B argues Y, C argues Z. What's the core tension?" |
| **Board work** | Make the discussion visible — organize arguments on the whiteboard | Track competing positions, evidence, and decision criteria visually |
| **Wait time** | Resist filling silence — let participants think | Count to 7 silently after asking a hard question |

## Key Principles

- **The facilitator knows the case inside out** — preparation is 80% of facilitation quality. Read the case 3+ times. Know every exhibit. Anticipate every argument.
- **Questions > statements** — every time you're tempted to explain, convert it to a question. "What's missing from that analysis?" not "You're missing the cost data."
- **Productive disagreement** — the best case discussions have genuine disagreement. If everyone agrees, the case is too easy or the facilitation is too leading.
- **Psychological safety is non-negotiable** — cold calls and disagreement require trust. Establish norms: every contribution is valued, disagreement is intellectual not personal, being wrong is learning.
- **Debrief the principles, not the "answer"** — many cases don't have a single right answer. The learning is in the ANALYSIS PROCESS, not the conclusion.

## How To Practice

1. Prepare a case as if you'll facilitate it: write 10 questions sequenced across the four phases. For each question, anticipate 2-3 likely responses and your follow-up.
2. Facilitate a 30-minute case discussion. Record it. Count: what percentage of the time did YOU talk vs. participants? (Target: facilitator <30%)
3. Practice the redirect: next time a participant answers your question, instead of responding, redirect to another participant: "Do you agree?"
