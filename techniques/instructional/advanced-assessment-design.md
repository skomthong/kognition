# Advanced Assessment Design

## Summary

Advanced assessment design goes beyond writing individual items ([Writing Assessment Items](writing-assessment-items.md)) to the systematic design of entire assessment programs — blueprinting, validity arguments, reliability analysis, item analysis, and defensible scoring. Essential for certification, credentialing, and any context where assessment decisions have consequences.

## Assessment Blueprinting

A blueprint specifies WHAT gets assessed and HOW MUCH, ensuring the assessment represents the full domain:

| Component | What it specifies |
|---|---|
| **Content coverage** | Which competencies/topics are tested, in what proportion |
| **Cognitive level** | Distribution across Bloom levels (not all recall; not all evaluation) |
| **Item types** | Mix of MC, short answer, performance tasks, etc. |
| **Weight** | How much each section contributes to the total score |
| **Alignment** | Every item traces to a specific learning outcome |

Without a blueprint, assessments drift toward what's easy to test (recall) rather than what matters (application, judgment).

## Validity

Validity is NOT a property of the test — it's a property of the INTERPRETATION of scores for a specific purpose.

| Type | Question | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Content validity** | Does the test cover the domain adequately? | Blueprint alignment, expert review |
| **Criterion validity** | Do scores predict real-world performance? | Correlation with job performance, supervisor ratings |
| **Construct validity** | Does the test measure what it claims to? | Factor analysis, convergent/discriminant evidence |
| **Consequential validity** | Are the consequences of score use fair and appropriate? | Impact analysis, adverse impact review |

### The validity argument approach (Messick, Kane)
Build a chain of evidence: define the claim ("this score means the person is competent") → identify the assumptions → collect evidence for each assumption → address threats.

## Reliability

Consistency of scores across occasions, raters, and items:

| Type | What it measures | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal consistency** | Do items measure the same construct? | Cronbach's alpha, KR-20 |
| **Inter-rater reliability** | Do different raters agree? | Cohen's kappa, ICC |
| **Test-retest** | Do scores remain stable over time? | Correlation between two administrations |

## Item Analysis

After administration, analyze each item:

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **Difficulty (p-value)** | Proportion who got it right. Too easy (>0.9) or too hard (<0.2) = limited discrimination |
| **Discrimination** | Does the item distinguish high and low performers? Positive = good. Zero or negative = problem item |
| **Distractor analysis** | Are wrong options attracting responses? Non-functional distractors = poorly written item |

## Key Principles

- **Blueprint first** — before writing ANY items, build the blueprint. It prevents the common failure of testing what's easy rather than what matters.
- **Validity is an argument, not a number** — you build a case for validity with multiple types of evidence. No single statistic "proves" validity.
- **Item analysis is quality control** — after every administration, analyze items and revise or replace weak ones.
- **Fairness is non-negotiable** — check for differential item functioning (DIF) across demographic groups. Items that function differently for different groups may be biased.
