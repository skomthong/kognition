# Learner Personas

## Summary

Learner personas are composite profiles representing distinct learner segments — adapted from UX personas for learning design. They synthesize learner analysis data into actionable characters that guide design decisions and stakeholder communication.

## What a Learner Persona Should Contain

| Field | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Name and photo** | Makes the persona memorable and human | "Maria, 34, Regional Sales Manager" |
| **Role and context** | Workplace situation, responsibilities | Manages 8 reps across 3 states; travels 60% |
| **Goals (JTBD)** | What job they're hiring the learning for | "I need my team to close more enterprise deals" |
| **Prior knowledge** | What they already know vs. gaps | 10 years in sales; no formal negotiation training |
| **Constraints** | What limits their learning | Time-scarce, mobile-only during travel, skeptical of "training" |
| **Motivation** | Why they would or wouldn't engage | Wants to be promoted to VP; sees training as box-checking |
| **Technology access** | Devices, connectivity, digital literacy | iPhone, spotty hotel wifi, comfortable with apps |
| **Quote** | A representative voice statement | "If I can't use it in my next client meeting, it's wasted time" |

## Key Principles

- **Build from data, not imagination**: personas should synthesize real learner analysis (interviews, surveys, analytics, CTA), not be fictional archetypes invented in a conference room
- **2-4 personas is enough**: too many defeats the purpose. Capture the most important variation that affects design decisions.
- **Focus on differences that matter for design**: two personas who differ only in age but have the same goals, constraints, and prior knowledge are really one persona
- **Personas are decision tools**: use them to test design choices — "Would Maria engage with this? Would she have time? Does she already know this?"
- **Personas are not stereotypes**: avoid reducing complex humans to demographic clichés. Capture behavioral patterns and decision contexts.
- **Pair with JTBD**: the persona describes who; the job-to-be-done describes why

## How To Practice This Skill

1. Interview 5-8 learners from a target population. Focus on goals, constraints, prior knowledge, and learning context.
2. Cluster patterns: which characteristics co-occur? What are the meaningful segments?
3. Write 2-3 personas. Test them: do colleagues recognize these people? Can they make design decisions based on the personas?
4. Use personas in a design review: "How would Persona A experience this module? What about Persona B?"
