# Choice Architecture and Nudges

## Summary

Choice architecture is the design of decision environments to influence behavior without forbidding options. A "nudge" is a specific intervention that steers choices while preserving freedom. Thaler, Sunstein, and Balz identify key tools: defaults, expecting error, understanding mappings, giving feedback, structuring complex choices, and creating incentives. Meta-analytic evidence shows small-to-medium effects (d = 0.45) across a large evidence base.

## The Science

- Based on: [Dual-Process Models](../foundations/dual-process-models.md) — nudges exploit System 1 heuristics (defaults, salience, framing) and support System 2 when deliberation is needed
- Related: [Behavior Change](../principles/behavior-change.md) — nudges are a primary intervention function; [Habit Formation](../principles/habit-formation.md) — defaults and friction reduction create stable cues for behavior

Key frameworks:
- **MINDSPACE** (UK Cabinet Office): Messenger, Incentives, Norms, Defaults, Salience, Priming, Affect, Commitments, Ego.
- **EAST** (Behavioural Insights Team): Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely.
- **Fogg Behavior Model**: Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt at the same moment.

## How To Apply It

**Nudge tactics for learning designers and facilitators:**
- **Defaults**: Set beneficial choices as the default (recommended weekly plan, pre-filled reminders) with easy opt-out.
- **Friction reduction**: Make the next learning action easy — fewer clicks, simpler interfaces, pre-loaded content (EAST "Easy").
- **Timely prompts**: Deliver prompts at the moment of choice — before a study session lapses, at enrollment decision points, at the start of a workflow (EAST "Timely").
- **Social norms**: Show peer behavior ("78% of your cohort completed this module") carefully — monitor for inequity and stigma (EAST "Social").
- **Salience**: Make important information visually prominent — progress bars, deadline callouts, consequence previews.
- **Commitment devices**: Ask learners to publicly or privately commit to a goal (MINDSPACE "Commitments").

**Adult learning examples:**
- **Enrollment and completion**: Set default pathways in the LMS, use pre-filled reminders, show social norms. Measure with A/B tests.
- **Safety compliance**: Pre-filled near-miss reporting forms, minimal required fields, friction reduced. Attach to stable context cue for habit formation.
- **Study routines**: Use COM-B diagnosis — is the barrier capability (strategy), opportunity (time/tools), or motivation (value/confidence)?

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:**
- The environment strongly shapes behavior and small changes can produce measurable effects
- You want to support learner persistence, completion, or healthy study habits
- Interventions are transparent and aligned with learners' own goals

**Use with caution when:**
- Nudges are invisible or non-disclosed — Bovens argues that invisibility in nudging can be ethically objectionable
- Nudges are context-fragile — the same intervention may not generalize across populations
- The behavior is complex and requires capability building, not just environmental prompts
