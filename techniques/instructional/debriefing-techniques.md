# Debriefing Techniques

## Summary

Debriefing is the structured reflection process AFTER a simulation, scenario, or experience that turns activity into learning. Without debrief, simulations are expensive entertainment. Fanning & Gaba identify debriefing as the central mechanism in simulation-based learning — where the actual learning consolidation occurs.

## Core Models

### Three-phase debrief (Rudolph et al. — "Debriefing with Good Judgment")
1. **Reactions**: "What happened? What are you thinking/feeling?" — ventilate emotion, establish shared understanding
2. **Analysis**: "What was your reasoning? What cues did you use? What alternatives existed?" — surface mental models, identify gaps
3. **Summary/Application**: "What will you do differently? What principle does this illustrate?" — extract transferable learning

### After-Action Review (AAR — US Army origin)
Four questions:
1. What was supposed to happen?
2. What actually happened?
3. Why was there a difference?
4. What will we do differently next time?

### PEARLS (Promoting Excellence and Reflective Learning in Simulation)
- **P**atient/case review — establish facts
- **E**xploration of frames — surface reasoning
- **A**nalysis of performance — identify gaps
- **R**eview of teaching points — connect to principles
- **L**earner self-assessment — metacognitive calibration
- **S**ummary — consolidate takeaways

## Key Principles

- **The debrief IS the learning** — the simulation provides the experience; the debrief provides the meaning. Budget at least as much time for debrief as for the simulation itself.
- **Psychological safety is prerequisite** — if people fear judgment, they'll perform defensively in debrief and learning stops. Establish norms: "We assume everyone did the best they could with what they knew."
- **Surface reasoning, not just actions** — "What did you DO?" matters less than "What were you THINKING? What cues did you notice? What did you assume?"
- **Connect to principles** — debrief should link specific experience to transferable concepts: "This is an example of anchoring bias" or "This is what cognitive load looks like under pressure."
- **Facilitator skill matters enormously** — debrief quality depends on the facilitator's ability to ask good questions, manage group dynamics, and resist lecturing.

## How To Practice

1. After any group activity, run a 5-minute AAR. Get comfortable with the four questions.
2. Write 5 debrief questions for a scenario from this knowledge base. Sequence them: reactions → analysis → application.
3. Practice the hardest facilitator skill: ask a question and WAIT. Count to 10 silently. The discomfort produces depth.
