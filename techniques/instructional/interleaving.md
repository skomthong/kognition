# Interleaving

## Summary

Interleaving is the practice of mixing different but related topics, problem types, or skills during practice rather than practicing each type in isolation (blocking). Meta-analyses show a moderate overall interleaving effect, with benefits dependent on similarity conditions and task type.

## The Science

- Based on: [Desirable Difficulties](../foundations/desirable-difficulties.md) — interleaving feels harder than blocking but improves long-term discrimination and transfer
- Related: [Retention](../foundations/retention.md) — interleaving strengthens retrieval by forcing learners to distinguish between similar cases; [Cognitive Load](../foundations/cognitive-load.md) — can overload novices without scaffolding

## How To Apply It

- **Mixed case sets**: alternate between different scenario types in a single practice session (e.g., different diagnostic categories, different negotiation styles, different financial instruments)
- **Mixed problem sets**: interleave problem types in homework or quizzes rather than grouping by chapter
- **Varied practice drills**: rotate between related skills within a training session
- **Pair with spacing**: interleaving within sessions, spacing between sessions, produces the strongest retention effects

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** learners need to discriminate between similar categories, select appropriate strategies, or transfer to novel cases. Especially effective for classification tasks. **Limitations:** can overload novices who don't yet have schemas for individual categories — build basic competence first, then interleave. Benefits are moderated by similarity between interleaved items.

## Sources

- Brunmair, M., & Richter, T. (2019). Similarity matters: A meta-analysis of interleaved learning. *Psychological Bulletin*, 145(11), 1029-1052.
- Rohrer, D. (2012). Interleaving helps students distinguish among similar concepts. *Educational Psychology Review*, 24(3), 355-367.
