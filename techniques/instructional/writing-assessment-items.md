# Writing Assessment Items

## Summary

Assessment items are the evidence mechanism for learning objectives. Item quality determines whether you're measuring what you intend (validity) and whether results are consistent (reliability). Writing good items is a distinct craft skill from writing good content.

## Item Types and When to Use Each

| Type | Best for | Bloom level | Pitfalls |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Multiple choice** | Factual recall, concept discrimination, diagnostic checks | Remember, Understand, Apply | Trivial distractors, "all of the above," cueing the answer |
| **Short answer / fill-in** | Recall without recognition cues; stronger retrieval practice | Remember, Understand | Scoring ambiguity; accept equivalent answers |
| **Matching** | Association, classification, terminology | Remember, Understand | Unequal list lengths, obvious pairings |
| **Scenario/case-based** | Application, analysis, judgment under constraints | Apply, Analyze, Evaluate | Extraneous complexity; unclear what's being assessed |
| **Performance task** | Authentic demonstration of skill | Apply through Create | Scoring consistency; rubric needed |
| **Portfolio** | Evidence of growth, reflection, capability over time | All levels | Assessment workload; inconsistent artifacts |

## Multiple Choice Best Practices

- **Stem**: clear, complete question or statement. Learner should be able to answer before seeing options.
- **Correct answer**: unambiguously correct, not "most correct"
- **Distractors**: plausible wrong answers based on common misconceptions — not obviously wrong
- **Avoid**: negatively worded stems ("which is NOT"), "all of the above," "none of the above," overlapping options, grammatical cues that reveal the answer
- **Align to objective**: if the objective says "apply," the item should present a novel situation requiring application, not a definition recall

## Rubric Design

- **Analytic rubric**: separate scores for each criterion — more diagnostic, more reliable, more time to score
- **Holistic rubric**: single overall score — faster, but less diagnostic
- **Single-point rubric**: describes proficiency only; space for notes on strengths and areas for growth — good for formative use

Rubric design principles:
- Criteria derived from the learning objective (what matters?)
- Performance levels describe observable differences (not just "excellent/good/poor")
- Calibrate with examples: norming sessions with multiple raters improve inter-rater reliability
- Pilot and revise: test items and rubrics with learners before high-stakes use

## Common Mistakes

- Assessing at the wrong Bloom level (objective says "evaluate," item asks to "recall")
- Trick questions that test reading comprehension, not domain knowledge
- Items that can be answered correctly without learning (too many context clues)
- Rubric criteria that are too vague to differentiate performance levels
- Not piloting items before use

## How To Practice This Skill

1. Write the objective first, then the assessment item, then check alignment
2. Have a colleague take the assessment blind — do they interpret items as intended?
3. After administration, do item analysis: which items were too easy, too hard, or didn't discriminate between high and low performers?
4. Build an item bank organized by objective and Bloom level
