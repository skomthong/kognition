# Interviewing for Insight

## Summary

Interviews are the primary tool for understanding what learners need, what experts know, and what the real performance context looks like. But most interviews produce surface-level responses because the interviewer asks surface-level questions. Skilled interviewing extracts the tacit knowledge, decision patterns, and contextual understanding that surveys and documents can't reach.

## Interview Types for Learning Design

| Type | Purpose | When |
|---|---|---|
| **Learner interview** | Understand goals, constraints, prior knowledge, motivation, pain points | Learner analysis, persona development, JTBD research |
| **SME interview** | Extract domain knowledge, decision logic, common errors | Content development, CTA, case writing |
| **Stakeholder interview** | Understand business goals, constraints, success criteria | Needs assessment, program scoping |
| **Critical incident interview** | Reconstruct specific decision moments in detail | CTA, scenario development, case writing |
| **Post-program interview** | Understand transfer, application barriers, impact | Evaluation, transfer assessment |

## Questioning Techniques

### Open → Probe → Confirm
1. **Open**: "Tell me about the last time you had to handle a difficult client conversation."
2. **Probe**: "What did you notice first? What options did you consider? What made you choose that approach?"
3. **Confirm**: "So if I understand correctly, the key decision point was when you noticed the client's tone shift — and you chose to acknowledge rather than redirect. Is that right?"

### Critical Incident Method
- "Think of a specific time when this went WELL. Walk me through it step by step."
- "Now think of a time it went POORLY. What was different?"
- "At what point did you realize something was wrong? What cues did you notice?"

The contrast between success and failure reveals the decision patterns and cues that distinguish expertise from novice performance — this is [CTA](cognitive-task-analysis.md) in practice.

### Eliciting Tacit Knowledge
Experts often can't articulate what they know — their knowledge is automated ([System 1](../../foundations/dual-process-models.md)). Techniques:
- **"Walk me through it"** — step by step forces articulation of automated sequences
- **"What would a novice miss?"** — the contrast surfaces what experts do unconsciously
- **"If you couldn't do X, what would you do instead?"** — constraints reveal priorities
- **"What's the dumbest mistake you could make here?"** — error knowledge reveals expert mental models
- **Silence** — after an answer, wait 5-10 seconds. The interviewee often fills the silence with deeper reflection.

## Common Mistakes

- **Leading questions** — "Don't you think spacing is important?" (suggests the answer)
- **Closed questions too early** — "Was the training effective?" (yes/no kills elaboration)
- **Accepting generalizations** — "It usually goes fine" → probe: "Tell me about a SPECIFIC time"
- **Talking too much** — the interviewer should speak 20-30% of the time, not 50%+
- **Not recording** — memory is unreliable. Record (with permission) and take notes.
- **No follow-up on surprises** — when something unexpected comes up, FOLLOW IT. The surprises are often the most valuable data.

## How To Practice

1. Interview a colleague about a decision they made recently using the Open → Probe → Confirm pattern. Record it. Listen back — where did you miss an opportunity to probe deeper?
2. Practice the Critical Incident Method: "Tell me about a time when X went wrong." Push for specifics — time, place, cues, decisions, outcomes.
3. After an interview, write up the 3 most important insights. Share them with the interviewee — did you capture what they meant?
