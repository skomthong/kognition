# Competency-Based Training (CBT/CBE)

## Definition

Competency-based training (CBT) — or competency-based education (CBE) in academic contexts — organizes learning around the demonstration of defined competencies rather than time spent in instruction. Learners advance by proving mastery of specific knowledge, skills, and abilities, not by completing seat-time requirements.

## How It Works

Three structural pillars:
1. **Competency framework**: a structured set of competencies (knowledge, skills, abilities, behaviors) required for a role, function, or credential — each with defined performance criteria and evidence requirements
2. **Flexible pacing**: learners progress at their own speed — those with prior knowledge accelerate; those who need more time get it
3. **Mastery demonstration**: assessment is the gating mechanism — learners must demonstrate competency to acceptable standards before advancing

## Key Principles

- Competencies must be observable, measurable, and aligned to real performance requirements — not abstract knowledge statements.
- Assessment design is the backbone: each competency needs valid, reliable evidence (performance tasks, simulations, portfolios, tests). Weak assessment invalidates the entire model.
- Prior learning assessment (PLA/RPL) allows experienced adults to demonstrate existing competencies and skip redundant instruction.
- Stackable credentials: competencies can be grouped into micro-credentials, certificates, and degrees — each independently meaningful.
- The facilitator role shifts from content deliverer to coach, assessor, and support provider.

## Relationship to Competency Models

A **competency model** defines *what* competencies matter for a role or profession. Examples:
- **IBSTPI** competencies for instructional designers
- **ATD Talent Development Capability Model** for L&D professionals
- **DigComp 2.2** for digital competence
- Organization-specific competency models tied to job families

CBT uses competency models as its design anchor — the model defines the target; CBT designs the path to get there.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** clear performance standards exist, learner populations have varied prior knowledge, credentials must signal specific capabilities, and the organization can support flexible pacing and robust assessment.

**Limitations:** requires high-quality assessment infrastructure (validity, reliability, fairness). Can feel reductive if competencies are overly narrow. Faculty/facilitator role change is significant and requires support. Accreditation constraints may limit flexibility.
