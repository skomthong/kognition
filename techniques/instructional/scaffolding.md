# Scaffolding

## Definition

Scaffolding (Wood, Bruner, & Ross, 1976) is expert support that enables learners to perform beyond their current independent capability. The support is adjusted — added when needed, removed (faded) as competence grows. The concept aligns with Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): the gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with assistance.

## How To Apply It

- Start with high support: worked examples, guided practice, checklists, decision aids, coaching
- Fade systematically: remove steps, hints, and prompts as learners demonstrate competence
- Monitor and adjust: if learners struggle after fading, re-scaffold temporarily
- Types: procedural scaffolds (step-by-step guides), conceptual scaffolds (concept maps, models), metacognitive scaffolds (planning prompts, self-check questions)

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** learners face tasks beyond their current ability but within reach with support. **Limitations:** Poor fading leads to dependency. Over-scaffolding for experts creates redundancy and boredom (expertise reversal in CLT). Requires skilled facilitation to judge when to fade.
