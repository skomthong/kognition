# Deliberate Practice

## Definition

Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993) propose deliberate practice as structured, effortful activities specifically designed to improve performance — characterized by clear goals, focused effort at the edge of current ability, immediate feedback, and repetition with refinement. Expertise develops through thousands of hours of deliberate practice, not just experience or exposure.

## How To Apply It

- Define target competencies and observable performances with conditions and criteria.
- Design repeated practice cycles with adjustable difficulty and clear feedback.
- Coach and debrief — practice without feedback is just repetition, not deliberate practice.
- Build increasing challenge: as schemas automate, reduce scaffolding and increase complexity (expertise reversal).
- Use simulations for safe repetition of high-stakes performance.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** outcomes are skill and judgment-based (leadership, clinical reasoning, sales, negotiation, safety). **Limitations:** time cost, need for feedback quality, organizational ability to provide practice contexts. Do not label repetition without feedback and goals as "deliberate practice."

## Sources

- Ericsson, K. A., Krampe, R. T., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993). The role of deliberate practice in the acquisition of expert performance. *Psychological Review*, 100(3), 363-406.
