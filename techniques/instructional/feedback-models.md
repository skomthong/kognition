# Feedback Models

## Definition

Hattie & Timperley (2007) propose a model where effective feedback answers three questions: Where am I going? (goals), How am I going? (progress), Where to next? (improvement actions). Feedback operates at four levels: task, process, self-regulation, and self (praise/blame).

## How It Works

| Level | Focus | Example | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Task** | Correct/incorrect, surface features | "Step 3 is wrong — the formula is X" | Effective for novices; limited transfer |
| **Process** | Strategies and approaches | "Your diagnostic approach missed the differential — try systematically ruling out alternatives" | More effective for developing skill |
| **Self-regulation** | Monitoring and planning | "How did you decide where to start? What would you check first next time?" | Builds independence; most effective for transfer |
| **Self** | Personal praise/blame | "You're smart" / "You're not trying" | Least effective for learning; can harm |

## Key Principles

- Move feedback UP the levels as learners progress: task feedback for novices, process and self-regulation feedback as competence grows.
- Timing: immediate feedback works best for procedural accuracy; delayed feedback can be better for conceptual learning (it creates desirable difficulty).
- Feedback must be actionable — "good job" contains no learning information.
- In digital environments: automated task-level feedback is easy; process and self-regulation feedback requires human judgment or well-designed prompts.
