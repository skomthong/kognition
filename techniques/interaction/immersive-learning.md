# Immersive Learning (VR/AR)

## Definition

Immersive learning uses virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) to create learning experiences where learners interact with simulated or enhanced environments. VR replaces the real environment with a digital one; AR overlays digital information onto the real environment.

## When VR/AR Adds Learning Value

Use VR/AR when the learning objective meets at least one criterion:
- **Dangerous or expensive to practice in reality** — surgery, hazardous materials, emergency response, high-altitude work
- **Spatial/procedural learning** — assembly, navigation, equipment operation where physical interaction matters
- **Perspective-taking** — empathy training, experiencing situations from another's viewpoint
- **Rare events** — crisis scenarios, equipment failures that can't be reliably reproduced

Do NOT use VR/AR for:
- Content that can be equally learned through video, text, or discussion — "wow factor" is not a learning outcome
- Audiences with significant accessibility barriers (motion sensitivity, visual impairment, hardware cost)

## Key Principles

- **Presence** (the feeling of "being there") enhances engagement but does not automatically produce learning — presence must be coupled with learning design (objectives, practice, feedback, debrief).
- **Embodied cognition** — physical interaction and spatial navigation can deepen procedural and spatial learning beyond what screen-based media achieves.
- **Fidelity should match objectives** — high visual fidelity is expensive and not always necessary. Functional fidelity (realistic decisions and consequences) matters more than photorealism.
- **Accessibility**: provide non-VR alternatives for equitable access. Some learners experience motion sickness, visual discomfort, or cannot use headsets. Every VR experience must have an equivalent alternative pathway.
- **Debrief is essential** — same as any simulation. VR without structured debrief is expensive entertainment.
