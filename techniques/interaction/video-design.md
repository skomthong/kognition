# Video Design for Learning

## Definition

Video design for learning applies multimedia learning principles (Mayer) and cognitive load theory to the specific medium of instructional video — addressing length, pacing, visual design, instructor presence, interactivity, and segmentation.

## How To Apply It

| Principle | What to do | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Segmenting** | Break into short segments (3-7 min) with pauses | Reduces per-segment load; supports learner-paced processing |
| **Signaling** | Use visual cues, highlights, on-screen annotations | Directs attention to essential elements |
| **Weeding** | Remove seductive details, tangents, filler | Reduces extraneous load |
| **Modality** | Narrate visuals rather than displaying text over them | Uses dual channels efficiently; avoids redundancy |
| **Instructor presence** | Talking head can increase social presence and engagement | But can split attention if competing with complex visuals |
| **Pacing** | Learner-controlled > system-paced for complex material | Supports self-regulation and reduces overload |
| **Interactivity** | Embed retrieval questions, pause prompts, branching | Transforms passive viewing into active processing |

## Key Principles

- Optimal length: research and practice converge around 6 minutes as a drop-off point for engagement. Shorter is generally better, but length should match cognitive demand, not arbitrary targets.
- Pair video with retrieval practice: embed questions within or after video segments. Video without retrieval is passive consumption.
- Accessibility: captions, transcripts, audio descriptions, keyboard-navigable player controls (WCAG).
- Production value matters less than instructional design — a well-structured screencast beats a poorly designed cinematic production.
