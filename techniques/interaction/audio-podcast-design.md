# Audio and Podcast Design for Learning

## Summary

Audio is underused in learning design despite being the only medium that works while the learner's eyes are busy — commuting, exercising, doing manual work. It leverages the verbal channel without requiring visual attention, but it also means the learner has NO visual support, which affects cognitive load and content suitability.

## When Audio Works

- **Narrative content**: stories, interviews, case discussions — audio's intimacy creates connection
- **Commute/exercise contexts**: learners can't use screens but can listen
- **Conversational explanation**: complex ideas explained through dialogue (interview format, panel)
- **Reflection prompts**: guided reflection during downtime
- **Supplementary content**: audio as companion to visual materials already studied

## When Audio Doesn't Work

- **Complex visual content**: processes, diagrams, data — without visual support, the learner can't hold spatial or structural information
- **Procedural instructions**: "Click the third button from the left" doesn't work in audio
- **Dense factual content**: tables, lists, comparisons — better as text or visual
- **Primary instruction for complex topics**: audio-only imposes high load for novel, complex material without visual support

## Design Principles

- **Conversational tone** — [Mayer's personalization principle](../../principles/multimedia-learning.md) applies even more strongly to audio. Formal narration at a distance; conversational narration draws in.
- **Verbal signaling** — structure must be communicated through words: "There are three reasons. First... Second... Third..." Listeners can't see headings.
- **Chunking by time** — 15-25 minute episodes for sustained listening; 3-5 minutes for micro-episodes
- **Pacing and silence** — deliberate pauses give processing time. Don't fill every second.
- **Interview format** — two voices create variety and natural structure (question-answer-follow-up)

## How To Practice

1. Write a 3-minute audio script explaining a concept from this knowledge base. Read it aloud. Does it sound natural? Is the structure clear without visual support?
2. Record yourself and listen back. Where do you lose the thread? That's where verbal signaling is missing.
