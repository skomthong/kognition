# Storyboarding for Learning

## Summary

A storyboard is a visual plan for a learning experience — mapping screen-by-screen (for e-learning/video) or activity-by-activity (for facilitated learning) what the learner sees, hears, does, and how the system responds. It's the bridge between instructional design and production.

## Storyboard Formats

| Format | Best for | Fidelity |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper/whiteboard sketch** | Early ideation, team brainstorming, rapid iteration | Low — disposable and fast |
| **Slide-based storyboard** | E-learning modules, client review | Medium — shows layout, text, and interaction logic |
| **Script-based storyboard** | Video, narrated content | Medium — two columns: audio script + visual description |
| **Interactive prototype** | Complex branching, simulations, usability testing | High — clickable mockup in Storyline, H5P, Figma |

## What a Storyboard Should Contain

For each screen/activity:
- **Screen number / activity label**
- **Visual description**: what the learner sees (layout, images, diagrams, video)
- **Text/narration**: what is read or heard (apply multimedia learning principles — avoid duplicating narration as on-screen text)
- **Learner action**: what the learner does (click, drag, type, discuss, practice)
- **Feedback/response**: what happens after the learner acts (correct/incorrect feedback, consequence, next step)
- **Navigation**: where the learner goes next (linear, branching, optional)
- **Notes for production**: animations, timing, accessibility requirements, technical specs

## Key Principles

- **Storyboard AFTER objectives and assessment are defined** (backward design) — the storyboard implements the design, it doesn't replace it
- **Apply CTML principles during storyboarding**: signaling, segmenting, coherence, spatial contiguity — catch multimedia errors before production, not after
- **Storyboards are for communication**: stakeholders, SMEs, developers, and media producers all need to understand the plan. Clarity > beauty.
- **Iterate cheaply**: it's 100x cheaper to revise a paper storyboard than a produced video. Prototype and test early (SAM, design thinking).
- **Include interaction, not just content**: if a storyboard is all content slides with no learner action, the design is passive

## How To Practice This Skill

1. Pick a 5-minute segment of an existing course and reverse-engineer a storyboard from it
2. Storyboard a 3-screen interaction: context → decision → feedback. Show it to someone and ask if they understand what the learner does.
3. Practice the two-column video script format: left column = audio narration, right column = visual description
4. Build a clickable prototype of a branching scenario (Twine is free and fast for this)
