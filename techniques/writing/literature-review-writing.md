# Literature Review Writing

## Summary

A literature review synthesizes existing research to establish what is known, what is debated, and what gaps remain — positioning your own work within that landscape. It is NOT a source-by-source summary ("A said X, B said Y, C said Z"). It is an ARGUMENT about the state of knowledge.

## Structure Options

### Thematic
Organize by themes or concepts, not by source. Each section makes a point supported by multiple sources:
- "Three mechanisms explain why spacing improves retention: [sources synthesized]"

### Chronological
Organize by how the field developed over time. Use when the historical progression IS the argument.

### Methodological
Organize by research approach. Use when comparing how different methods produce different conclusions.

### Theoretical
Organize by competing theoretical perspectives. Use when the debate between theories IS the point.

**Best practice for most reviews: thematic.** It forces synthesis rather than summary.

## Key Moves

| Move | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Synthesize** | Combine multiple sources into a single insight | "Across these three meta-analyses, the pattern is consistent: spacing produces robust retention effects regardless of content type" |
| **Identify convergence** | Show where sources agree | "Both CLT and CTML research converge on the finding that..." |
| **Identify tension** | Show where sources disagree | "However, the evidence for interleaving is more moderated — Brunmair & Richter (2019) find that..." |
| **Identify gaps** | Show what hasn't been studied | "What remains underexplored is how spacing schedules interact with expertise level in workplace settings" |
| **Position your work** | Explain how your research addresses the gap | "This study addresses that gap by..." |

## Common Mistakes

- **Source-by-source summary** — listing what each author says without connecting them. This is a annotated bibliography, not a review.
- **Uncritical acceptance** — presenting all sources as equally valid without evaluating methods, sample sizes, or limitations
- **No argument** — the review describes the field but doesn't take a position on what it means
- **Missing gap statement** — the review covers existing knowledge but doesn't identify what's MISSING (which justifies your own work)

## How To Practice

1. Take 5 sources on a topic from this knowledge base. Write a synthesis paragraph that combines them into a single insight — no source should get its own sentence.
2. Identify one tension between two sources and write a paragraph that presents both sides, then takes a position.
3. End with a gap statement: "What remains underexplored is..."
