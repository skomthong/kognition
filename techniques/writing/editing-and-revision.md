# Editing and Revision

## Summary

Writing is rewriting. First drafts are for getting ideas down; revision is where clarity, precision, and persuasion emerge. Editing has distinct stages — each with a different focus and a different kind of attention. Skipping stages or conflating them produces mediocre results.

## The Four Stages of Editing

| Stage | Focus | Key question | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Developmental editing** | Structure, argument, completeness, organization | "Does the piece achieve its purpose? Is the argument sound? Is anything missing or misplaced?" | After first draft |
| **Line editing** | Sentence quality, flow, voice, precision | "Does each sentence say what it means clearly and economically?" | After structure is solid |
| **Copy editing** | Grammar, punctuation, consistency, style guide compliance | "Is it correct? Is it consistent?" | After content is final |
| **Proofreading** | Typos, formatting, broken links, layout errors | "Is it clean?" | Last pass before publication |

**Critical rule**: do NOT proofread before you've finished developmental editing. You'll waste time perfecting sentences that get cut.

## Revision Moves

### Cutting redundancy
- Apply the "does this sentence add value?" test to every sentence
- Apply the 30% cut exercise: most drafts survive losing 30% without losing meaning
- Watch for: throat-clearing openings ("It is important to note that..."), duplicated ideas across paragraphs, examples that make the same point twice

### Strengthening topic sentences
- Read only the topic sentences. Do they form a coherent argument? If not, the structure has problems.
- Each topic sentence should make a claim, not just introduce a topic.

### Tightening arguments
- Check every claim: is it supported? Is the reasoning explicit?
- Check every evidence citation: does it include analysis (so what?)?
- Check qualifications: are you overclaiming or underclaiming?

### Improving flow
- Check transitions between paragraphs
- Check parallel structure in lists and series
- Read aloud: where you stumble is where the writing needs work

## The Editing Mindset

- **Separate writing from editing** — write first, edit later. The inner editor kills the inner writer if active during drafting.
- **Time distance** — edit at least a few hours (ideally a day) after writing. Fresh eyes catch what immersion blinds you to.
- **Read aloud** — your ear catches what your eye skips
- **Print it** — changing the medium (screen → paper) reveals errors
- **Ask: "Is this the best I can make it, or just the first thing that came to mind?"**

## How To Practice

1. Edit someone else's writing — it's easier to see problems in others' work. Then apply the same lens to your own.
2. Take a piece you wrote a month ago and do a developmental edit. What would you restructure?
3. Track what you cut most during editing — that's your default writing habit to work on.
