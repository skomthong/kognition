# Cohesion and Flow

## Summary

Cohesion is the connective tissue of writing — the techniques that link sentences, paragraphs, and sections so the reader experiences smooth progression rather than a series of disconnected statements. Flow is the reader's experience of that cohesion: when writing flows, the reader moves forward effortlessly; when it doesn't, they stumble and reread.

## Cohesion Techniques

### Transitions
Words and phrases that signal the relationship between ideas:

| Relationship | Transition words |
|---|---|
| **Addition** | also, furthermore, moreover, in addition |
| **Contrast** | however, nevertheless, on the other hand, yet, but |
| **Cause/effect** | therefore, consequently, as a result, because, since |
| **Sequence** | first, second, then, next, finally, subsequently |
| **Example** | for instance, for example, specifically, such as |
| **Summary** | in short, to summarize, overall, in other words |
| **Concession** | although, while, admittedly, granted |

**Caution**: transitions are signals, not substitutes for logical connection. "However" between two unrelated sentences creates false cohesion.

### Referencing back
- **Repeated key terms**: use the same word for the same concept (don't alternate synonyms for "variety")
- **Pronouns**: "this approach," "these findings," "such conditions" — connect to what came before
- **Echo phrases**: "The spacing effect described above..." links back to earlier content
- **Callbacks**: returning to an earlier example, metaphor, or question creates a sense of unity

### Thematic consistency
- Each paragraph should connect to the section's theme. If a paragraph doesn't, it belongs somewhere else or doesn't belong at all.
- Headings create a promise — the content must deliver on that promise.
- Consistent terminology throughout — don't call it "retrieval practice" in one paragraph and "testing effect" in another without explaining they're the same thing.

### Parallel structure
Express similar ideas in similar grammatical form:
- *Not parallel:* "The program improved retention, was helpful for engagement, and they also transferred better."
- *Parallel:* "The program improved retention, increased engagement, and supported transfer."

Parallelism creates rhythm, reduces processing effort, and signals that items are at the same logical level.

### Signposting
Explicit statements that tell the reader where they are and where they're going:
- "This section covers three barriers to transfer."
- "Having examined the evidence, we now turn to practical implications."
- "The key point is..."

Signposting is the written equivalent of Gagné's "inform the learner of objectives" — it creates expectancy and schema activation.

## How To Practice

1. Read a paragraph you've written and circle every transition. If there are none, the paragraph probably feels choppy. Add appropriate connections.
2. Check for consistent terminology — do a find-and-replace to ensure you use the same term for the same concept.
3. Read only the first sentence of each paragraph in a section. Do they tell a connected story?
