# Voice and Personality

## Summary

Voice is the distinctive personality that comes through in writing — the cumulative effect of word choice, sentence rhythm, what you include, what you leave out, and how you relate to the reader. Every writer has a default voice; skilled writers develop it deliberately and can modulate it across contexts while remaining recognizably themselves.

## What Creates Voice

| Element | How it shapes voice |
|---|---|
| **Word choice** | Anglo-Saxon vs. Latinate; simple vs. complex; concrete vs. abstract |
| **Sentence length** | Short and punchy vs. flowing and complex |
| **What you include** | Details, examples, caveats, humor, personal references |
| **What you omit** | What's left unsaid carries as much voice as what's said (Hemingway's iceberg) |
| **Relationship to reader** | Peer, teacher, guide, provocateur, companion |
| **Humor and irony** | Present, absent, dry, warm, dark |
| **Confidence level** | Assertive, tentative, measured, bold |

## Voice Models for Learning Designers

| Voice | Characteristics | Good for |
|---|---|---|
| **The clear guide** | Direct, warm, organized, no wasted words | Instructional text, facilitator guides |
| **The thoughtful analyst** | Evidence-based, measured, acknowledges complexity | Research summaries, evaluation reports |
| **The provocateur** | Challenges assumptions, uses questions, contrarian | Opening hooks, debate prompts, critical pedagogy |
| **The storyteller** | Narrative, vivid, character-driven, emotional arc | Scenarios, cases, keynote writing |
| **The minimalist** | Stripped down, every word earns its place, powerful silence | Slide text, key messages, executive summaries |

## Simplicity as a Voice Choice

Vonnegut's 8 rules for creative writing include: "Do not ramble," "Keep it simple," "Have the guts to cut." Hemingway's iceberg theory: the dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water.

Simplicity is not dumbing down — it's the result of deep understanding expressed without pretension. Writing that sounds simple usually took more work than writing that sounds complex.

## How To Practice

1. Write the same paragraph in 3 different voices: clear guide, provocateur, minimalist. Notice which feels natural.
2. Identify a writer whose voice you admire. Analyze: what specific choices create that voice?
3. Write for 10 minutes without editing. Your unedited voice is your default — notice its patterns, then decide which to keep and which to develop.
