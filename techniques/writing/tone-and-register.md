# Tone and Register

## Summary

Tone is the writer's attitude toward the subject and reader (authoritative, empathetic, urgent, casual). Register is the level of formality appropriate to the context (academic, professional, conversational, casual). Skilled writers control both deliberately, shifting as context demands while maintaining consistency within a piece.

## Register Spectrum

| Register | Characteristics | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Formal/academic** | Third person, passive acceptable, precise terminology, hedged claims, citation | Research papers, policy documents, regulatory writing |
| **Professional** | Direct, clear, evidence-based, moderate formality | Reports, proposals, facilitator guides, stakeholder communications |
| **Conversational** | Second person, contractions, accessible language, personal tone | Instructional text, e-learning, coaching, learner-facing materials |
| **Casual** | First person, colloquial, humor acceptable, minimal structure | Internal team chat, social media, informal community posts |

## Discourse Norms (Know Your Arena)

| Context | Norms | Violation risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Academic** | Hedged claims, citation, objectivity stance, formal structure | Sounds unrigorous or unprofessional without hedging and sourcing |
| **Business** | Action-oriented, concise, results-framed, pyramid structure | Sounds academic if too hedged; sounds unprofessional if too casual |
| **Public/media** | Accessible, concrete, story-driven, emotional connection | Sounds cold if too formal; sounds lightweight if too casual |

## Key Principles

- **Match the audience**: a CEO and a new hire need different registers for the same information
- **Consistency within a piece**: shifting register mid-document confuses the reader (unless deliberate for effect)
- **Tone ≠ content**: you can deliver critical feedback in a supportive tone, or positive news in a measured tone. Tone is HOW you say it.
- **Understatement vs. emphasis**: "This is a significant concern" (understated) vs. "This is a crisis" (emphasis). Both have uses; choose deliberately.

## How To Practice

1. Write the same message at three registers: formal report, professional email, conversational Slack message.
2. Read a piece of your own writing and label the tone. Is it consistent? Where does it shift unintentionally?
3. Read one paragraph of academic writing and one paragraph of journalism on the same topic. List the register differences.
