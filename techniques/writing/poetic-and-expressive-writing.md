# Poetic and Expressive Writing

## Summary

Poetic writing compresses maximum meaning into minimum words through imagery, rhythm, and precise word choice. Learning designers don't need to write poetry — but the SKILLS of poetic writing (compression, imagery, voice, emotional precision) make every other form of writing better. A facilitator guide that uses vivid language is more memorable. A scenario with sensory detail is more immersive. A slide with compressed phrasing is more powerful.

## Core Skills (Light Mastery)

### Imagery — writing that creates pictures
- **Sensory details**: what can you see, hear, feel, smell, taste? "The conference room was cold and fluorescent" is more real than "the meeting environment was suboptimal."
- **Show, don't tell**: "She closed her laptop mid-presentation and left" carries more than "She was upset and disengaged."
- **Concrete over abstract**: "three missed deadlines in two weeks" not "recurring performance issues"

### Compression — saying more with less
- Every word must earn its place. Poetic writing is the extreme version of [Precision and Concision](precision-and-concision.md).
- Vonnegut: "Here is a lesson in creative writing. First rule: do not use semicolons... All they do is show you've been to college."
- Hemingway: remove every word that doesn't carry load. What's left should be stronger than what was there before.

### Voice — the writer's personality on the page
- Voice emerges from consistent choices: sentence length, word choice, rhythm, what you include and leave out
- Vonnegut's voice: simple words, short sentences, dark humor, direct address, moral clarity
- Hemingway's voice: spare, declarative, emotional weight carried by what's NOT said (the iceberg theory)

### Rhythm — the music of prose
- Prose has rhythm even when it doesn't rhyme. Varied sentence length, stressed syllables, pauses.
- Read your writing aloud. Where it flows, the rhythm works. Where you stumble, it needs fixing.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Scenario openings**: "It's 4:47 PM on a Friday. Your phone buzzes. It's the client." (Imagery + compression + tension)
- **Key messages**: "Design for forgetting. Build for retrieval. Measure at the moment of performance." (Rhythm + parallelism)
- **Slide text**: "Not coverage. Competence." (Compression + antithesis)
- **Facilitator energy**: writing with voice and rhythm keeps the facilitator engaged, which keeps the learner engaged

## How To Practice

1. Describe a workplace moment in exactly 50 words using at least 2 sensory details.
2. Take a key insight from this knowledge base and compress it to a single sentence that could go on a slide.
3. Read one page of Vonnegut (*Slaughterhouse-Five* or *Cat's Cradle*) and one page of Hemingway (*The Old Man and the Sea*). Notice what they leave out.
