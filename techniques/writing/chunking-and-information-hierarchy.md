# Chunking and Information Hierarchy

## Summary

Chunking is grouping related information into manageable units. Information hierarchy is organizing those chunks into levels of importance — headings, subheadings, body text, details — so the reader can navigate and process efficiently. Both are direct applications of [cognitive load](../../principles/cognitive-load.md) and [working memory](../../foundations/memory-systems.md) principles to written communication.

## Why It Works

- Working memory holds 3-5 chunks (Cowan). Grouping related items into chunks lets the reader process more information within capacity limits.
- Hierarchy creates a visual and conceptual scaffold — headings function as signaling (CTML) that guides attention and creates a schema for incoming information.
- Progressive disclosure — show the overview first, let the reader drill into detail as needed. Not everyone needs every detail.

## How To Apply It

### Chunking rules
- **Group by theme, not by source** — organize by what the reader needs, not by when you found the information
- **3-5 items per group** — matches working memory capacity. If a list has 12 items, chunk them into 3-4 groups of 3-4.
- **Label each chunk** — a heading or topic sentence tells the reader what the chunk is about before they read it
- **One level of nesting is fine; three is too many** — deep nesting creates its own cognitive load

### Heading hierarchy
- **H1** — the document's subject (one per document)
- **H2** — major sections (the argument's skeleton)
- **H3** — subsections within a major section
- **H4+** — usually a sign you need to restructure rather than nest deeper

### Progressive disclosure in digital contexts
- **Lead with the overview** — table of contents, summary, key takeaways
- **Expandable sections** — accordions, "read more" links for optional detail
- **Layered documents** — executive summary → main report → appendix → data tables

## Common Mistakes

- **Wall of text** — no headings, no white space, no visual breaks. Reader has no entry point.
- **Flat lists** — 15 bullet points with no grouping. Reader can't remember or navigate.
- **Decorative headings** — headings that don't describe content ("Introduction," "Discussion") vs. informative headings ("Spaced practice improves retention," "Three barriers to transfer")

## How To Practice

1. Take a long list and chunk it into 3-4 named groups.
2. Write informative headings — a reader should understand the argument from headings alone.
3. Create a progressive disclosure version of an existing document: 50-word summary → 500-word overview → full document.
