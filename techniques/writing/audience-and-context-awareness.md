# Audience and Context Awareness

## Summary

Every piece of writing has an audience (who will read it), a purpose (what it should accomplish), and a context (when, where, and under what conditions it will be read). Effective writers analyze all three before writing and adapt throughout. This is the meta-skill that governs every other writing technique — the decision about WHICH technique to use depends on audience, purpose, and context.

## Audience Analysis Questions

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Who are they?** | Role, expertise level, decision authority |
| **What do they already know?** | Determines how much background, what jargon is safe, what can be assumed |
| **What do they need from this?** | Decision support, information, instruction, persuasion, entertainment |
| **What's their emotional state?** | Anxious, skeptical, enthusiastic, indifferent, overwhelmed |
| **What are they doing while reading?** | Focused study, skimming on mobile, multitasking, preparing for a meeting |
| **What will they DO with this?** | Act on it, share it, file it, discuss it, nothing |

## Purpose Clarity

Before writing, answer: **"After reading this, the reader should ___."**
- ...understand the three principles of CLT (inform)
- ...approve the budget for the new LMS (persuade)
- ...complete the onboarding checklist (instruct)
- ...feel confident about starting the program (motivate)
- ...decide which vendor to select (support decision)

If you can't fill in that blank, you're not ready to write.

## Context Adaptation

| Context factor | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| **Time-constrained reader** | Pyramid principle, executive summary, scannable formatting |
| **Technical audience** | Precise terminology, citation, deeper analysis, fewer basic explanations |
| **Non-expert audience** | Plain language, analogies, examples before theory, glossary |
| **High-stakes decision** | Evidence-heavy, qualified claims, alternatives presented, risks acknowledged |
| **Resistant audience** | Lead with their concerns, concede valid objections, build credibility before recommendation |
| **Mobile reader** | Short paragraphs, clear headings, scannable lists, minimal scrolling per point |

## How To Practice

1. Before your next piece of writing, answer all 6 audience analysis questions. Notice how the answers change what you write.
2. Write the same update for two audiences: your team (peers) and senior leadership. What changes?
3. After writing, ask a member of the target audience to read it and tell you what they think the purpose is. If they can't, rewrite the opening.
