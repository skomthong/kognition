# Critical Thinking

## Summary

Critical thinking is the disciplined process of analyzing, evaluating, and interpreting information to form well-reasoned judgments. Strong writing depends on thinking quality — you cannot write a clear argument if your thinking is muddled, and you cannot evaluate evidence if you can't distinguish strong from weak reasoning.

## Core Operations

| Operation | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Analysis** | Breaking something into components to understand structure | "This program has three components: content delivery, practice, and assessment. Let's examine each." |
| **Evaluation** | Judging the quality, credibility, or strength of something | "This meta-analysis has a large sample but high heterogeneity — the average effect may not apply to our context." |
| **Inference** | Drawing conclusions from available evidence | "Since spacing improves retention and our program has no spacing, retention decline is predictable." |
| **Interpretation** | Making sense of data, identifying patterns and meaning | "The dropout pattern concentrates at week 3, suggesting an onboarding-to-learning transition problem." |
| **Explanation** | Articulating reasoning clearly so others can follow and evaluate it | "I recommend spaced practice because [evidence], which means [reasoning], despite [limitation]." |
| **Self-regulation** | Monitoring and correcting your own reasoning | "Am I overclaiming? What am I assuming? What would change my mind?" |

## Common Thinking Errors in Learning Design

- **Confirmation bias** — seeking evidence that supports your preferred approach while ignoring contradictory evidence
- **Authority bias** — "This expert recommends it" without examining the evidence
- **Anecdotal reasoning** — "It worked at my last company" generalized to all contexts
- **False dichotomy** — "Either we gamify it or it's boring" (there are other options)
- **Sunk cost** — "We've already built the content, so we should use it" even if it's the wrong approach
- **Correlation as causation** — "Completion rates went up after we added badges" (did badges cause it?)

## How To Practice

1. For your next design decision, explicitly list: what evidence supports this? What evidence contradicts it? What am I assuming?
2. Practice the "pre-mortem": before implementing a design, imagine it failed. What went wrong? This surfaces hidden assumptions.
3. When reading a research claim, ask: who funded it? What was the sample? What was NOT measured? What alternative explanations exist?
