# Case Writing

## Summary

Case writing (Christensen tradition, Harvard Business School) produces protagonist-centered, decision-driven teaching cases designed to develop judgment through structured discussion. A case is NOT an example or illustration — it's a decision situation that forces the learner to analyze, evaluate, and choose under uncertainty.

## The Christensen Case Method Principles

1. **Protagonist-centered**: the case follows a specific person facing a specific decision. The reader steps into their shoes.
2. **Decision-driven**: the case ends at the decision point — BEFORE the outcome is revealed. The learner must decide, not just observe.
3. **Neutral tone**: the case presents facts, context, and stakeholder perspectives without editorializing. The writer's opinion is invisible.
4. **Rich context**: enough detail for meaningful analysis — industry, organizational culture, financial data, interpersonal dynamics, time pressure, competing stakeholder interests.
5. **Genuine tension**: the decision should be genuinely difficult — reasonable people should disagree about the right course of action.

## Case Anatomy

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Opening** | Hook — the protagonist, the moment of decision, the stakes |
| **Background** | Company/organization context, history, relevant industry dynamics |
| **Protagonist** | Who they are, what they know, what they're responsible for, their constraints |
| **The situation** | Events leading to the decision point, with enough detail to analyze |
| **Data/exhibits** | Financials, organizational charts, market data, interview quotes, emails — the evidence base |
| **Decision point** | The question the protagonist faces — stated or implied. Case ends here. |

### Teaching Note (separate document)

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Synopsis** | Summary for the instructor |
| **Learning objectives** | What the case is designed to teach |
| **Discussion questions** | Sequenced questions that guide analysis (opening, analysis, decision, generalization) |
| **Analysis** | The instructor's analytical framework for the case — what a strong analysis covers |
| **Teaching plan** | How to run the case: timing, board plan, discussion flow, key pivot questions |

## Key Principles

- **Resist the urge to editorialize** — let the facts create the tension. If you need to tell the reader what to think, the case is poorly written.
- **Real decisions are messy** — include conflicting data, incomplete information, and stakeholder disagreements. Clean cases produce shallow analysis.
- **The protagonist must be relatable** — adult learners engage when the protagonist's constraints match their own (time pressure, political complexity, resource limits).
- **End at the decision, not after it** — revealing the outcome kills the analytical exercise. "What would YOU do?" requires not knowing what happened.

## How To Practice

1. Interview someone about a difficult decision they faced at work. Write a 2-page case from their perspective, ending at the decision point.
2. Write teaching notes for a case: 3 discussion questions that move from descriptive ("What's happening?") to analytical ("What are the trade-offs?") to prescriptive ("What should the protagonist do?").
3. Read 3 HBS cases and analyze: how does the writer maintain neutrality? Where is the tension created?
