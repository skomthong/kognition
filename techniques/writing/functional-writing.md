# Functional Writing

## Summary

Functional writing exists to GET THINGS DONE — emails, reports, proposals, procedures, job aids, checklists, policy documents. Its measure of success is not elegance but effectiveness: did the reader understand what to do, decide, or know?

## Genres and Key Principles

### Emails
- Subject line = the action needed or the one-sentence summary
- First sentence = the point. Do not build up to it.
- One topic per email. Two topics = two emails.
- End with a clear next step and deadline: "Please review and reply by Friday."

### Reports
- [Pyramid principle](pyramid-principle.md): recommendation/finding first, evidence and analysis second
- Executive summary that stands alone — a decision-maker who reads only this should get the full picture
- Headings that carry information, not just labels ("Spaced practice reduced error rates by 23%" not "Results")

### Proposals
- Lead with the problem and its cost (why should they care?)
- Propose the solution with clear scope, timeline, and resource needs
- Anticipate objections ([counterargument](counterargument-and-concession.md))
- End with a specific ask

### Procedures and job aids
- Numbered steps in order of execution
- One action per step
- Active voice, imperative mood: "Click Submit" not "The submit button should be clicked"
- Include decision points: "If X, go to step 5. If Y, continue."
- Test with actual users doing the task

### Checklists
- Binary items (done/not done) — not vague descriptions
- Ordered by workflow sequence
- Include verification criteria: "Confirm all participants received the pre-work email"

## How To Practice

1. Write an email that conveys its full message in the first two sentences. Everything else is supporting detail.
2. Take a 10-page report and write a 200-word executive summary that makes the full report optional.
3. Write a 5-step procedure for something you do daily. Give it to someone else. Watch where they get confused.
