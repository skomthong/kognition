# Note-Taking and Knowledge Synthesis

## Summary

Note-taking is how knowledge enters your system. Knowledge synthesis is how isolated notes become connected understanding. Most people take notes they never revisit; effective knowledge workers build systems that compound — where each note connects to existing knowledge and new insights emerge from the connections.

## Note-Taking Methods

| Method | Structure | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Linear notes** | Sequential, follows the source order | Lectures, meetings, quick capture |
| **Cornell method** | Three sections: cues, notes, summary | Study notes with built-in retrieval cues |
| **Mind mapping** | Central idea + radiating branches | Brainstorming, overview, seeing connections |
| **Sketch notes** | Visual + text combination | Conferences, presentations, dual-coded capture |
| **Outline** | Hierarchical indentation | Structured content, reading notes, chapter summaries |

## Knowledge Synthesis Systems

### Zettelkasten (Luhmann)
A networked note system where each note:
- Contains **one idea** (atomic notes)
- Is written in **your own words** (forces processing)
- **Links to other notes** with explicit connection rationale
- Has a **unique identifier** for cross-referencing

The power is in the LINKS — over time, clusters of connected notes reveal patterns, contradictions, and insights that no single source contains. This is synthesis as an emergent property of a system, not a deliberate act.

### Progressive Summarization (Tiago Forte)
Layer-based approach to notes:
1. **Layer 1**: capture — save the source passage
2. **Layer 2**: bold — highlight the most important parts
3. **Layer 3**: highlight — mark the key phrases within the bold
4. **Layer 4**: executive summary — write the insight in your own words
5. **Layer 5**: remix — use the distilled insight in your own work

Each layer compresses; the final layer is YOUR understanding, not the source's words.

### Evergreen Notes (Andy Matuschak)
Notes that:
- Are concept-oriented (titled as claims, not topics: "Spaced practice is more effective than massed practice" not "Spaced practice")
- Are densely linked to other notes
- Are developed incrementally over time
- Accumulate into a personal knowledge system

## Why This Matters for Learning Design

- **You are building a knowledge base** — this entire project IS a Zettelkasten-style system
- **Synthesis is the highest-value analytical skill** — combining sources into original insight is what separates [literature review writing](literature-review-writing.md) from source summarization
- **Retrieval practice applies to YOUR learning** — writing notes in your own words is a retrieval event that strengthens your understanding
- **Schema building** — connecting notes to existing notes mirrors how [schemas](../../principles/schema-and-mental-models.md) form in long-term memory

## Key Principles

- **Write in your own words** — copying quotes is capture, not learning. Restating forces retrieval and integration.
- **One idea per note** — atomic notes are reusable and linkable. Multi-idea notes are locked in their original context.
- **Connection over collection** — a note that links to nothing is a dead end. Ask: "What does this connect to? What does it contradict? What does it extend?"
- **Review and revise** — notes are living documents. Update them as your understanding evolves.
- **Build for your future self** — write notes that are clear enough to be useful months later without context.

## How To Practice

1. After reading an article, close it and write the 3 most important ideas in your own words. Then open the article and check — what did you miss? What did you distort?
2. Take 5 existing notes and find connections between them. Write a synthesis note that combines them.
3. Pick a note-taking system (Zettelkasten, progressive summarization, or evergreen) and commit to it for 30 days.
