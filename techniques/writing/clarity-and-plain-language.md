# Clarity and Plain Language

## Summary

Clear writing reduces the cognitive load on the reader. Every unclear sentence forces the reader to reprocess, guess, or give up. In learning design, unclear writing doesn't just annoy — it directly impairs learning by consuming working memory that should be spent on understanding content.

## Why It Builds on Foundations

- [Cognitive Load](../../principles/cognitive-load.md) — unclear writing is extraneous load. Every ambiguous sentence, jargon term, or convoluted structure wastes working memory on decoding language rather than processing meaning.
- [Attention](../../foundations/attention.md) — clear writing reduces the reader's need to search for meaning; opaque writing forces rereading and divided attention.

## Orwell's Six Rules (from "Politics and the English Language," 1946)

1. Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

Rule 6 is the most important — these are heuristics, not laws. Every rule can be broken when breaking it serves clarity.

## Plain Language Principles

- **Write for the reader, not the writer** — the goal is understanding, not impression
- **Use common words** — "use" not "utilize," "help" not "facilitate," "start" not "commence"
- **Short sentences for complex ideas** — when the concept is hard, the sentence should be simple
- **One idea per sentence** — compound sentences with multiple clauses force the reader to hold too much in working memory
- **Define jargon when you must use it** — if a technical term is necessary, define it on first use
- **Use concrete language** — "the completion rate dropped 20%" not "performance metrics indicated a downward trend"

## Common Clarity Killers

| Problem | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominalization** (turning verbs into nouns) | "The implementation of the program..." | "We implemented the program..." |
| **Hedge stacking** | "It could potentially perhaps be argued that..." | "This suggests..." |
| **Passive chains** | "The report was reviewed and it was determined that changes were needed" | "We reviewed the report and found it needs changes" |
| **Jargon without definition** | "Leverage the synergies of cross-functional alignment" | "Help teams work together" |
| **Unnecessary complexity** | "In the event that" | "If" |

## How To Practice

1. Take a paragraph you've written and cut it by 30% without losing meaning
2. Read your writing aloud — if you stumble, the sentence is too complex
3. Use the Hemingway App or readability checkers as diagnostic tools (not as oracles)
4. Ask someone outside your field to read it — where do they get confused?
