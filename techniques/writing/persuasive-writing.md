# Persuasive Writing

## Summary

Persuasive writing moves the reader toward a position, decision, or action. It combines logical argument ([claims](claims-and-arguments.md) + [evidence](evidence-and-reasoning.md)), rhetorical appeal ([ethos/pathos/logos](rhetoric.md)), and psychological influence principles to create writing that doesn't just inform but convinces.

## Cialdini's Principles of Influence (Applied to Writing)

| Principle | Mechanism | Writing application |
|---|---|---|
| **Reciprocity** | People return favors | Give value first — useful frameworks, honest analysis, practical tools — before asking for buy-in |
| **Commitment/consistency** | People want to be consistent with prior commitments | Get small agreements early ("You'd agree that retention matters...") then build toward larger conclusions |
| **Social proof** | People follow what others do | "78% of organizations that adopted spaced practice saw improvement..." |
| **Authority** | People defer to credible experts | Cite research, demonstrate expertise, use precise language — builds ethos |
| **Liking** | People are persuaded by those they like | Conversational tone, shared experience, genuine acknowledgment of the reader's challenges |
| **Scarcity** | People value what's limited | "The window for intervention is narrow — skills decay begins within 48 hours" |

**Ethical boundary**: Cialdini's principles describe how influence works. In learning contexts, use them to reduce barriers and support learner goals — not to manipulate, coerce, or create false urgency.

## Persuasive Moves

### Framing an issue
How you DEFINE the problem determines what solutions are visible:
- "How do we make training more engaging?" → gamification
- "How do we make training produce measurable behavior change?" → spacing, retrieval, transfer design
- The frame is a persuasive choice. Declare it honestly.

### Positioning your argument
Where you place your position relative to alternatives:
- "Unlike traditional approaches that..." (positioning against the status quo)
- "Building on the evidence for X, we can extend this to..." (positioning as a natural evolution)
- "While [competing approach] addresses Y, it overlooks Z, which is critical because..." (positioning against alternatives)

### Guiding interpretation
Controlling how the reader processes evidence:
- "The most striking finding was..." (directing attention)
- "What this means in practice is..." (translating to implications)
- "The pattern across these three studies suggests..." (naming the pattern for the reader)

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** writing recommendations, proposals, stakeholder communications, calls to action, program marketing, change management communications.

**Use with caution**: distinguish persuasion (honest advocacy for a position supported by evidence) from manipulation (exploiting biases to get compliance). In learning contexts, the standard is: would the learner, fully informed, endorse this influence attempt?

## How To Practice

1. Write a one-page recommendation using at least 3 of Cialdini's principles. Label each one in the margin.
2. Take a factual report and rewrite the introduction as a framing statement — how does the frame change what feels important?
3. Write the same recommendation for two different audiences (executives vs. instructors). Notice how the persuasive moves change.
