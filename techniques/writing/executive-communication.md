# Executive Communication

## Summary

Executive communication is writing and presenting for the most time-constrained, highest-authority audiences — boards, C-suite, senior stakeholders. Every word competes with a 60-hour work week. The standard is not "clear" but "instantly actionable." This extends [executive summary writing](executive-summary-writing.md) and [leadership communication](../narrative/leadership-communication.md) into the specific craft of board-level and strategic messaging.

## Key Principles

### Strategic brevity
- One page maximum for written communication. One key message per slide.
- The first sentence carries the entire message. If the reader stops there, they should have enough.
- Every supporting point must answer "so what?" for THIS audience — not for learners, not for designers, for decision-makers.

### Decision architecture
Structure every communication around a decision:
- **What decision is needed?** (or what information informs the next decision?)
- **What do you recommend?** (state it upfront — [pyramid](pyramid-principle.md))
- **What evidence supports it?** (3 points maximum — [rule of three](../writing/rhetorical-devices.md))
- **What are the risks?** (acknowledge honestly — builds [trust](../../principles/trust-and-credibility.md))
- **What's the ask?** (specific: approval, budget, decision, endorsement)

### Framing for power
- Frame business impact, not learning metrics: "Reduced time-to-competence by 40%, saving $2M in productivity loss" not "Completion rates improved 15%"
- Use [loss framing](../../principles/framing-effects.md) for urgency: "Each month without this costs $X"
- Use [gain framing](../../principles/framing-effects.md) for investment: "This produces $Y in the first year"

### What executives don't want
- Process descriptions ("We conducted a comprehensive analysis...")
- Jargon ("The andragogical implications of our CLT-informed design...")
- Hedging without position ("There are many perspectives on this issue...")
- Data without interpretation (charts without annotation or recommendation)

## How To Practice

1. Take a 10-page proposal and compress it to one page that a CEO could act on in 2 minutes.
2. Write the opening sentence of a board memo. Does it contain the recommendation AND the business impact?
3. Remove every sentence that describes YOUR process rather than THEIR decision.
