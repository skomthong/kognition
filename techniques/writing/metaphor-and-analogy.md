# Metaphor and Analogy

## Summary

Metaphor and analogy connect unfamiliar concepts to familiar ones, making abstract ideas concrete and memorable. They are among the most powerful tools for learning design writing — a good analogy can accomplish in one sentence what a paragraph of explanation cannot.

## Distinctions

| Term | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Metaphor** | Implicit comparison (A IS B) | "Working memory is a bottleneck" |
| **Simile** | Explicit comparison (A is LIKE B) | "Forgetting is like water leaking from a bucket" |
| **Analogy** | Extended comparison mapping multiple features | "A schema is like a filing cabinet — organized knowledge that lets you quickly retrieve what you need without searching every drawer" |
| **Extended metaphor** | Metaphor sustained across multiple sentences/paragraphs | Building an entire section around "learning as architecture" — foundations, scaffolding, load-bearing walls |

## Why They Work (Connection to Foundations)

- [Schema and Mental Models](../../principles/schema-and-mental-models.md) — analogies work by mapping a new concept onto an existing schema. The reader already understands "bottleneck" — attaching "working memory" to that schema transfers structural understanding instantly.
- [Dual Coding](../../principles/dual-coding.md) — vivid metaphors evoke imagery, creating a visual code alongside the verbal one.
- [Cognitive Load](../../principles/cognitive-load.md) — a good analogy reduces load by leveraging existing knowledge rather than building from scratch.

## When Analogy Clarifies vs. Misleads

**Clarifies when:**
- The structural mapping is accurate (the analogy shares relevant features with the target concept)
- The audience has strong schemas for the familiar concept
- You explicitly note where the analogy breaks down

**Misleads when:**
- Surface similarity masks structural difference (the analogy looks right but maps incorrectly)
- The analogy is taken too literally (students remember the analogy, not the actual concept)
- The familiar concept carries unwanted associations

*Example of misleading*: "The brain is like a computer" — implies sequential processing, perfect storage, and binary logic, none of which accurately describe how brains work.

## Best Practices

- **Name the mapping explicitly**: "Working memory is like a bottleneck — there's high capacity on either side (sensory input, long-term storage), but the passage between them is narrow."
- **Flag the limits**: "Unlike an actual bottleneck, working memory capacity can be effectively expanded through schema automation."
- **Use concrete, sensory comparisons**: "like water leaking" is stronger than "like a suboptimal process"
- **One analogy per concept**: multiple competing analogies confuse rather than clarify

## How To Practice

1. Pick an abstract concept from this knowledge base and write 3 different analogies for it. Which one maps most accurately?
2. Find a metaphor you use regularly and test it: where does the mapping hold? Where does it break?
3. Explain a concept to someone outside the field using only analogy. Where do they get confused? That's where the analogy fails.
