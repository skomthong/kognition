# Evidence and Reasoning

## Summary

Evidence is what you present to support a claim. Reasoning is the logical connection between evidence and claim — the explanation of WHY this evidence supports THIS position. Strong writers handle both; weak writers dump evidence and assume it speaks for itself.

## Evidence Types

| Type | Strength | Example | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Empirical** — experimental, meta-analytic | Strongest for causal/factual claims | "A meta-analysis of 200+ studies found d = 0.45 for nudge interventions" | Publication bias, effect size context, generalizability |
| **Statistical** — surveys, observational data | Good for descriptive claims | "78% of participants completed the spaced practice program" | Correlation ≠ causation, sampling bias |
| **Expert testimony** — cited authorities | Adds credibility when aligned with evidence | "Sweller argues that..." | Appeal to authority without evidence is weak |
| **Case/example** — specific instances | Makes abstract claims concrete | "When Company X switched to retrieval-based onboarding, error rates dropped 30%" | Single cases don't prove general claims |
| **Theoretical** — logical derivation from established theory | Supports mechanism claims | "CLT predicts that adding decorative images will increase extraneous load" | Theory without empirical test is speculation |
| **Anecdotal** — personal experience | Illustrates, connects | "In my experience facilitating..." | Weakest as sole evidence; strongest as complement |

## Reasoning Patterns

| Pattern | Logic | Signal words |
|---|---|---|
| **Deductive** | General rule → specific case | "Since... therefore," "If A then B; A; therefore B" |
| **Inductive** | Specific observations → general conclusion | "In each case... suggesting that," "The pattern indicates" |
| **Causal** | X causes/leads to Y | "Because," "as a result," "this leads to," "consequently" |
| **Analogical** | Similar case → similar conclusion | "Just as... so too," "similarly," "in the same way" |
| **Abductive** | Best explanation for observed facts | "The most likely explanation is," "this is best explained by" |

## The Reasoning Gap

Most weak arguments fail HERE — between evidence and claim:

*Weak:* "Spaced practice improves retention (Cepeda et al., 2006). Organizations should implement spaced practice."

The gap: WHY does the research finding lead to that recommendation? What about the organizational context?

*Strong:* "Spaced practice improves retention (Cepeda et al., 2006). Since most corporate training delivers content in a single session with no follow-up, the forgetting curve predicts rapid decay. Implementing even basic spacing — a retrieval quiz 1 week and 4 weeks after training — would directly address this design flaw."

## How To Practice

1. For every piece of evidence you cite, write one sentence starting with "This means..." or "This matters because..."
2. Identify the reasoning pattern you're using (deductive, inductive, causal, analogical). Is it valid?
3. Ask: could someone accept my evidence but reject my conclusion? If yes, your reasoning needs strengthening.
