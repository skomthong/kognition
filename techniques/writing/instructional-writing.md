# Instructional Writing

## Summary

Instructional writing — the text in modules, courses, job aids, and learning materials — is a distinct genre. It's not academic writing (too formal, too dense). It's not marketing writing (too promotional). It's not creative writing (not the point). Instructional writing is EXPLANATION WRITING: its job is to build understanding as efficiently as possible while managing cognitive load.

## Key Principles

### Write for processing, not coverage
Every sentence should help the learner build or refine a schema. If a sentence doesn't advance understanding, cut it. "Coverage" is the enemy of learning — more text does not mean more learning.

### Second person, active voice
- "You will learn to..." not "The learner will..."
- "Calculate the margin" not "The margin should be calculated"
- Direct address creates engagement and clarity

### One concept per chunk
- Each paragraph or screen introduces ONE idea
- Signal what's coming: "There are three types of cognitive load..."
- Provide the concept, then an example, then a practice check

### Examples carry the weight
- Abstract explanations build on examples, not the reverse
- "Here's what cognitive overload looks like in a real onboarding session..." is more effective than a definition followed by an example
- Use worked examples for procedural content

### Conversational style (Mayer's personalization principle)
- [Multimedia Learning](../../principles/multimedia-learning.md) research shows conversational tone improves learning over formal tone
- "Let's look at why this happens" not "The following section examines causal mechanisms"
- But conversational ≠ sloppy — maintain precision

### Signal structure relentlessly
- Headings that tell you what's coming
- "First... Second... Third..." for sequences
- "The key point is..." for emphasis
- "Here's why this matters..." for significance

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing like a textbook** — dense, passive, impersonal. Adults skim or disengage.
- **Writing like marketing** — hype without substance. "This revolutionary approach will transform..."
- **Information dump** — presenting everything the SME knows instead of what the learner needs
- **No examples** — abstract explanations without concrete application
- **No practice** — text followed by more text, with no retrieval check or application

## How To Practice

1. Take a dense paragraph from an academic source and rewrite it as instructional text — second person, conversational, with an example.
2. Write a 200-word explanation of a concept. Then cut it to 100 words without losing the core idea. Then add one example.
3. Read your instructional text and ask: at what point should the learner DO something? If the answer is "never," the text is passive content, not instruction.
