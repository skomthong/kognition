# Rhetorical Devices

## Summary

Rhetorical devices are specific techniques of language that create emphasis, rhythm, surprise, or emotional impact. They are tools — each has a purpose, and using them without purpose creates purple prose instead of persuasion.

## Device Catalog

### Repetition family
| Device | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Anaphora** | Repeating the start of successive clauses | "We will design for retention. We will design for transfer. We will design for the learner's real work." |
| **Epistrophe** | Repeating the end of successive clauses | "When they forget, we fail. When they can't apply it, we fail. When they disengage, we fail." |
| **Anadiplosis** | End of one clause becomes start of next | "Practice builds skill. Skill builds confidence. Confidence builds performance." |

### Contrast family
| Device | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Antithesis** | Juxtaposing opposites in parallel structure | "Not coverage, but competence. Not exposure, but practice." |
| **Chiasmus** | Reversed parallel structure (ABBA) | "Ask not what training can do for metrics — ask what metrics can do for training." |
| **Oxymoron** | Contradictory terms paired | "Desirable difficulty" (Bjork — the term itself is a rhetorical device) |

### Structural devices
| Device | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Rule of three** | Three items — the minimum for a pattern | "Spacing, retrieval, feedback." |
| **Parallelism** | Same grammatical structure across items | "Design it. Build it. Test it. Fix it." |
| **Rhetorical question** | Question that implies its answer | "If learners forget 80% within a month, are we designing training or theater?" |
| **Climax** | Building to the most important point | "It affects engagement. It affects retention. It affects whether anyone actually does their job differently." |

### Figurative language
| Device | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Metaphor** | Implicit comparison | "Working memory is a bottleneck" |
| **Simile** | Explicit comparison (like/as) | "A single workshop without follow-up is like watering a plant once and expecting it to grow" |
| **Analogy** | Extended comparison to explain | See [Metaphor and Analogy](metaphor-and-analogy.md) |

## Key Principles

- **Purpose first**: use a device because it serves the point, not because it sounds impressive
- **Sparingly**: a page full of rhetorical devices exhausts the reader. One well-placed antithesis per section is powerful; three per paragraph is noise.
- **Match the context**: anaphora works in speeches and presentations; it feels overwrought in a technical report. Know your genre.
- **The rule of three is real**: three items feel complete. Two feel incomplete. Four feel like a list. Use three when you want the reader to feel "that's the whole picture."

## How To Practice

1. Take a flat, factual paragraph and rewrite one sentence using antithesis or anaphora. Does it add emphasis or just ornamentation?
2. Study one speech (MLK "I Have a Dream," Obama's 2004 DNC speech, JFK's inaugural). Catalog every rhetorical device. Notice how they combine.
3. Write a 3-sentence pitch for a learning program using the rule of three and one other device.
