# Writing Structures (PEEL, CER, EVID)

## Summary

Writing structures are repeatable patterns for organizing paragraphs and sections. They ensure every paragraph has a point, supports it, and connects to the larger argument. These are scaffolds for the writer — internalize them, then flex as needed.

## PEEL

| Component | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **P** — Point | State the paragraph's main claim | "Retrieval practice is more effective than rereading for long-term retention." |
| **E** — Evidence | Support with data, research, example | "Roediger & Karpicke (2006) found that students who took practice tests retained 50% more after one week." |
| **E** — Explain | Analyze why the evidence matters | "This matters because most corporate training relies on content exposure without retrieval — designing for forgetting." |
| **L** — Link | Connect to the next point or larger argument | "This is why spaced retrieval should be an architectural feature, not an optional add-on." |

## CER (Claim–Evidence–Reasoning)

| Component | What it does |
|---|---|
| **C** — Claim | The assertion you're making |
| **E** — Evidence | The data or facts that support it |
| **R** — Reasoning | The logical connection between evidence and claim — WHY this evidence supports THIS claim |

CER is particularly useful for analytical and research writing where the reasoning chain must be explicit. It's also a framework for teaching learners to write analytically — the "R" is where most beginners fail.

## EVID

| Component | What it does |
|---|---|
| **E** — Evidence | Present the data or observation |
| **V** — Validate | Confirm its relevance and credibility |
| **I** — Interpret | Explain what it means |
| **D** — Draw conclusions | State the implication |

EVID works well for evidence-heavy sections like literature reviews and research summaries.

## When to Use Each

- **PEEL** — default for most body paragraphs in any structured writing
- **CER** — when the argument chain matters and reasoning must be explicit (academic, analytical, case writing)
- **EVID** — when working with empirical evidence and need to show the interpretation chain

## How To Practice

1. Take a paragraph you've written and label each sentence: is it Point, Evidence, Explanation, or Link? If any component is missing, add it.
2. Write a CER paragraph about a concept from this knowledge base — make the Reasoning explicit.
3. Rewrite the same paragraph using PEEL. Notice what changes.
