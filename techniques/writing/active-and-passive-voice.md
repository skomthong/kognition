# Active and Passive Voice

## Summary

Active voice: the subject performs the action ("The facilitator reviewed the submissions"). Passive voice: the subject receives the action ("The submissions were reviewed by the facilitator" or "The submissions were reviewed"). Choosing between them is a clarity and emphasis decision, not a grammar rule.

## When to Use Active Voice

- **Default choice** — active is usually clearer, shorter, and more direct
- **When the actor matters** — "The manager approved the budget" (who approved matters)
- **Instructions and procedures** — "Click the submit button" not "The submit button should be clicked"
- **When you want energy and directness** — active voice propels the reader forward

## When to Use Passive Voice

Passive is NOT always wrong. Use it when:
- **The actor is unknown or irrelevant** — "The data was collected over 6 months" (who collected doesn't matter)
- **The receiver is the focus** — "Three participants were excluded from the analysis" (the participants matter, not who excluded them)
- **Diplomacy or objectivity** — "Mistakes were made" (deliberately de-emphasizing blame, common in research and formal reports)
- **Scientific convention** — "The survey was administered to 200 participants" (standard in methods sections)

## How to Convert

| Passive | Active |
|---|---|
| The report was written by the team | The team wrote the report |
| Feedback should be provided within 24 hours | Provide feedback within 24 hours |
| The decision was made to restructure | We decided to restructure |
| It was observed that performance improved | We observed that performance improved |

## Common Mistakes

- **Zombie test** (Rebecca Johnson): if you can add "by zombies" after the verb and it makes grammatical sense, it's passive. "The report was written [by zombies]" = passive.
- **Confusing passive with past tense**: "The team reviewed the report" is active past tense, not passive
- **Over-correcting**: eliminating all passive voice makes writing sound aggressive or blame-heavy in contexts requiring neutrality

## How To Practice

1. Highlight every verb in a paragraph. For each passive construction, ask: does the reader need to know who did this? If yes, rewrite as active.
2. Write instructions for a task entirely in active voice. Then identify which instructions genuinely benefit from passive (usually none).
