# Research Writing

## Summary

Research writing makes and defends knowledge claims using evidence, analysis, and transparent reasoning. It is the genre of academic papers, literature reviews, evidence summaries, and analytical reports. Its distinguishing features: every claim is supported, sources are cited, reasoning is explicit, and limitations are acknowledged.

## Structure

Most research writing follows a predictable architecture:

| Section | Job |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Frame the problem, state the purpose, preview the argument |
| **Literature review / Background** | Synthesize what's known, identify gaps, establish context |
| **Methods** (if empirical) | Describe how evidence was gathered, with enough detail to evaluate and replicate |
| **Findings / Analysis** | Present evidence and interpret it |
| **Discussion** | What the findings mean, how they connect to existing knowledge, limitations |
| **Conclusion** | Summary + implications + what comes next |

## Key Principles

- **Argument + evidence + analysis** — the trinity of research writing. All three must be present.
- **Academic tone**: precise, measured, qualified where appropriate. Not stiff or jargon-heavy — Joseph Williams (*Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*) shows that academic writing can be clear AND rigorous.
- **Citation is not name-dropping** — cite to give credit, provide evidence, and show the reader where to verify. Cite the specific claim, not just the author generally.
- **Synthesis over summary** — don't just report what each source says; build a narrative about what the sources TOGETHER mean (see [Analytical Writing](analytical-writing.md)).
- **Qualify appropriately** — "suggests" not "proves"; "under these conditions" not "always"
- **Limitations section is a strength** — acknowledging what you didn't study, what could go wrong, and what alternatives exist builds credibility.

## How To Practice

1. Write a 500-word evidence summary on a topic from this knowledge base. Include: claim, 3+ sources synthesized (not listed), analysis, and one qualification.
2. Take a paragraph of research writing and check: is every claim supported? Is the reasoning explicit? Are qualifications appropriate?
3. Read Williams' *Style* — it demonstrates that academic clarity is a skill, not a contradiction.
