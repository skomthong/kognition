# Claims and Arguments

## Summary

A claim is a statement that requires support. An argument is a claim backed by evidence and reasoning. Most weak writing fails not because the ideas are bad, but because the writer confuses claims with topics, assertions with arguments, and opinions with reasoned positions.

## Key Distinctions

| Term | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Topic** | What you're writing about | "Spaced practice in corporate training" |
| **Claim** | A debatable position about the topic | "Most corporate training is designed to be forgotten because it ignores spacing" |
| **Argument** | Claim + evidence + reasoning | The claim above + Cepeda's meta-analysis + analysis of typical training schedules |
| **Assertion** | A claim stated without support | "Gamification doesn't work" (says who? under what conditions?) |
| **Fact** | A verifiable statement not requiring argument | "Ebbinghaus published his forgetting curve research in 1885" |

## Argument Architecture

A complete argument has three parts (mapping to [CER](writing-structures.md)):
1. **Claim** — what you're asserting
2. **Evidence** — data, research, examples that support it
3. **Reasoning** — the logical bridge explaining WHY this evidence supports THIS claim

The reasoning is where most arguments fail. Evidence doesn't speak for itself — you must explain the connection.

## Types of Claims

| Type | What it asserts | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Factual** | Something is true/false | "Retrieval practice produces larger retention gains than rereading" |
| **Causal** | X causes Y | "Cognitive overload during onboarding causes higher early-tenure attrition" |
| **Evaluative** | Something is good/bad/better/worse | "SAM is better suited than ADDIE for agile L&D teams" |
| **Policy** | Something should be done | "Organizations should replace annual compliance training with spaced micro-assessments" |

Each type requires different evidence: factual claims need data, causal claims need controlled evidence or strong mechanism, evaluative claims need criteria, policy claims need all three plus feasibility.

## Common Mistakes

- **Claim without evidence** — "This approach is more effective" (than what? based on what?)
- **Evidence without claim** — listing statistics without stating what they mean
- **Overclaiming** — "This proves that..." (most evidence suggests, indicates, or supports — rarely "proves")
- **Hidden assumptions** — claims that depend on unstated premises the reader may not share
- **Confusing correlation with causation** in causal claims

## How To Practice

1. Write a claim about something in this knowledge base. Now ask: is this a factual, causal, evaluative, or policy claim? What evidence type does it need?
2. Find an assertion in your writing and upgrade it to an argument by adding evidence + reasoning.
3. For every claim you make, try writing the opposing claim. If you can't, your claim might be too obvious to be worth making.
