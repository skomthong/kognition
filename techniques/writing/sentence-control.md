# Sentence Control

## Summary

Sentence control is the ability to vary sentence length, structure, and rhythm deliberately — using short sentences for emphasis and clarity, longer sentences for nuance and connection, and variation to maintain reader engagement. It's the difference between competent writing and writing that carries the reader forward.

## How It Works

### Length as a tool

- **Short sentences punch.** They create emphasis. They force attention. Use them for key points, transitions, and moments of clarity.
- **Medium sentences carry the main workload** — they explain, connect, and develop ideas at a comfortable cognitive pace (15-25 words).
- **Long sentences build complexity** — subordinate clauses, qualifications, multi-part ideas that need to be held together. Use sparingly and only when the parts genuinely belong together.

### The rhythm principle

Monotonous sentence length puts the reader to sleep regardless of content. Variation creates rhythm:

*Bad (all same length):*
> Adults learn best when content is relevant. They need to see practical application. Motivation increases with autonomy. Feedback should be timely and specific.

*Better (varied):*
> Adults learn best when content is relevant — when they can see exactly how it applies to their work. Motivation increases with autonomy. And feedback? It needs to be timely, specific, and actionable.

### Hemingway's principle

Hemingway wrote short, declarative sentences that carried enormous weight through what they left out. The lesson for learning designers: when the idea is complex, the sentence should be simple. Don't make the reader decode your syntax AND your concept at the same time.

## Techniques

- **Front-load the main point** — put the subject and verb early. Don't make the reader wait through a subordinate clause to find out what the sentence is about.
- **One idea per sentence for complex content** — compound sentences work for simple connections, not for introducing new concepts.
- **Use fragments deliberately** — "Not always." Fragments create emphasis when used sparingly. They violate grammar rules in service of clarity.
- **Read aloud** — if you run out of breath, the sentence is too long. If it sounds robotic, it needs variation.

## How To Practice

1. Write a paragraph with no sentence longer than 8 words. Then rewrite it with no sentence shorter than 20 words. Then combine the best of both.
2. Count the word length of every sentence in a paragraph you've written. If they're all within 5 words of each other, revise for variation.
3. Find a piece of writing you admire and map the sentence lengths. Notice the pattern.
