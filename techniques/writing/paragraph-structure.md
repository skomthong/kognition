# Paragraph Structure

## Summary

A paragraph is the basic unit of organized thought in writing. Each paragraph should do ONE job — make one point, develop one idea, present one piece of evidence. The internal structure determines whether the reader follows the argument or gets lost.

## Anatomy of a Strong Paragraph

1. **Topic sentence** — states the paragraph's main point. Usually first. The reader should be able to skim topic sentences and get the argument's skeleton.
2. **Evidence / support** — data, examples, quotes, observations that back the claim
3. **Analysis / interpretation** — explains WHY the evidence supports the point. This is where most weak writing fails — evidence without analysis is a data dump.
4. **Link / transition** — connects to the next paragraph or back to the larger argument

## The "So What?" Test

After every piece of evidence, the reader is silently asking "So what?" Your analysis answers that question. If you present a statistic, explain what it means. If you quote a source, explain why it matters for your argument.

*Weak:* "Spaced practice improves retention (Cepeda et al., 2006)."
*Strong:* "Spaced practice improves retention (Cepeda et al., 2006) — which means that a single workshop without follow-up boosters is designed to be forgotten."

## Common Mistakes

- **No topic sentence** — the reader has to read the whole paragraph to figure out what it's about
- **Evidence without analysis** — listing facts without explaining their significance
- **Multiple points in one paragraph** — forces the reader to track two threads; split it
- **Paragraph too long** — if it fills more than half a page, it probably contains multiple ideas
- **Paragraph too short** — a single sentence is rarely a complete thought (except for emphasis)

## How To Practice

1. Write a paragraph. Underline the topic sentence. If you can't find it, add one.
2. For every piece of evidence, write one sentence of analysis: "This means..." or "This matters because..."
3. Read only the topic sentences of a document. Do they tell a coherent story on their own?
