# Precision and Concision

## Summary

Precision means choosing the exact right word. Concision means using no more words than needed. Together they are the highest-leverage writing skills — every unnecessary word dilutes meaning and adds cognitive load; every imprecise word forces the reader to guess.

Strunk & White's Rule 17: "Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences."

## Precision: Choosing the Right Word

| Imprecise | Precise (depending on what you mean) |
|---|---|
| "good" | effective, valid, reliable, clear, appropriate, sufficient |
| "things" | factors, variables, elements, constraints, principles |
| "very important" | critical, essential, foundational, non-negotiable |
| "get" | obtain, achieve, acquire, develop, receive, earn |
| "do" | perform, execute, implement, conduct, complete |
| "interesting" | (say WHY it's interesting — "this contradicts the standard model" is more useful than "this is interesting") |

### The "said what you mean" test
After writing a sentence, ask: could this be misinterpreted? If someone read this in a different context, would they understand the same thing you intended?

## Concision: Cutting What Doesn't Earn Its Place

| Wordy | Concise |
|---|---|
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "Due to the fact that" | "Because" |
| "At this point in time" | "Now" |
| "It is important to note that" | (just say the thing) |
| "In the event that" | "If" |
| "A large number of" | "Many" |
| "Has the ability to" | "Can" |
| "For the purpose of" | "To" or "For" |
| "On a daily basis" | "Daily" |

### The "does this sentence add value?" test
For every sentence, ask: if I deleted this, would the reader miss anything? If not, delete it.

### The 30% cut exercise
Take any draft and cut 30% of the words without losing meaning. Almost every draft can survive this — and gets stronger.

## Key Principles

- **Precision and concision work together** — a precise word replaces a vague phrase. "Effective" replaces "really good at getting results."
- **Concision is not brevity** — a 500-word explanation can be concise if every word earns its place. A 50-word sentence can be bloated.
- **Jargon is sometimes precise** — "scaffolding" is more precise than "providing support that is gradually removed as the learner improves." Use jargon when the audience knows it; define it when they don't.
- **Qualifiers weaken** — "somewhat," "rather," "quite," "very," "fairly" usually add nothing. Cut them and see if the sentence improves.

## How To Practice

1. Take a paragraph and cut 30%. Repeat until you can't cut more without losing meaning.
2. Circle every adjective and adverb. Delete each one and check if the sentence still works. Keep only the ones that change the meaning.
3. Search your writing for "it is," "there are," "this is" — these often introduce empty constructions that can be tightened.
