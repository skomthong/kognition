# Decision Theory

## Definition

Decision theory studies how decisions SHOULD be made (normative theory) and how they ACTUALLY are made (descriptive theory). The gap between the two is where biases, heuristics, and design interventions live.

## Normative vs. Descriptive

| Approach | Assumption | Key framework |
|---|---|---|
| **Normative** (rational) | Decisions should maximize expected utility given preferences and probabilities | Expected utility theory, Bayesian updating, decision analysis |
| **Descriptive** (behavioral) | Decisions are shaped by heuristics, biases, framing, and context | Prospect theory, bounded rationality, dual-process models |
| **Prescriptive** (applied) | How to improve real decisions given human limitations | Decision aids, debiasing, structured processes, choice architecture |

The prescriptive approach is most relevant for learning design: accept that humans are boundedly rational, then design decisions structures, training, and tools that improve judgment within those limits.

## Key Concepts

- **Expected value**: probability × outcome. Rational choice maximizes expected value — but humans don't, because of loss aversion, certainty effects, and framing.
- **Risk vs. uncertainty**: risk has quantifiable probabilities; uncertainty doesn't. Most real decisions are under uncertainty, not risk.
- **Decision quality vs. outcome quality**: a good decision can have a bad outcome (due to uncertainty), and a bad decision can have a good outcome (due to luck). Evaluate PROCESS, not just results.
- **Satisficing vs. optimizing** (Simon): satisficers seek "good enough" solutions efficiently; optimizers seek the best solution exhaustively. In complex environments with time pressure, satisficing often outperforms optimizing.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Design decisions are decisions under uncertainty** — you don't KNOW whether spacing will work with THIS audience until you try. Decision theory says: use the best available evidence, accept uncertainty, and build in evaluation.
- **Evaluation should assess decision quality** — not just "did scores improve?" but "was the design decision well-reasoned given available evidence?"
- **Teach decision skills through practice** — [decision-making programs](../patterns/decision-making-programs.md) apply decision theory to leadership development.
