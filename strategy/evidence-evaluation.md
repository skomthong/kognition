# Evidence Evaluation

## Definition

Evidence evaluation is the systematic assessment of how much trust to place in a claim based on the quality, quantity, and relevance of supporting evidence. It's the strategic skill that connects [critical thinking](../techniques/writing/critical-thinking.md) to professional practice: not just "can I think critically?" but "how do I make good decisions given imperfect evidence?"

## Evidence Hierarchy

| Level | Type | Strength for causal claims |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs | Strongest — synthesize multiple controlled studies |
| **2** | Randomized controlled trials | Strong — controls for confounds |
| **3** | Quasi-experimental studies | Moderate — some control, some confounds |
| **4** | Observational / correlational studies | Weak for causation, useful for patterns |
| **5** | Case studies, expert opinion, practitioner experience | Weakest for causation, valuable for mechanism and context |

**Important nuance**: the hierarchy ranks CAUSAL warrant, not overall value. A rich case study may be more useful for design decisions than a meta-analysis of a different population. Context relevance matters alongside evidence level.

## Evaluation Questions

For any evidence claim, ask:
1. **What type of evidence?** (meta-analysis, experiment, survey, anecdote?)
2. **How was it produced?** (methods, sample, controls, measurement)
3. **How strong is it?** (effect size, confidence interval, replication)
4. **How relevant is it?** (to MY context, population, constraints)
5. **What are the limits?** (what wasn't studied, what could confound, what's the generalizability boundary)
6. **Who produced it?** (funding, conflicts of interest, reputation)
7. **Does it converge with other evidence?** (one study is suggestive; multiple convergent studies are convincing)

## Types of Knowledge

| Type | Characteristics | How to evaluate |
|---|---|---|
| **Empirical** | Based on observation, experiment, data | Methods rigor, replication, effect sizes |
| **Theoretical** | Based on logical derivation from established principles | Internal coherence, predictive power, parsimony |
| **Tacit/experiential** | Based on practice experience, intuition | Track record, context match, triangulation with other evidence |
| **Procedural** | Know-how: how to do things | Does it work? Is it efficient? Does it transfer? |

## Epistemological Awareness

- **What counts as "knowing"?** Different fields and cultures have different evidence standards. Being aware of YOUR standards — and others' — prevents talking past each other.
- **Reflective judgment** (King & Kitchener): mature thinkers recognize that knowledge is constructed, uncertain, and contextual — but still make well-reasoned judgments based on available evidence. This is the target state for evidence-literate professionals.
