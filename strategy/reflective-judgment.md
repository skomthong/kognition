# Reflective Judgment

## Definition

The Reflective Judgment Model (King & Kitchener, 1994) describes how people's assumptions about knowledge and knowing develop through stages — from believing knowledge is certain and given by authorities, to recognizing that knowledge is constructed, uncertain, and must be evaluated through evidence and reasoning. This is the epistemic development trajectory that separates mature professional judgment from naive certainty.

## The Seven Stages (Simplified into Three Levels)

### Pre-reflective thinking (Stages 1-3)
- Knowledge is CERTAIN — it's either known or not known
- Authorities have the answers; the learner's job is to find the right authority
- Evidence is not distinguished from belief
- "The expert says so, therefore it's true"

### Quasi-reflective thinking (Stages 4-5)
- Knowledge involves UNCERTAINTY — but uncertainty is uncomfortable and often leads to relativism ("everyone has their opinion")
- Evidence is recognized but not systematically evaluated
- "There are different perspectives, so who can really say?"

### Reflective thinking (Stages 6-7)
- Knowledge is CONSTRUCTED through inquiry but can be evaluated for quality
- Evidence is systematically weighed; conclusions are probabilistic and revisable
- Uncertainty is accepted as normal; judgment is based on best available evidence
- "Based on the evidence, here's what I conclude — and here's what would change my mind"

## Key Principles

- **Reflective judgment is the target state for professional learning** — the ability to make well-reasoned judgments under uncertainty, using evidence, while acknowledging limits. This is what evidence-based practice means.
- **Development can be supported but not forced** — challenging learners' epistemic assumptions (through ill-structured problems, competing perspectives, evidence evaluation) promotes development. Lectures and certainty reinforce pre-reflective thinking.
- **Many adults remain at quasi-reflective levels** — recognizing uncertainty but defaulting to relativism ("everyone's entitled to their opinion") rather than evidence-based judgment. The move from Stage 5 to Stage 6 is the critical transition for professional practice.
- **Problem-based learning and case method develop reflective judgment** — ill-structured problems with no single right answer force learners to construct, defend, and revise knowledge claims.
- **This connects everything in Layer 7** — [evidence evaluation](evidence-evaluation.md), [calibration](../principles/calibration-and-judgment-accuracy.md), [causal inference](causal-inference.md), and [sensemaking](sensemaking.md) are all reflective judgment skills.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Design for ill-structured problems** — well-structured problems (one right answer) develop competence. Ill-structured problems (multiple defensible positions) develop judgment.
- **Require evidence-based reasoning** — "What's your position AND what's your evidence AND what would change your mind?"
- **Expose learners to competing perspectives** — not to produce relativism but to force evaluation: which perspective is better supported?
- **Model reflective judgment** — facilitators who say "I'm not sure, but here's what the evidence suggests" model the target epistemic stance.
