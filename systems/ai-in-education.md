# AI in Education

## Definition

AI in education encompasses intelligent tutoring systems, adaptive learning platforms, AI-assisted assessment, and generative AI tools used for content creation, feedback, and learner support. UNESCO's 2023 guidance emphasizes human-centered approaches and flags risks to agency, inclusion, equity, and privacy.

## Key Applications

| Application | Affordance | Risks |
|---|---|---|
| **GenAI for content creation** | Drafting, adapting, translating learning materials | Hallucination, quality assurance, over-reliance |
| **AI tutoring** | Personalized explanation, practice dialogue, Socratic questioning | Shallow interactions, replacing human facilitation |
| **AI-assisted assessment** | Automated scoring, feedback generation, rubric application | Bias, fairness, academic integrity concerns |
| **AI as practice partner** | Role-play, scenario dialogue, coaching simulation | Unreliable behavior in edge cases, false sense of competence |
| **Prompt engineering for learning** | Scaffolding learner thinking through structured prompts | Prompts that replace thinking rather than scaffold it |

## Key Principles

- AI is a tool, not a pedagogy — the quality of the underlying instructional design determines whether AI helps.
- **Human oversight**: AI should augment, not replace, human judgment in high-stakes decisions (assessment, credentialing, feedback on sensitive topics).
- **Transparency**: disclose when AI is used, what it can and cannot do, and how it handles learner data.
- **Privacy**: AI systems that process learner data require governance (NIST PF, data minimization, consent). Model training on learner data is a specific risk requiring explicit policy.
- **Bias and fairness**: AI systems can perpetuate or amplify bias in assessment, feedback, and content recommendation.
- **Academic integrity**: establish clear policies on acceptable AI use in learning and assessment contexts.
