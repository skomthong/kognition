# TAM and UTAUT (Technology Adoption Models)

## Definition

**TAM** (Technology Acceptance Model; Davis, 1989) proposes that **perceived usefulness** and **perceived ease of use** are key determinants of whether people adopt and use technology. **UTAUT** (Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology; Venkatesh et al., 2003) synthesizes eight prior models into four core determinants: performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions — moderated by age, gender, experience, and voluntariness.

## How It Works

| Construct | TAM term | UTAUT term | Design implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Will this help me?" | Perceived usefulness | Performance expectancy | Demonstrate value with evidence and peer testimonials |
| "Is this easy to use?" | Perceived ease of use | Effort expectancy | Reduce friction, improve UX, provide onboarding |
| "Do others expect me to use it?" | — | Social influence | Leverage peer adoption, manager endorsement, norms |
| "Does the organization support it?" | — | Facilitating conditions | Provide training, help desk, IT support, time allocation |

## Key Principles

- Use TAM/UTAUT as a diagnostic when ed-tech adoption stalls: is the barrier usefulness (people don't see the value), ease (it's too hard), social (no one else uses it), or support (no help available)?
- Perceived usefulness is the strongest predictor — even clunky tools get adopted if people believe they help.
- Ease of use matters most for initial adoption; usefulness matters most for continued use.
- Combine with diffusion of innovations (Rogers) for system-level rollout strategy.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** introducing or evaluating learning technologies — LMS, LXP, new tools, mobile learning platforms. **Limitations:** explains adoption intent better than actual sustained use. Contextual factors (organizational culture, competing tools, mandatory vs. voluntary use) moderate everything.
