# Diffusion of Innovations

## Definition

Diffusion of innovations (Rogers, 1962/2003) explains how new ideas, practices, and technologies spread through social systems over time. Adoption follows a predictable pattern across five adopter categories, and five attributes of innovations predict adoption speed.

## How It Works

**Adopter categories** (bell curve distribution):
1. **Innovators** (2.5%) — risk-tolerant, seek novelty
2. **Early adopters** (13.5%) — opinion leaders, strategic adopters
3. **Early majority** (34%) — deliberate, adopt when evidence and peers confirm value
4. **Late majority** (34%) — skeptical, adopt under social pressure or necessity
5. **Laggards** (16%) — tradition-bound, adopt last or never

**Innovation attributes** that predict adoption speed:
- **Relative advantage** — is it better than what exists?
- **Compatibility** — does it fit existing values, experiences, needs?
- **Complexity** — is it easy to understand and use?
- **Trialability** — can it be experimented with on a limited basis?
- **Observability** — are results visible to others?

## Key Principles

- For ed-tech rollouts: target early adopters first, make success visible, reduce complexity (usability), enable trialability (sandboxes, pilots), and demonstrate relative advantage with evidence.
- The "chasm" between early adopters and early majority is where most ed-tech adoptions stall — early majority needs peer evidence and institutional support, not just innovation excitement.
- Faculty/facilitator adoption follows the same curve — resistance is predictable, not personal.
- Use alongside TAM/UTAUT: Rogers explains system-level diffusion; TAM explains individual-level adoption decisions.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** introducing new learning technologies, platforms, or pedagogical practices at organizational scale. **Limitations:** descriptive, not prescriptive — tells you *what happens* during adoption, not exactly *how to make it happen*. Must be combined with change management tactics.
