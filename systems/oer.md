# Open Educational Resources (OER)

## Definition

OER are teaching, learning, and research materials in the public domain or released under open licenses (typically Creative Commons) that permit free use, adaptation, and redistribution. UNESCO's 2019 OER Recommendation promotes OER as a tool for equitable access to quality education.

## Creative Commons Licenses

| License | Allows | Restrictions |
|---|---|---|
| **CC BY** | Share, adapt, commercial use | Must give credit |
| **CC BY-SA** | Share, adapt, commercial use | Must give credit; derivatives share same license |
| **CC BY-NC** | Share, adapt | Must give credit; no commercial use |
| **CC BY-NC-SA** | Share, adapt | Credit, non-commercial, same license |
| **CC BY-ND** | Share only | Credit; no derivatives (cannot adapt) |
| **CC BY-NC-ND** | Share only | Credit, non-commercial, no derivatives |

For remixing and adaptation, use **CC BY** or **CC BY-SA**. ND licenses prevent adaptation and are less useful for instructional designers.

## Key Principles

- **Quality assurance**: OER quality varies widely. Evaluate for accuracy, currency, accessibility, and alignment before adopting. Peer review and institutional endorsement help.
- **Adaptation is the value**: OER's power is not free content — it's the legal right to adapt, localize, and improve materials for your specific learners.
- **Accessibility**: OER must meet the same WCAG/UDL standards as proprietary content. "Free" does not mean "accessible."
- **Sustainability**: who maintains and updates OER over time? Plan for curation and revision.
- **Cost reduction vs. quality**: OER can dramatically reduce textbook and material costs, but only if quality and alignment are maintained.
