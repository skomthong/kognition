# Content Operations and Lifecycle

## Summary

Content operations is the system for creating, reviewing, publishing, maintaining, retiring, and governing learning content across its full lifecycle. Without content ops, organizations accumulate stale, redundant, inconsistent content that confuses learners and wastes maintenance effort. Content ops treats learning content as a managed asset, not a one-time deliverable.

## The Content Lifecycle

| Phase | Activities | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| **Plan** | Needs analysis, content audit, gap identification | What content is needed? What already exists? |
| **Create** | Writing, design, media production, review | Who creates? What standards apply? Who reviews? |
| **Review/approve** | SME review, QA, accessibility check, stakeholder sign-off | Is it accurate, aligned, accessible, and approved? |
| **Publish** | Deploy to LMS/LXP, metadata tagging, cataloging | Where does it live? How do learners find it? |
| **Maintain** | Update for accuracy, relevance, regulatory changes | Who owns updates? How often? What triggers a review? |
| **Measure** | Usage analytics, effectiveness data, learner feedback | Is it being used? Is it working? |
| **Retire** | Archive or remove outdated content | When does content expire? Who decides? |

## Key Principles

- **Content has an expiry date** — compliance content changes with regulations. Case studies age. Tools get updated. Build review cycles into the system, not as afterthoughts.
- **Ownership must be explicit** — every piece of content needs an OWNER responsible for accuracy and currency. Unowned content decays.
- **Consistency requires standards** — style guides, templates, naming conventions, metadata schemas, and accessibility checklists create consistency across creators.
- **Reuse over recreation** — modular content (learning objects, reusable components) reduces duplication and maintenance burden. But reuse requires [information architecture](information-architecture.md) — findable content gets reused; buried content gets recreated.
- **Governance ≠ bureaucracy** — lightweight governance (clear ownership, simple review triggers, automated reminders) works better than heavy approval workflows that nobody follows.

## Content Audit

A content audit inventories existing content and evaluates its status:

| Audit dimension | Questions |
|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | Is the content still factually correct? |
| **Currency** | Does it reflect current tools, policies, practices? |
| **Usage** | Is anyone actually using it? (analytics) |
| **Alignment** | Does it align with current learning objectives and competency models? |
| **Accessibility** | Does it meet current WCAG/UDL standards? |
| **Redundancy** | Does another asset cover the same content better? |
| **Quality** | Does it meet current design and production standards? |

## Application to Learning Design

- **Before building new content**: audit what exists. You may need to update, not create from scratch.
- **After building**: assign ownership, set review dates, establish update triggers.
- **At scale**: content ops becomes infrastructure — metadata standards, taxonomy, review workflows, analytics dashboards, retirement policies.
