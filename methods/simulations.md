# Simulations

## Definition

A simulation is an instructional method or environment that reproduces critical features of real tasks and contexts to allow practice, decision-making, and feedback under controlled conditions. Issenberg et al. (BEME systematic review) identify features associated with effective learning: feedback/debriefing, deliberate practice, curriculum integration, and controlled environments. Sitzmann's meta-analysis reports higher self-efficacy (+20%), declarative knowledge (+11%), procedural knowledge (+14%), and retention (+9%) from simulation games, while noting strong evidence of publication bias.

## How To Apply It

- Design scenarios for deliberate practice: repeatable cycles with adjustable difficulty and clear feedback.
- Build debrief as a required phase with structured prompts and facilitator guidance (Fanning & Gaba).
- Fidelity should match objectives — increase when it improves cue validity or transfer, not "high fidelity by default."
- Include explicit transfer supports: job aids, spaced boosters, supervisor reinforcement.
- Align simulation assessments to learning outcomes; use testing standards when simulations inform high-stakes decisions.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** performance requires judgment under constraints, safe failure is essential, repeated practice and feedback are needed. **Limitations:** high development cost; risk of extraneous load from UI complexity; publication bias in the research base means effects should be verified locally.
