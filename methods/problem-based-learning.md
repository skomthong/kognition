# Problem-Based Learning (PBL)

## Definition

PBL (Barrows; Hmelo-Silver, 2004) is a learner-centered approach where students learn through facilitated engagement with ill-structured, real-world problems. Learners identify what they need to know, conduct self-directed inquiry, and synthesize findings to address the problem.

## How To Apply It

1. Present an authentic, ill-structured problem (no single right answer, multiple relevant factors)
2. Learners identify learning needs — what do they need to know to address this problem?
3. Self-directed inquiry — research, study, consult resources
4. Return to the problem — apply findings, discuss, revise understanding
5. Reflect on the learning process and content

The facilitator guides inquiry without lecturing — asking questions, managing group dynamics, prompting deeper analysis.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** learning objectives include integration, clinical reasoning, complex judgment, and self-directed inquiry. Strong in medical education, executive education, and professional development.

**Limitations:** can overload novices who lack schemas for the problem domain — scaffold heavily. Requires skilled facilitation. Less efficient than direct instruction for teaching foundational facts (use PBL after a base of knowledge exists).
