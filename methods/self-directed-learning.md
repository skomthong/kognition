# Self-Directed Learning (SDL)

## Definition

Self-directed learning (Tough, 1971; Knowles, 1975; Garrison, 1997) describes how adults plan and execute substantial "learning projects" outside formal instruction. Tough's research found that adults engage in an average of 5+ major learning projects per year, mostly self-planned. Knowles positioned SDL as a core andragogical assumption. Garrison's PRO model frames SDL as requiring self-management (control), self-monitoring (cognitive responsibility), and motivation (entering and persisting).

## How To Apply It

- **Scaffold self-direction** — not all adults are equally self-directed. Provide planning tools, choice architecture, resource guides, and progress tracking to support SDL readiness.
- **Learning contracts** — negotiated agreements between learner and facilitator specifying goals, strategies, evidence, and evaluation criteria (Knowles).
- **Resource design** — curate and organize resources so self-directed learners can find what they need when they need it (connects to Five Moments of Need and microlearning).
- **Coaching and mentoring** — provide human support for goal-setting, strategy selection, and reflection.
- **Gradually release control** — start with more structure, then shift ownership to the learner as readiness grows.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** adult learners have diverse goals, varied experience, and need autonomy — professional development, independent study, self-paced programs, informal workplace learning.

**Limitations:** SDL readiness varies — assuming all adults can self-direct without support leads to dropout and frustration. Low self-regulation skills (common in adults returning to learning after gaps) need explicit scaffolding. SDL is not "leave them alone."
