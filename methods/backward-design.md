# Backward Design (Understanding by Design)

## Definition

Understanding by Design (Wiggins & McTighe, 1998) frames backward design as starting with desired results and transfer goals, then determining acceptable evidence, and only then planning learning experiences and instruction. It directly combats "content-first" design where slides precede outcomes.

## How To Apply It

Three stages:
1. **Identify desired results**: What should learners understand and be able to transfer? (Not "what content should I cover?")
2. **Determine acceptable evidence**: What would count as proof of understanding and transfer? Design assessments before activities.
3. **Plan learning experiences**: What activities, instruction, and resources will prepare learners to demonstrate the evidence?

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** designing courses, modules, executive education — any context where coherence between goals, evidence, and activities matters. Especially valuable when SMEs provide content but outcomes and evidence are undefined.

**Limitations:** UbD is a planning framework, not a causal learning theory. Weak outcome statements or weak evidence designs still produce misalignment. Does not automatically guarantee good outcomes.
