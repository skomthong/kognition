# Transformative Learning

## Definition

Transformative learning (Mezirow, 1991) is the process by which adults critically examine their assumptions, beliefs, and frames of reference — and revise them when they prove inadequate. The result is a fundamental shift in perspective, not just new knowledge or skills. Mezirow calls these "meaning perspectives" (broad worldviews) and "meaning schemes" (specific beliefs/attitudes).

## How It Works

Mezirow's phases of transformation:
1. **Disorienting dilemma** — an experience that doesn't fit existing assumptions
2. **Self-examination** — emotional and cognitive processing of the dilemma
3. **Critical assessment** — questioning underlying assumptions
4. **Recognition** — discovering that others share similar experiences
5. **Exploring options** — identifying new roles, relationships, or actions
6. **Planning a course of action** — deciding what to do differently
7. **Acquiring knowledge/skills** — building capacity for the new perspective
8. **Trying new roles** — provisional practice
9. **Building competence/confidence** — gaining efficacy in the new frame
10. **Reintegration** — incorporating the new perspective into one's life

## How To Apply It

- **Use for deep change**: leadership development, DEI, ethics, professional identity formation, career transitions — contexts where the goal is shifting how people *see*, not just what they *know*.
- **Create disorienting dilemmas**: carefully designed cases, cross-cultural exposure, perspective-taking exercises, immersive experiences that surface assumptions.
- **Facilitate critical reflection**: structured prompts that ask "why do I believe this?" and "what evidence would change my mind?" — not just "what did I learn?"
- **Rational discourse**: create conditions for open, honest dialogue where assumptions can be examined without threat.
- **Requires psychological safety**: challenging core beliefs is risky. Without safety, learners defend rather than examine.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** the learning goal involves perspective shift, identity development, or challenging deeply held assumptions. Leadership, ethics, DEI, professional identity, and career transitions.

**Limitations:** can be emotionally intense — facilitators need skill and ethical awareness. Not appropriate for routine skill training. Critiqued for emphasizing rational reflection over emotional and relational dimensions. Can be difficult to operationalize and measure.
