# Mixed Methods Research

## Definition

Mixed methods research (Creswell & Plano Clark) deliberately integrates quantitative and qualitative data collection and analysis within a single study or program of inquiry. The integration — not just the coexistence — of both data types is what distinguishes mixed methods from running a survey AND doing interviews without connecting them.

## Core Designs

| Design | Structure | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Convergent** | Collect quant + qual simultaneously, compare/merge results | When you want to triangulate — do the numbers and the stories agree? |
| **Explanatory sequential** | Quant first → qual explains the results | When quant shows WHAT happened and you need to understand WHY |
| **Exploratory sequential** | Qual first → quant tests what you found | When you need to discover themes before measuring them at scale |
| **Embedded** | One method nested within the other (e.g., qual interviews within an experiment) | When you need process data alongside outcome data |

## Why Mixed Methods Matters for Learning Design

- **Kirkpatrick Level 3-4 evaluation**: quant shows whether behavior changed (completion rates, performance metrics); qual explains why or why not (interviews with learners and managers)
- **Learner research**: surveys give patterns at scale; interviews give depth and context
- **Transfer assessment**: performance data shows IF transfer happened; follow-up interviews reveal barriers and enablers
- **Program improvement**: analytics show WHERE learners drop off; interviews reveal WHY

## Key Principles

- **Integration is the point** — if you don't connect the quant and qual findings, you have two separate studies, not mixed methods. The "mixing" happens in analysis and interpretation.
- **Purpose drives design** — choose the design based on your research question, not convention. "What happened AND why?" → explanatory sequential. "Do different data sources agree?" → convergent.
- **Pragmatic stance** — mixed methods is pragmatist: use whatever method best answers the question. Paradigm wars between quant and qual are unproductive.
- **Resource reality** — mixed methods takes more time and skill than either method alone. Scope realistically.

## Common Mistakes

- **Parallel but unconnected** — running quant and qual without integrating findings
- **Qual as afterthought** — a few interview quotes stapled onto a quantitative study
- **Mixing without rationale** — "we did both" without articulating WHY both were needed
- **Ignoring contradictions** — when quant and qual disagree, that's the most interesting finding, not an inconvenience
