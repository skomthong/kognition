# Constructive Alignment

## Definition

Biggs (1996) defines constructive alignment as marrying constructivist learning theory with alignment from instructional design. The constructivist view frames the design logic: derive outcomes as performances at appropriate cognitive levels, decide learning activities likely to elicit those performances, and assess those performances directly.

## How To Apply It

1. Specify **intended learning outcomes** (ILOs) as what learners should be able to do, at what cognitive level
2. Design **assessment tasks** that directly capture those outcomes — the assessment IS the evidence of learning
3. Design **teaching/learning activities** that support learners in developing the performances required by assessment

The three elements must align: if the ILO says "analyze," the assessment must require analysis (not recall), and activities must provide practice in analysis.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** higher-ed course design, program QA, assessment redesign, credentialing contexts — anywhere coherence between outcomes, activities, and assessment is a governance requirement.

**Limitations:** Quality depends on outcome clarity and assessment validity. Can become compliance-driven ("we aligned on paper") without genuine pedagogical improvement. When decisions are high-stakes, use professional testing standards for validity and fairness.
