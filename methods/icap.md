# ICAP Engagement Framework

## Definition

The ICAP framework (Chi & Wylie, 2014) classifies learning activities by engagement mode and predicts that learning outcomes increase across four levels: Interactive > Constructive > Active > Passive.

## How It Works

| Mode | Learner behavior | Example activities | Predicted outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Passive** | Receiving without overt processing | Listening to lecture, reading without annotation | Lowest — store |
| **Active** | Manipulating materials without generating | Highlighting, copying notes, repeating steps | Low-moderate — activate |
| **Constructive** | Generating output beyond what was provided | Self-explaining, concept mapping, creating summaries | Moderate-high — infer |
| **Interactive** | Co-constructing with peers through dialogue | Debating, collaborative problem-solving, peer teaching | Highest — co-infer |

## Key Principles

- Use ICAP as an **activity audit tool**: review a course and classify each activity. If most activities are Passive or Active, redesign toward Constructive and Interactive.
- Not all "Interactive" is good — facilitation quality matters. Poorly facilitated discussion can be worse than a well-designed Constructive task.
- ICAP predicts engagement quality, not content coverage. A Passive lecture may cover more topics; a Constructive activity may cover fewer but produce deeper learning.
- Use ICAP-coded activity audits as a measure of engagement quality in [Learning Analytics](../systems/learning-analytics.md).
