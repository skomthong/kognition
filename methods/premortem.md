# Premortem and Red Teaming

## Definition

A **premortem** (Klein, 2007) inverts the post-mortem: before a project launches, the team imagines it has ALREADY FAILED and works backward to identify what went wrong. This leverages [mental simulation](../foundations/mental-simulation.md) and overcomes the optimism bias that suppresses risk identification during planning.

**Red teaming** is a structured adversarial review where a team deliberately tries to break, defeat, or find flaws in a plan, product, or argument — before the real world does.

## How a Premortem Works

1. "Imagine it's 6 months from now. This program has FAILED completely. It was a disaster."
2. Each team member independently writes down reasons WHY it failed. (Individual before group prevents groupthink.)
3. Share all reasons. Cluster into themes.
4. For each major failure mode: is it plausible? What would we do to prevent it? What would we do to detect it early?
5. Update the plan based on findings.

## How Red Teaming Works

- Assign a team (or individual) to deliberately attack the plan: "Your job is to find every way this could go wrong, be misinterpreted, fail to transfer, or waste money."
- Red team operates with permission to be critical — [psychological safety](../principles/psychological-safety.md) is essential.
- Red team findings are treated as gifts, not threats. The goal is to STRENGTHEN the plan.

## Key Principles

- **Premortems overcome optimism bias** — teams planning a project are psychologically invested in success and unconsciously suppress doubt. The premortem LICENSES doubt by making failure the starting assumption.
- **Individual before group** — if you brainstorm failure modes as a group first, conformity suppresses minority concerns. Write individually, then share.
- **Specificity matters** — "It could fail" is useless. "The manager reinforcement plan won't work because regional managers were never briefed" is actionable.
- **Regular, not one-time** — run premortems at each project phase, not just at kickoff. New risks emerge as the project evolves.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Before program launch**: premortem the program — what could cause dropout, transfer failure, negative learner experience, stakeholder rejection?
- **Before assessment deployment**: red team the assessment — where could it be unfair, biased, gamed, or misinterpreted?
- **Before tech rollout**: premortem the adoption — what would prevent faculty or learners from using the new system?
- **Before case/scenario publication**: red team the case — is it offensive, unrealistic, too easy, or does it have an obvious "right answer"?
