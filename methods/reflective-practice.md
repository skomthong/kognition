# Reflective Practice

## Definition

Reflective practice (Schön, 1983) distinguishes two modes: **reflection-in-action** (thinking about what you're doing while doing it — adjusting in real time) and **reflection-on-action** (thinking back on what happened after the fact — learning from experience). Professional expertise depends on both.

## How To Apply It

- **Structured debrief**: after-action reviews, simulation debrief, case discussion wrap-ups with prompts: "What happened? What did I assume? What would I do differently?"
- **Reflective journals**: periodic written reflection tied to practice experiences — effective when structured with prompts, less effective as open-ended journaling.
- **Coaching conversations**: facilitator helps the practitioner surface tacit reasoning and test assumptions.
- **Critical incident analysis**: identify a specific moment where something went well or poorly, analyze the reasoning, extract transferable lessons.

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** developing professional judgment, tacit knowledge, clinical reasoning, leadership — contexts where "knowing-in-action" matters as much as declarative knowledge. **Limitations:** can be vague without structured prompts or rubrics. Reflection fatigue is real — avoid mandating reflection without providing feedback on it.
