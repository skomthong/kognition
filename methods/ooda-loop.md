# OODA Loop

## Definition

The OODA loop (Boyd) is a rapid decision cycle: Observe → Orient → Decide → Act. Originally developed for military fighter pilots, it applies to any context requiring fast decisions under uncertainty. The key insight is that speed of ORIENTATION — not just speed of action — determines decision quality.

## How It Works

| Phase | What happens | Critical question |
|---|---|---|
| **Observe** | Gather information from the environment | What's actually happening? What data is available? |
| **Orient** | Interpret the observations through mental models, experience, culture, and context | What does this MEAN? How does it fit what I know? What am I missing? |
| **Decide** | Select a course of action | Given my orientation, what should I do? |
| **Act** | Execute the decision | Do it — then immediately begin observing again |

**The cycle repeats continuously.** OODA is not a one-shot process; it's a loop. Speed comes from faster cycling, not from skipping phases.

### Orient is the critical phase
Boyd emphasizes that Orientation is where the loop is won or lost. Orientation is shaped by:
- Previous experience and mental models ([expertise](../foundations/expertise-development.md))
- Cultural traditions and organizational norms
- New information being processed
- Ability to destroy and rebuild mental models when they no longer fit reality

A person with richer, more flexible mental models orients faster and more accurately.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Training rapid decision-makers**: use time-pressured scenarios where learners must cycle through OODA quickly — observe cues, orient to the situation, decide, act, then debrief
- **Simulation debrief**: structure debrief using OODA — "What did you observe? How did you orient? What did you decide? What happened when you acted?"
- **Design iteration**: OODA applies to iterative design — observe learner behavior, orient (what does the data mean?), decide on a design change, implement, observe again
- **Crisis and safety training**: OODA is the natural rhythm of emergency response — train the loop explicitly

## Key Principles

- **Speed of loop > speed of action** — the person who cycles through OODA faster makes better decisions, not just faster ones
- **Orient is trainable** — richer schemas, diverse experience, and deliberate practice improve orientation quality
- **Destroyed mental models** — the ability to let go of a model that no longer fits is the hardest and most valuable OODA skill
