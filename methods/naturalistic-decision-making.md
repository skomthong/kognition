# Naturalistic Decision Making

## Definition

Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM; Klein, 1998) studies how experienced people actually make decisions in real-world settings — under time pressure, uncertainty, high stakes, ambiguous information, and dynamic conditions. It contrasts with classical decision theory (which assumes rational comparison of options) by observing that experts rarely compare options at all.

## Recognition-Primed Decision Model (RPD)

Klein's central finding: experts don't compare options. They:
1. **Recognize the situation** as familiar (pattern matching from experience)
2. **Mentally simulate** the first option that comes to mind — "If I do this, what happens?"
3. **If it works in simulation** → act on it
4. **If it fails in simulation** → modify or try the next recognized pattern

This is NOT random or careless — it's System 1 expertise built from thousands of prior experiences. The expert's pattern library IS their decision quality.

## Key Findings

- **Experts generate one option, not many** — option comparison is a novice strategy. Experts recognize and simulate.
- **Experience IS the decision tool** — the more patterns an expert has seen, the faster and more accurately they recognize situations. This is why [deliberate practice](../techniques/instructional/deliberate-practice.md) with diverse cases matters.
- **Simulation quality depends on mental models** — richer, more accurate [schemas](../principles/schema-and-mental-models.md) produce better mental simulations.
- **Time pressure doesn't degrade expert decisions as much as novice ones** — because recognition is fast; deliberation is slow.

## Application to Learning Design

- **Build pattern libraries** through exposure to diverse cases, scenarios, and examples. The MORE situations a learner has encountered, the better their recognition.
- **[CTA](../techniques/instructional/cognitive-task-analysis.md) captures expert patterns** — interview experts to elicit the cues, patterns, and mental models they use for recognition.
- **Scenario-based training works BECAUSE of RPD** — scenarios give learners pattern-matching practice with feedback that builds the recognition-simulation loop.
- **Debrief the recognition process** — "What did you recognize? What pattern did you match? What cues told you this was similar to [prior experience]?"

## Distinction from Bias Research

| Kahneman tradition | Klein tradition |
|---|---|
| Focus: where intuition FAILS (biases) | Focus: where intuition SUCCEEDS (expertise) |
| Prescription: slow down, use System 2 | Prescription: build better System 1 through experience |
| Context: lab experiments with novices | Context: field studies with experts |
| Risk: over-trusting intuition | Risk: over-distrusting intuition |

Both are valid. The design implication: train BOTH — build expert intuition through practice AND teach debiasing for situations where intuition fails.
