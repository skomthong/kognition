# Experiential Learning (Kolb)

## Definition

Kolb's Experiential Learning Theory (1984) proposes a four-stage learning cycle: Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualization → Active Experimentation. Learning occurs through the transformation of experience — grasping experience (concrete or abstract) and transforming it (reflective or active).

## How To Apply It

1. **Concrete Experience**: do something — simulation, case discussion, workplace task, role-play
2. **Reflective Observation**: reflect on what happened — debrief, journaling, peer discussion
3. **Abstract Conceptualization**: derive principles and models — connect experience to theory, frameworks, rules
4. **Active Experimentation**: plan how to apply — practice with variation, transfer to new context, test the model

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** designing action learning, simulations, labs, projects, internships — any context where "learning by doing" is central. The cycle provides practical structure for turning experience into learning.

**Limitations:** the "cycle" can become oversimplified if treated as a rigid sequence. Effective use requires well-designed reflection and feedback at each stage. Kolb's "learning styles" dimension (Diverging, Assimilating, Converging, Accommodating) lacks evidence for style-matching interventions — use the cycle, not the styles.
