# Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

## Definition

Jobs-to-Be-Done (Christensen, 2003/2016) reframes product design around the "job" a customer is trying to accomplish — not the product's features. Applied to learning: what "job" is the learner hiring this learning experience to do?

## How It Works

Three dimensions of every job:

| Dimension | Learning example |
|---|---|
| **Functional** | "I need to pass this certification" / "I need to handle difficult conversations" |
| **Emotional** | "I want to feel confident" / "I want to stop feeling incompetent in meetings" |
| **Social** | "I want my team to see me as capable" / "I want to belong in this professional community" |

JTBD reframes design away from content coverage ("what should we teach?") toward outcome demand ("what is the learner trying to accomplish, and what's preventing them?").

## Key Principles

- Adults don't "want training" — they want to solve a problem, advance a career, gain confidence, or meet a requirement. JTBD surfaces the real demand.
- Connects directly to andragogy's "need to know" and "immediacy of application" assumptions.
- Use JTBD for program design, marketing, enrollment, and learner motivation — not just instructional sequencing.
- Interview learners about their "hiring criteria" — when did they decide to learn this? What alternatives did they consider? What would make them quit?

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** designing new programs, repositioning existing ones, understanding dropout, or aligning L&D with business needs. **Limitations:** practitioner framework, not a tested learning theory. Best for strategic design decisions, not lesson-level planning.
