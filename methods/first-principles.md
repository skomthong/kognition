# First Principles of Instruction (Merrill)

## Definition

Merrill (2002) synthesizes across instructional design theories to identify five principles present in effective instruction: learning is promoted when (1) instruction is **problem-centered**, (2) existing knowledge is **activated**, (3) new knowledge is **demonstrated**, (4) the learner **applies** new knowledge, and (5) new knowledge is **integrated** into the learner's world.

## How To Apply It

1. **Problem-centered**: organize instruction around real-world problems or tasks, progressing from simple to complex
2. **Activation**: activate relevant prior knowledge and experience as a foundation for new learning
3. **Demonstration**: show learners what to do (examples, models, cases) — don't just tell them
4. **Application**: provide practice with coaching and feedback — not just passive exposure
5. **Integration**: help learners transfer to their own context through reflection, creation, and public demonstration

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** designing any instruction — First Principles serve as a meta-checklist across ID models. **Limitations:** Merrill notes the paper does not itself establish empirical support for all principles — it's a synthesis, not a primary study.
