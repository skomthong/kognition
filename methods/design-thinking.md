# Design Thinking for Learning

## Definition

Design thinking (IDEO/d.school) is a human-centered problem-solving approach organized into five stages: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. Applied to learning experience design, it shifts the starting point from content or objectives to learner needs and experience.

## How To Apply It

| Stage | In learning design | How it differs from traditional ID |
|---|---|---|
| **Empathize** | Observe and interview learners in their context — beyond surveys | Deeper than standard learner analysis; ethnographic observation |
| **Define** | Frame the design challenge as a learner problem, not a content gap | "How might we help new managers handle difficult conversations?" not "Create a management training module" |
| **Ideate** | Generate multiple design approaches before committing | Divergent thinking; avoids defaulting to "same thing we did last time" |
| **Prototype** | Build quick, low-fidelity versions to test with learners | Paper storyboards, clickable mockups, role-played sessions |
| **Test** | Get learner feedback on the prototype; iterate | Validates design before expensive production |

## When To Use It / When Not To

**Use when:** the problem is poorly defined, user needs are unclear, conventional approaches have failed, or you want to explore multiple design directions before committing. Especially valuable for new program launches and experience redesigns.

**Limitations:** can feel unfocused without discipline. Empathy research takes time. Prototyping learning is harder than prototyping products — a paper prototype of a 12-week program is inherently limited. Works best as a complement to ID frameworks (backward design, action mapping), not a replacement.
