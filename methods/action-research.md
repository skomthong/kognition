# Action Research

## Definition

Action research (Lewin, 1946; Stringer, 2014) is a cyclical process where practitioners systematically investigate their own practice to improve it — blending research rigor with practical action. Unlike traditional research (researcher studies others), action research is conducted BY the practitioner ON their own work, with the explicit goal of changing practice, not just describing it.

## How It Works

Iterative cycle:
1. **Identify** — what problem or question arises from my practice?
2. **Plan** — what change will I make? What data will I collect?
3. **Act** — implement the change in the real setting
4. **Observe** — collect data on what happened
5. **Reflect** — what did I learn? What will I change next cycle?

Then repeat — each cycle builds on the previous one.

## Distinction from Other Methods

| Method | Who does it | Goal | Setting |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Action research** | Practitioner studies own practice | Improve practice + generate local knowledge | Own classroom/workplace |
| **Design-based research** | Researcher-practitioner team | Improve design + generate theory | Collaborative, real setting |
| **Traditional research** | External researcher | Generate generalizable knowledge | Controlled or observed setting |
| **Evaluation** (Kirkpatrick/CIPP) | Evaluator | Judge effectiveness | Post-implementation |

## Key Principles

- **Practitioner as researcher** — you don't need external researchers. The person closest to the practice is best positioned to study it.
- **Systematic, not casual** — action research differs from "trying stuff" because data is collected deliberately, reflection is structured, and cycles are documented.
- **Local knowledge** — findings may not generalize broadly, but they're immediately actionable in YOUR context. This is a feature, not a limitation.
- **Ethical practice** — even when studying your own work, consider: informed consent from learners, data privacy, power dynamics (especially if you also grade or evaluate).
- **Multiple data sources** — triangulate: learner performance data + your observations + learner feedback + peer observation. No single source is sufficient.

## Application to Learning Design

- "I redesigned the feedback cycle in Module 3. Did retrieval scores improve? What did learners say about the change? What will I adjust next iteration?"
- "I introduced peer assessment. Did discussion quality change? What problems emerged? How do I refine the process?"
- Perfect for DBA-level practitioner inquiry — systematic, evidence-based, directly applicable.
