# Design-Based Research

## Definition

Design-based research (DBR) is an iterative methodology that develops and refines learning interventions in real educational settings while simultaneously studying them. Unlike traditional research (which tests a fixed intervention), DBR treats the design as evolving — each iteration generates both practical improvements AND theoretical insights.

## How It Works

Iterative cycle:
1. **Analyze the problem** — with practitioners, in context
2. **Design a solution** — grounded in theory and prior research
3. **Implement and test** — in the real learning environment, not a lab
4. **Evaluate and reflect** — collect data, identify what worked and what didn't
5. **Redesign** — revise based on evidence, then cycle again

Each cycle produces:
- **Practical output** — an improved intervention
- **Theoretical output** — design principles that generalize beyond this specific case

## Key Principles

- **Ecological validity** — research happens in real classrooms/workplaces, not labs. Messy, but applicable.
- **Collaboration with practitioners** — designers, facilitators, and researchers work together. Research isn't done TO practitioners but WITH them.
- **Theory-driven iteration** — design changes are grounded in learning theory, not just "try something different." Each iteration tests a theoretical prediction.
- **Mixed methods** — typically combines quantitative outcome data with qualitative process data to understand both WHETHER it works and WHY.
- **Design principles as output** — the goal is not just "this intervention worked" but "these principles can guide similar designs in other contexts."

## When To Use It

**Use when:** developing new learning approaches where no validated model exists; studying learning in complex, real-world settings; building evidence-based design guidelines for a domain.

**Limitations:** time-intensive (multiple cycles); lacks the control of experimental research; generalizability depends on how well design principles are articulated; researcher bias risk (the designer is also the researcher).
