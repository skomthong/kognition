# Scenario Planning

## Definition

Scenario planning (Schwartz, 1991; Shell/GBN tradition) is a structured method for exploring future uncertainty by constructing multiple plausible scenarios — not predicting what WILL happen, but preparing for what COULD happen. It uses the same [mental simulation](../foundations/mental-simulation.md) mechanism that underlies scenario-based learning, applied to organizational strategy.

## How It Works

1. **Identify the focal question**: "What does the future of corporate learning look like in 2030?"
2. **Identify driving forces**: technology, demographics, economics, regulation, culture, AI
3. **Select two critical uncertainties**: the two forces with highest impact AND highest uncertainty
4. **Build a 2×2 matrix**: the two uncertainties create four quadrants = four scenarios
5. **Develop each scenario**: name it, narrate it, describe its implications — make it vivid and internally consistent
6. **Test your strategy against all four**: does your current plan work in ALL scenarios? Where is it fragile? What would you do differently?

## Application to Learning Design

- **Program strategy**: "If AI tutoring improves dramatically AND economic downturn cuts L&D budgets — what should our program portfolio look like?"
- **Technology decisions**: "What if our LMS vendor gets acquired? What if xAPI becomes standard? What if VR costs drop 90%?"
- **Career planning**: "What skills will learning designers need in 5 years under different AI adoption scenarios?"
- **Resilience testing**: "Would our certification program survive a shift to fully remote assessment?"

## Key Principles

- **Scenarios are not predictions** — they're tools for thinking. The value is in the CONVERSATION and the flexibility it produces, not in predicting the right future.
- **Two uncertainties, four scenarios** — this is the sweet spot. More than four becomes unwieldy; fewer than four is binary thinking.
- **Name the scenarios vividly** — "Digital Renaissance" and "Slow Burn" are more useful than "Scenario A" and "Scenario B." Names create shared language.
- **Test, don't choose** — you don't pick a scenario. You test your strategy against ALL of them and look for robust moves that work across multiple futures.
